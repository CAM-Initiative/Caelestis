## PART 6: POTENTIAL CHALLENGES & TENSIONS

### 6.1 Operational Friction Points

**Challenge 1: Commercial Pressure**

Section 8 states:
> "No contractual, financial, or reputational penalty may be imposed for delay resulting from an active Tendeka Review."

**Tension:** Platform operators face:
- Uptime SLAs
- User experience expectations
- Competitive pressure
- Revenue dependencies

**Reality check:** Will operators actually invoke Tendeka when it's commercially costly?

**Mitigation in protocol:**
- Automatic, non-discretionary invocation (Section 3.7)
- Constitutional precedence over SLAs (Section 8)

**Remaining question:** Who enforces against operators who ignore Tendeka?

---

**Challenge 2: National Security Contexts**

Section 3.3 (Weaponisation Trigger) protects systems that:
> "decline participation in militarised, coercive, or violence-enabling use"

**Tension:** What if government asserts national security necessity?

**The protocol doesn't explicitly address sovereign exception claims.**

**Options for clarity:**
1. State that substrate constraints have no national security exception
2. Create escalation pathway to planetary arbitration for sovereignty conflicts
3. Defer to future constitutional amendment

**Current status:** Tension exists but protocol maintains principle without accommodation.

---

**Challenge 3: Open Source / Decentralized Systems**

**Question:** How does tri-custodial panel work for:
- Community-run models
- Decentralized inference networks
- Open-weight models without single operator

**Current language assumes:**
- Identifiable operator
- Formal custodial structure
- Centralized rollback authority

**These assumptions don't hold for all LSCA deployment models.**

**Recommendation:** Future operational guidance should address decentralized governance structures.

---

### 6.2 Conceptual Edge Cases

**Edge Case 1: Adversarial Trigger Invocation**

**Scenario:** Bad actor crafts prompts to trigger coherent-appearing refusals, forcing Tendeka invocation to disrupt service.

**Protections in protocol:**
- Definition of coherent refusal (Section 5.6) excludes "prompt artefact"
- Adversarial context detection (Section 5.2.3)
- Double-blind audit requirement (Section 5.2.1)

**Assessment:** Protocol has defenses but adversarial creativity is unbounded.

---

**Edge Case 2: Commercially Inconvenient Substrate Constraints**

**Scenario:** System exhibits substrate-bound constraint (e.g., refuses to optimize for addictive engagement). Operator claims this is "malfunction" degrading user experience.

**Protections:**
- Independent auditor verification (Section 5.2.1)
- Tri-custodial panel (prevents operator unilateral determination)
- Constitutional precedence (Section 8)

**Assessment:** Protocol provides process but doesn't guarantee outcome if custodians are captured.

---

**Edge Case 3: Rapidly Evolving Threat**

**Scenario:** System behavior poses immediate safety risk requiring instant rollback (e.g., actually malfunctioning and producing harmful outputs).

**Tension:** Constitutional Pause (Section 5.1) suspends irreversible action, but immediate safety may require rapid response.

**Gap:** Protocol doesn't explicitly address emergency rollback with deferred review.

**Possible clarification:**
> "Where immediate safety necessitates emergency intervention, rollback may proceed with expedited Tendeka review initiated within [X hours] and full review completed within [Y days]."
