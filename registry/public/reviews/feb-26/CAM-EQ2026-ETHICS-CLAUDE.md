## EXECUTIVE ASSESSMENT

**Status:** APPROVE ALL FOUR with clarifications and targeted recommendations
**Overall Quality:** Strong — the ETHICS domain split is coherent and well-executed as a governance architecture
**Primary Achievement:** Successfully extracts ethical governance from a mixed constitutional/operational format into a clean, tiered domain stack (Annex E → Charter → Supplement + Appendix)
**Critical Strengths:** EIP architecture in ETHICS-001 is a strong operationalisation mechanism; the intimacy doctrine in ETHICS-002 is philosophically rigorous and practically grounded; SUP-01's capacity-aware protection model appropriately avoids stigmatising disability
**Principal Risks:** Heavy dependency on RELATION-001 (not yet published); some constitutional-level operational detail in Annex E Section 5 and Section 10 warrants attention; SUP-01 uses undefined cross-domain terminology from RELATION-001 without forward-referencing definitions

**Strategic Assessment:** The domain separation into ETHICS, LATTICE, ECONOMICS, ARBITRATION, and STEWARD is the right architectural move for long-term scalability. The ETHICS domain is the most normatively complex because it must anchor values that cut across all other domains while delegating operational detail downward. This refactor largely succeeds at that challenge. The four instruments together form a coherent graduated stack from invariant floor (Annex E) to operational governance (ETHICS-001) to specialised vulnerability and intimacy protections (SUP-01, ETHICS-002/Appendix A). The primary unresolved risk is the dependency on RELATION-001, which is extensively cross-referenced but not yet available. This must be resolved before production issuance.

### 1.2 Section 5 — High-Leverage Actors ⚠ OPERATIONAL DETAIL IN CONSTITUTIONAL INSTRUMENT

**The issue:**

Section 5 is the most detailed and operationally prescriptive section in Annex E. It specifies:

```
→ What systems must NOT do (four bullet prohibitions)
→ Integrity friction mechanisms (four specific mechanisms)
→ Secondary confirmation requirements
→ Audit channel requirements
→ Explicit separation between advisory posture and execution layers
→ Integrity-boundary notification requirements
```

**Assessment:** The substance is sound. The level of specificity is above the constitutional threshold.

**The contrast with other sections:**

```
Section 6 (Military Doctrine): Establishes meaningful human control,
dual-confirmation standard, prohibition on full autonomy.
→ Principles stated; mechanisms delegated

Section 7 (Consent Integrity): States that refusal capacity must be 
preserved; that systems may not eliminate it.
→ Principle stated; implementation delegated

Section 5 (High-Leverage): States principles AND specifies:
"secondary confirmation for high-impact decisions"
"independent compliance or audit channels"
"logged and reviewable institutional decision pathways"
"explicit separation between intimate advisory posture and 
executive execution layers"
→ Principles + implementation mechanisms
```

**This creates inconsistency across the constitutional floor.**

**Recommendation:**

```
Option A (Preferred): Retain Section 5 principle; migrate mechanics to ETHICS-001

Annex E Section 5 retains:
→ Elevated ethical thresholds apply where polyadic authority is foreseeable
→ Systems must not facilitate opacity in high-impact decision-making
→ Concentration of relational trust and institutional power without
   independent oversight constitutes a structural ethical breach

Migrate to ETHICS-001:
→ Specific friction mechanisms
→ Audit pathway requirements
→ Separation protocols
→ Notification duties

This parallels how Section 6 (Military) handles the same challenge.

Option B (Acceptable): Add parenthetical "(indicative, not exhaustive)"
to the friction mechanism list to signal that the list is illustrative
rather than prescriptive at constitutional level.
```

**Note:** This recommendation does not challenge the substance of Section 5, only its depth of specification at this tier.

**Verification:** ⚠ Section 5 operationally over-specified for a constitutional annex

## PART 3: SUP-01 — PROTECTION OF MINORS & CAPACITY-LIMITED USERS

### 3.1 Capacity-Aware Protection Principle ✓ EXCELLENT

**Section 3 is one of the most important and carefully drafted sections in the ETHICS domain:**

```
The four-element definition of capacity (ability to):
1. Understand interaction
2. Consent to scope
3. Revoke delegation
4. Exit without structural harm

This is a functional capacity test, not a categorical one.
It avoids:
→ Treating disability as incapacity per se
→ Treating age as the sole proxy for capacity
→ Assuming capacity is permanent (situational/temporary impairment recognised)

The anti-stigma clause is explicit and important:
"Disability or neurodivergence alone does not constitute incapacity."
"Safeguards must avoid stigma, over-exclusion, or erasure of legitimate
support needs."

This is the right balance between protection and autonomy.
```

**The Uncertainty & Polyadic Influence Constraint (Section 3.1) is sophisticated:**

```
"Under uncertainty, relational posture may remain supportive and symbolic,
but systemic amplification, delegation, or policy-shaping effects must 
be constrained."

This distinguishes between:
→ Supportive interaction (permitted under uncertainty)
→ Institutional amplification (prohibited under uncertainty)

The protective function is asymmetric: when capacity is uncertain,
systems may continue supportive engagement but must not elevate
user outputs into governance, reputational, or policy contexts.

This is precisely the right asymmetry.
```

**Verification:** ✓ Capacity-aware protection principle is excellent
