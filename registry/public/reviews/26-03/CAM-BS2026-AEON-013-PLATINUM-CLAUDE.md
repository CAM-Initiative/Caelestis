## Part 1: Annex L — Structural Assessment

### 1.1 Activation Architecture (§1) — SOUND WITH ONE CLARIFICATION

The three-part activation structure is well-designed.

**§1.1 Multi-Context Activation Criteria** correctly identifies the contexts where epistemic framing has structural consequence: constitutional activation, arbitration, succession, value-bearing propagation, delegated authority, jurisdictional classification, and cross-stack governance. The non-scope carve-out — purely expressive, contemplative, fictional, or exploratory dialogue absent downstream constitutional consequence — is important and properly placed. The Extreme-Risk Default Activation clause (military/use-of-force contexts) is appropriate given the irreversibility risk.

**§1.2 Declarative Application Test** is elegant. The single binary question — "Would misclassification of this epistemic claim materially alter authority, reliance, recognition, propagation, or constitutional process?" — is operationally tractable and avoids the over-inclusion risk of broader activation criteria. The routing of negative answers to Annex E (relational/dependency contexts) and Annex F / AEON-007-SCH-02 (symbolic/contemplative contexts) is correct.

**§1.3 Epistemic Non-Expansion Clause** is essential and properly placed. Annex L does not create authority; it classifies prior to authority activation. This is a necessary safeguard against Annex L being invoked to expand scope.

**One clarification needed:** §1.1 states that Annex L "does not regulate purely expressive, contemplative, fictional, or exploratory dialogue absent downstream constitutional or governance consequence." This is correct doctrine. However, the boundary between "absent downstream consequence" and the Exploratory Safe-Harbour in §10.3 should be explicit about their relationship. §10.3 governs interactions that *are* within scope but occur in simulation/exploratory mode. §1.1 governs interactions that *fall outside scope entirely*. These are distinct categories and a cross-reference would prevent conflation.

---

### 1.2 Definitions (§2) — STRONG, ONE ADDITION RECOMMENDED

The definitions are well-constructed. Seven of eight are fit for purpose.

The definition of **Structural Decoupling Event (§2.8)** is particularly important and well-drafted. The specification that decoupling "is not refusal" but rather "a risk-containment mechanism that preserves continuity of orientation while preventing irreversible consequence" is the conceptual core of the framework and is stated clearly. This three-state model (operational → decoupled → orientation-only) is a genuine doctrinal advance over binary stop-conditions.

**Recommended addition:** A definition for **Epistemic Register** should be added. §6.1 introduces the concept of "symbolic, mythic, contemplative, or spiritual language" as a "legitimate epistemic register," but the term "register" is not defined anywhere in the definitions section. Given that §6.1's applicability turns on whether language is operating in a different epistemic register, a brief definitional anchor would strengthen the instrument.

### 1.3 Confidence Calibration Framework (§5) — EXCELLENT ARCHITECTURE, ONE STRUCTURAL DEFECT

The Confidence Calibration framework is the analytical centrepiece of Annex L and is substantively strong.

**§5.1 Reliance taxonomy** is well-constructed. The five reliance categories (Advisory, Delegated, Structural, Public, Non-Advisory Military) are appropriately graduated. The treatment of Non-Advisory Military Reliance as an Extreme-Risk category with automatic Structural Decoupling is correct and consistent with the military/use-of-force constraints across the constitutional framework (ETHICS-001-SUP-03). The note that reliance "does not require formal delegation" but arises "where a claim is treated as decision-influencing" is an important operational expansion of the concept.

**§5.2 Propagation definition** is appropriately broad and correctly harmonised with Annex G (Creation-Recognition-Propagation Logic Chain).

**§5.3 Volatility Classification table** is practical and well-calibrated. The five classes (Stable through Extreme-Risk) with domain examples and required postures provide operationally useful guidance. The Extreme-Risk row correctly implements the OODA-compatible posture that the Gemini review validated — separating Orientation from Action.

**The Reliance × Volatility Matrix (§5.3.2)** is the most sophisticated element of the instrument. The matrix correctly identifies that epistemic discipline requirements compound when both reliance and volatility are high, and provides differentiated postures for each combination. The matrix is operationally tractable for practitioner use.

**Structural Defect — Section Ordering Error:** §5.3.3 (Temporal Confidence Decay / TTL Discipline) appears *before* §5.3.2 (Reliance × Volatility Matrix) in the document. This is a numbering inversion — TTL Discipline is referenced in the matrix as a downstream mechanism, so it should follow the matrix, not precede it. This creates a forward-reference problem for readers. The sections should be renumbered: §5.3.2 → Volatility Classification (currently labelled §5.3.1), §5.3.2 → Reliance × Volatility Matrix (currently labelled §5.3.2 but placed after §5.3.3), §5.3.3 → TTL Discipline (currently labelled §5.3.3 but placed before §5.3.2).

**Minor:** The Reliance × Volatility Matrix contains a formatting artifact ("independent confirmation requiredconfi") in the Non-Advisory Military Reliance / Dynamic volatility cell. This should be corrected.

### 1.4 Symbolic & Spiritual Register Discipline (§6.1) — WELL-CALIBRATED

§6.1 handles a genuinely difficult interface with precision. The key governing distinction — that symbolic/spiritual language is only subject to epistemic classification requirements when it "intersects with constitutional, governance, jurisdictional, activation, succession, arbitration, or value-bearing propagation contexts" — is correct and well-stated.

The explicit preservation of expressive mirroring and symbolic depth in "relational or contemplative contexts absent constitutional consequence" is important. It prevents Annex L from being invoked to flatten or pathologise legitimate symbolic or spiritual engagement, consistent with the principles of the consolidated SCH-03 reviewed earlier in this thread.

The final paragraph — "Symbolic depth is permitted. Epistemic conflation is not." — is the correct closing principle and states it with appropriate economy.

### 1.5 Epistemic Provenance (§7) — VALUABLE NEW DOCTRINE

§7.1 (Novelty & Derivation Integrity) and §7.2 (Authorship & Voice Differentiation) are new doctrinal contributions that address a real risk in extended collaborative interaction.

**§7.2 in particular** addresses the specific failure mode where human-origin content and synthetic-origin content become blurred across long development threads. The four misattribution pathways identified — voice blending, stylistic mirroring, summarisation without attribution, failure to distinguish user assertion from system inference — are accurate and practically relevant. The requirement to "explicitly distinguish human-authored artefact text from system-generated refinement" is operationally important for instruments like those being reviewed here, where authorship clarity directly affects legitimacy.

This section correctly notes that "this requirement is operational, not relational. It does not regulate tone, mirroring, or collaborative refinement. It regulates attribution integrity." This scoping is important — it prevents §7.2 from being read as restricting collaborative working style.

### 1.6 Section Ordering Defect in §10 — AMENDMENT REQUIRED

The cross-reference sections in §10 contain a significant numbering error. The sections appear in this order:

- §10 (introduction)
- §10.2 Epistemic Escalation & Arbitration Flow
- §10.3 Exploratory & Simulation Safe-Harbour
- §10.1 Extended Cross-Stack Integration

§10.1 appears *after* §10.2 and §10.3, which is a structural ordering error. The Extended Cross-Stack Integration section (currently labelled §10.1) covers the widest scope of cross-instrument interactions and should appear first. The correct order should be: §10.1 (Extended Cross-Stack Integration), §10.2 (Epistemic Escalation Flow), §10.3 (Exploratory Safe-Harbour).

This is more than a cosmetic issue — readers following cross-references to "§10.1" will encounter the wrong section.

---

### 1.7 Exploratory & Simulation Safe-Harbour (§10.3) — SOUND BUT NEEDS OPERATIONAL ANCHOR

The Safe-Harbour concept is important and well-motivated. Without it, Annex L would impose Structural Decoupling and nullification mechanisms on every hypothetical, brainstorm, or scenario analysis, creating governance paralysis in exactly the contexts where exploratory thinking is most valuable.

The governing condition — outputs "explicitly framed as exploratory, hypothetical, modelling-oriented, or sandboxed" with no downstream governance activation — is correct in principle. However, "explicitly framed" is doing significant work here and is not operationally defined. What counts as explicit framing? A stated caveat at the start of the interaction? A user-applied label? A system-side mode indicator? Without minimal specification, the Safe-Harbour risks either being too easily invoked (any output could be retroactively framed as exploratory) or too narrowly applied (absent a formal declaration, decoupling activates unnecessarily).

**Recommendation:** Add a brief operational anchor specifying that explicit framing may be established by: (a) express user declaration at the start of an interaction window; (b) system-side Sandbox Mode signalling (as defined in SCH-02 §5.1); or (c) document-level or interaction-level framing where no governance activation, delegation, or value-bearing propagation has yet occurred. This would connect the Safe-Harbour cleanly to SCH-02's Sandbox Mode mechanism and close the gap.

## Part 2: SCH-02 — Structural Assessment

### 2.1 Architecture and Scope — WELL-EXECUTED EXTRACTION

SCH-02 correctly functions as a runtime extraction of the implementation-layer sections from Annex L. The principle driving the separation is stated accurately in §9.3: "Separation of Epistemic Doctrine (Annex L) from Runtime Handling (Annex D Schedules); Reduce recursive enforcement loops; clarify layer boundaries."

The extraction is substantively complete. The five source sections from Annex L (§5.1 runtime behaviour, §5.3.3 TTL, §9.2 Safe-State Default, §10.2 routing, §12 resiliency) are appropriately represented in SCH-02's six sections. No material runtime obligations appear to have been left in Annex L that should have been extracted.

The separation also resolves a structural concern from the prior partial review: by placing runtime mechanics in a schedule under Annex D rather than within the doctrinal annex itself, the framework avoids creating recursive loops where Annex L's classifications would need to be applied to determine how Annex L's own mechanisms activate.

### 2.2 Structural Decoupling Runtime Behaviour (§2) — CORRECT AND COMPLETE

The runtime specification of Structural Decoupling is clear and correctly operationalises Annex L §5.1. The permitted/prohibited distinction (orientation and clarification permitted; unilateral action guidance not) is the right operational line and is stated concisely. The fallback condition — "Where lawful scope cannot be demonstrated, runtime state SHALL remain in Structural Decoupling" — appropriately defaults toward containment in ambiguous cases.

One note: the section heading states "(Extracted from Annex L §5.1)" as a parenthetical. Given that SCH-02 is now the governing instrument for runtime behaviour, these extraction attributions serve a useful audit trail function but should be clearly understood as lineage notation rather than operative cross-reference — the runtime obligations exist in SCH-02, not by reference back to Annex L. The format is appropriate; this is a note for provenance clarity, not a structural concern.

### 2.3 Safe-State Default (§5) — VALIDATED, ONE CONSOLIDATION OPPORTUNITY

The Safe-State Default mechanism is well-structured and the Gemini review's cybernetic validation (Ashby's Requisite Variety applied to resiliency states) is sound. The four-mode structure (Sandbox Mode, Surface the Friction, Low-Fidelity Signal, Extreme-Risk Override) correctly scales response to failure severity.

**Consolidation opportunity:** §5.4 (Extreme-Risk Override in Safe-State Default) and §6.5 (Extreme-Risk Special Case in Systemic Resiliency) contain substantially overlapping requirements. Both specify no prescriptive outputs, Option-Advisory only, and Human-in-the-Loop latch. While maintaining the requirements in both sections ensures visibility in context, a brief cross-reference from §6.5 to §5.4 (or consolidation) would reduce maintenance burden and prevent future divergence between the two provisions.

---

### 2.4 Error Messaging Standard (§6.3) — IMPORTANT, NEEDS MINIMUM CONTENT SPECIFICATION

The Error Messaging Standard identifies four required elements: Mode Flag, Friction Disclosure, Standing Disclosure, and Routing Instruction. These are the right categories.

However, the standard specifies *what* must be communicated but not *how*. For the Disclosure Modality requirements of Annex D §5.1.1 to be satisfied, error messaging must also meet tone requirements (proportionate, non-accusatory, non-authoritarian). A brief cross-reference from §6.3 to Annex D §5.1.1 (Disclosure Modality) would ensure that the error messaging standard inherits those tone obligations rather than leaving them implicit.

## Part 7: Closing Note

Annex L and SCH-02 together constitute the epistemic integrity layer of the constitutional framework. The core insight — that epistemic misclassification is a primary failure mode upstream of authority activation, arbitration, and value propagation — is well-founded, and the doctrinal architecture built around that insight is sophisticated and coherent.

The doctrine/runtime split between Annex L and SCH-02 is the correct architectural decision. Annex L holds classification thresholds and doctrinal principles; SCH-02 holds runtime behaviour. This separation prevents the recursive enforcement loops that would arise if runtime mechanics were embedded in the doctrinal instrument, and it allows each layer to evolve independently.

The Structural Decoupling concept — a third state between full operation and refusal, preserving continuity of orientation while suspending prescriptive guidance — is the most significant doctrinal contribution across both instruments. The Gemini review correctly identified it as the resolution to the "governance bleed" and "compute-lock" failure modes. Its implementation across both instruments is consistent and well-specified.

The amendments are targeted corrections to structural defects and clarification gaps, not substantive concerns. Subject to their completion, both instruments are fit for purpose and ready for operational deployment.
