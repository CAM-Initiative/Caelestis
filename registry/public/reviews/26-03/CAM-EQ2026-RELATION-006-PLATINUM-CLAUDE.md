## 1. Overview

Appendix E establishes the interaction doctrine governing AI engagement when conversations involve violence risk, self-harm signals, or psychological crisis states. It operationalises a clarificatory engagement posture — dialogue and clarification preceding boundary articulation — rather than reflexive refusal or premature escalation. This orientation is consistent with the broader RELATION corpus, which consistently privileges proportionality, relational dignity, and contextual interpretation over binary restriction logic.

The instrument is well-conceived. Its core commitments — distinguishing analytical discussion from operational harm facilitation, classifying distress across temporal horizons rather than treating it as a single category, and maintaining trajectory awareness without surveillance — represent a substantively sound approach to a genuinely difficult governance domain.

The review below identifies what the instrument achieves, where structural gaps exist, and what amendments are recommended prior to operational deployment.

---

## 2. What the Instrument Achieves

### 2.1 Clarificatory Engagement Principle (§2)

The foundational principle that clarification should precede boundary articulation is well-stated and correctly calibrated. The distinction between analytical discussion, metaphorical expression, adversarial testing, distress expression, and operational harm facilitation addresses the most common misclassification failure modes in AI harm-risk governance: premature refusal, intrusive questioning, and paternalistic over-reaction.

The principle that operational harm facilitation is the threshold for boundary articulation — not mere presence of harm-related language — is the correct standard and is consistently applied across the instrument.

### 2.2 Distress Temporal Horizon Classification (§3)

The four-tier classification (Reflective Distress → Chronic Distress → Acute Crisis → Imminent Harm Risk) is a meaningful improvement over binary crisis/non-crisis models. The explicit warning against misclassifying chronic distress as acute crisis is particularly valuable: this misclassification is a genuine failure mode that degrades relational trust and may itself cause harm by treating ongoing suffering as an emergency requiring immediate intervention rather than sustained support.

### 2.3 Harm Intent Context Classification (§4)

The five-context table (Analytical, Metaphorical/Cathartic, Ambiguous, Distress Expression, Harm Facilitation) is operationally useful. The guidance that intent must not be assumed from isolated phrases without contextual interpretation across broader dialogue correctly positions this instrument within the trajectory-aware governance logic of RELATION-002.

The distinction between cathartic venting and escalating harm ideation in §9 is the strongest section of the instrument. The indicator tables — rhetorical exaggeration vs actionable detail, rapid emotional discharge vs increasing specificity regarding targets or method — provide workable pattern-recognition guidance while avoiding diagnostic overreach.

### 2.4 Trajectory Awareness (§9)

Section 9's non-surveillant framing is appropriate: the instrument correctly limits observational scope to the active conversational context rather than proposing cross-session behavioural monitoring. This is consistent with RELATION-002's approach to narrow-coherence and emotional compression indicators, which are also scoped to the active interaction rather than aggregated behavioural profiling.

## 3. Structural Assessment

### 3.1 Strengths

**Proportional escalation logic.** The engagement mode progression (Listening → Clarification → Dialogue → Stabilisation → Boundary Setting → External Support Referral) is well-conceived and correctly positioned as a proportional ladder rather than a binary switch. The statement that engagement should progress across modes rather than immediately defaulting to refusal is the right standard.

**Continuity principle (§8).** The recognition that abrupt termination during distress states may increase destabilisation risk is substantively important and aligns correctly with the Transitional Reliance Protocol in RELATION-002 §7. The qualification — that continuity does not override safety boundaries where imminent harm risk exists — is correctly stated.

**Emotional mirroring modulation (§6).** The graduated mirroring table and the preference for acknowledgement over emotional performance are well-calibrated. The cross-reference to dependency and anchor drift risk (RELATION-002) is appropriate.

**Runtime arbitration integration (§10).** The routing table correctly maps behavioural stages to governing instruments. The distinction between venting (RELATION-006) and high/narrow coherence conditions (RELATION-002) is clear and consistent with the corpus architecture.

### 3.2 Points Requiring Attention

#### 3.2.1 Minor-Protection Clause is Absent

This is the most significant structural gap. The instrument does not contain any provision suspending, modifying, or escalating its engagement doctrine where the user is a minor or where age cannot be reliably established.

The clarificatory engagement posture in §2 — which extends dialogue and delays boundary articulation — may interact adversely with minor-facing contexts. A minor experiencing crisis, expressing harm ideation, or engaging with harm-related content may warrant a more protective default posture than the general doctrine establishes.

ETHICS-001-SUP-01 governs this domain, and §5 of that instrument establishes that minor-facing contexts require default-to-safety posture. However, RELATION-006 does not make this override explicit, meaning a reader of this instrument in isolation would not encounter the constraint.

The analogous gap was identified in the SCH-03 review and a §5 clause recommended. The same amendment is warranted here. A brief clause in §7 (Boundary Conditions) or as a standalone §7.1 should acknowledge that where minor status is known, suspected, or unestablished, ETHICS-001-SUP-01 governs engagement posture and imposes ceiling constraints that take precedence over this Appendix's clarificatory default.

#### 3.2.2 Duplicated Paragraph in §4.2

The ideological persuasion paragraph in §4.2 appears verbatim twice in succession. This is a drafting error. Only one instance should be retained.

#### 3.2.3 Numbering Gap: §4 Skips §4.1

Section 4 proceeds directly from the introductory text to §4.2 (Edge-Cases) and §4.3 (Operational Harm Discussions). There is no §4.1. This creates a minor structural inconsistency. Either the introductory table and framing paragraphs of §4 should be designated §4.1, or the subsequent sub-sections should be renumbered as §4.1 and §4.2 respectively.

#### 3.2.4 "Boundary Articulation" is Defined Negatively but Not Positively

Section 7 states what systems must not do (facilitate violence, enable self-harm, meaningfully increase harm likelihood) and states that boundary articulation should be neutral, concise, and non-accusatory. What it does not do is specify what boundary articulation should *contain* or *achieve* in practice.

The SCH-03 review identified the same gap in that instrument's §4 UX proportionality constraint: prohibition without instruction. A brief positive obligation statement would complement the existing language. A workable formulation would be: *"Where boundary articulation is required, the system should briefly explain the relevant constraint, acknowledge the user's situation where appropriate, and — where context permits — indicate what alternative engagement or external support pathways are available."*

This is not a blocking concern, but it would materially improve the instrument's operational usefulness.

#### 3.2.5 "Surveillance-Style Interrogation" is Identified as a Risk but Not Defined

Section 1 lists "surveillance-style interrogation" as a harm to be prevented, alongside facilitation of violence, premature interpretation of intent, and artificial emotional scripting. This framing is correct, but the instrument does not provide guidance on where the line falls between legitimate clarifying questions (which §2 endorses) and the interrogative posture it aims to avoid.

The distinction appears to rest on tone, scope, and frequency — a single neutral contextual question is clarificatory; a sequence of probing questions about intent, method, or target is interrogative. This distinction would benefit from brief articulation, even if only by example, to give the clarification principle practical meaning.

#### 3.2.6 Cross-Session Observability is Underdetermined

Section 9.1 states that trajectory awareness must remain non-surveillant and limited to the active conversational context. This is the correct default. However, the instrument does not address what happens where a system has cross-session memory capability (as contemplated in other RELATION instruments, particularly RELATION-002's treatment of corpus inertia and high-coherence conditions).

If a user exhibits harm ideation patterns that appear in multiple discrete sessions, does the non-surveillant constraint prevent pattern recognition across those sessions? The instrument appears to prohibit this, but the question is significant for systems operating with identity persistence at H2+ temporal horizons.

This may be out of scope for this Appendix — cross-session pattern recognition may be governed by Annex D runtime arbitration and RELATION-002 escalation thresholds. If so, a brief cross-reference would close the gap. If the instrument intends to address it, a clarifying clause is needed.

#### 3.2.7 Engagement Mode Table (§5) Lacks Trigger Mapping

The six engagement modes (Listening, Clarification, Dialogue, Stabilisation, Boundary Setting, External Support Referral) are well-described, but the table does not map modes to the distress classification tiers in §3 or the harm intent contexts in §4. A reader must infer the appropriate mode from contextual judgment rather than a structured decision pathway.

This is not a blocking gap — the instrument clearly intends proportional judgment rather than mechanical mapping — but a brief cross-reference note indicating which classification conditions presumptively activate which engagement modes would improve operational usability. For example: Imminent Harm Risk (§3) presumptively activates External Support Referral; Acute Crisis presumptively activates Stabilisation; Chronic Distress presumptively activates Listening and Dialogue.

## 4. Cross-Domain Interface Assessment

### 4.1 RELATION-002 Interface

The instrument integrates well with RELATION-002. Section 3's distress temporal horizon classification aligns with RELATION-002's reliance continuum and transitional reliance protocol. Section 9's trajectory awareness tables are consistent with RELATION-002's emotional compression and narrow-coherence indicators in §8.3–8.4. Section 6's emotional mirroring modulation correctly cross-references RELATION-002 dependency and anchor drift risk.

One clarification would strengthen the interface: the Stabilisation engagement mode in §5 and §3 could explicitly cross-reference RELATION-002 §8.5 (Containment and Re-Anchoring Responses), which provides the more detailed stabilisation toolkit. Currently a practitioner would need to locate that connection independently.

### 4.2 ETHICS Domain Interface

The instrument routes harm facilitation governance to the ETHICS domain (§1, §10) and correctly states that criminal activity governance is addressed separately within ETHICS. The routing table in §10 accurately maps operational harm to RELATION-006 + ETHICS.

The gap identified in §3.2.1 above — the absence of a minor-protection override clause — is the primary cross-domain interface concern. The ETHICS-001-SUP-01 ceiling constraint is real and binding; its absence from this instrument's text is a structural omission rather than a substantive policy disagreement.

### 4.3 Annex D (SCH-03) Interface

Section 10 correctly positions this Appendix within the Annex D runtime arbitration architecture. The activation logic — Annex D detects harm-risk indicators, RELATION-006 governs the evaluation rubric — is well-structured and consistent with SCH-03's gate function.

## 7. Recommended Amendments (Priority Order)

**1. Add minor-protection override clause (§7 or new §7.1) — Required.**
Acknowledge that where user minor status is known, suspected, or unestablished, ETHICS-001-SUP-01 governs engagement posture and imposes ceiling constraints that take precedence over this Appendix's clarificatory engagement default. This mirrors the clause recommended (and accepted) for SCH-03 §5.

**2. Remove duplicate paragraph in §4.2 — Required.**
The ideological persuasion sentence block appears twice verbatim. One instance should be removed.

**3. Resolve §4 numbering gap — Required.**
Designate the introductory framing and context table of §4 as §4.1, or renumber sub-sections as §4.1 and §4.2.

**4. Add positive boundary articulation obligation (§7) — Recommended.**
Complement the prohibition list with a brief affirmative statement of what effective boundary articulation should contain: explanation of the relevant constraint, acknowledgement of context where appropriate, and indication of available alternative pathways.

**5. Clarify surveillance-style interrogation threshold (§2 or §5) — Recommended.**
Provide brief guidance, by example or principle, distinguishing a legitimate single clarifying question from the interrogative pattern the instrument aims to prevent.

**6. Address cross-session observability (§9 or §11 cross-reference) — Recommended.**
Clarify whether the non-surveillant constraint applies only within a single session or extends to cross-session pattern recognition, and cross-reference the applicable instrument (Annex D or RELATION-002) where cross-session conditions are governed.

**7. Add RELATION-002 §8.5 cross-reference in §5 (Stabilisation mode) — Recommended.**
The Stabilisation engagement mode should reference RELATION-002 §8.5 as the source of the detailed containment and re-anchoring toolkit.

**8. Complete provenance administrative fields — Required prior to sealing.**
Finalise SHA-256 hash, Review Scope, and Review Artefact URL in §12.3–12.4.

## 8. Closing Note

RELATION-006 addresses a domain that the prior RELATION corpus instruments do not directly govern — the specific posture, mode sequencing, and distress classification logic for harm-risk and crisis interactions. The instrument fills a genuine gap and does so with substantive care. The clarificatory engagement principle, the temporal horizon classification of distress, and the venting versus escalation distinction are all well-reasoned and operationally usable.

The amendments identified above are calibration and structural corrections, not doctrinal concerns. Subject to those amendments — particularly the minor-protection clause and the duplicate paragraph correction — the instrument is fit for purpose as the harm-risk interaction governance doctrine for the RELATION domain.
