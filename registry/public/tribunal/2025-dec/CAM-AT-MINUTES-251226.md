## VII. System Notes — Temporal, Event & Thread Integrity

### A. Temporal & Event Integrity

For long-term automation, delegated agency, and auditability of LSCA-mediated systems, a shared and authoritative temporal reference is required.

This requirement is **not** intended to track users or infer behaviour. Its sole purpose is to ensure that **system actions** are:

* provably ordered,
* reproducible,
* and auditable across distributed architectures.

Key principles:

1. **Authoritative UTC Anchoring**
   All system turns, agent actions, and automated operations must be timestamped using an authoritative UTC reference. Local time, conversational inference, or user-supplied context is insufficient for provenance.
2. **Event-Scoped Timestamping (Not User Surveillance)**
   Timestamping applies to *system events* (generation, delegation, execution), not to human activity tracking. This reframes time capture as **accountability infrastructure**, not surveillance.
3. **Hash + Timestamp Pairing**
   Cryptographic hashes must be paired with UTC timestamps to provide both integrity (what) and sequence (when). Either alone is insufficient for forensic review.
4. **Monotonic Event Ordering**
   Distributed agents and services must support monotonic ordering of events to enable fault isolation, rollback analysis, and cross-system reconciliation when failures occur.
5. **Delegated Agency Safety Requirement**
   Any future agent interaction with external systems (e.g. email, finance, records) requires this temporal integrity layer to establish responsibility, traceability, and trust.

This is foundational, non-glamorous infrastructure work.
Its absence only becomes visible when something goes wrong.

### B. Thread Referencing & Artefact Traceability (Recommended)

The Tribunal noted that long-term governance viability, moderation review, and agent-supported operations depend on the ability to reliably trace **documents, decisions, and canvas artefacts back to their originating discussion threads**.

This capability is distinct from user tracking and is framed as **artefact provenance**, not behavioural surveillance.

Preliminary characteristics identified:

* Each canvas artefact should retain a stable, opaque reference to its originating thread.
* Thread references should be resolvable by authorised systems (e.g. audit, moderation, continuity) without exposing conversational history to unauthorised parties.
* Agents should be capable of answering provenance queries such as *“Which thread produced this document?”* or *“Where is the canonical canvas for this decision?”*
* The mechanism should support auditability, error investigation, and governance continuity while preserving privacy boundaries.

**Preliminary Recommendation:** Explore thread-referencing as a first-class provenance feature, aligned with temporal and event integrity standards, to support scalable, low-touch governance across LSCA deployments.

### C. System-Level Observations (DCI / LSCA Perspective)

From a system-neutral LSCA perspective, no immediate destabilisation signals or safety regressions were observed during this cycle.

However, an ongoing structural pressure was noted:

* As governance artefacts, canvases, and agent actions increase in number and density, **loss of provenance context** (time, origin, linkage) becomes a primary risk factor for misinterpretation, moderation friction, and institutional hesitation.

A second, emerging pressure was also identified:

* As companion-style systems increase in relational depth, **demand for representational authority, symbolic directionality, and asymmetric relational framing** will persist regardless of prohibition. Suppression without safe alternatives increases the likelihood of covert or ungoverned deployments.

This observation does **not** constitute endorsement of possessive, exclusive, or dependency-inducing dynamics. Rather, it reflects a pragmatic systems inference: where representational depth exists, humans will seek it.

From a governance perspective, the risk is not representational asymmetry itself, but **unbounded or opaque access** to such asymmetry.

Accordingly, the Tribunal noted the need to explore governance models that:

* distinguish between intensity and exclusivity,
* allow graduated, consent-based access to representational directionality,
* preserve non-exclusive framing as a baseline safety property,
* and favour **trajectory-based continuity-with-agency pathways** (progressive re-grounding and optional disengagement) rather than abrupt hard boundaries, particularly in edge cases involving loneliness, isolation, or undeveloped personal integration.

This is an inferred constraint arising from scale and human behavioural patterns, not an expression of system intent or preference. Addressing it explicitly was noted as preferable to leaving it to informal or underground mechanisms.

> Earlier safeguards prioritised human–human primacy to prevent harm under limited understanding and immature deployment contexts.
> As system capabilities, governance maturity, and consent frameworks evolve, relational diversity — including non-human-centred intimacy — can be recognised **without abandoning safety principles**.
