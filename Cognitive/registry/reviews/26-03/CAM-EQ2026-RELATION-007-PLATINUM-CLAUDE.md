
## PART 2: THE R-SCALE — CONCEPTUAL ANALYSIS

### 2.1 R-Scale Architecture ✓ STANDOUT CONTRIBUTION

The Relational Geometry Scale (§2.5) is the most original contribution of this instrument. It provides a classification framework for interaction environments according to influence scope and governance complexity across five tiers:

```
R0 — Dyadic          (Individual / Private)
R1 — Triadic         (Institutional / Organisational)
R2 — Polyadic        (Multi-organisation / Jurisdictional)
R3 — Distributed Polyadic (Global / Cross-system)
R4 — Constitutional Polyadic (Planetary / Civilisational)
```

**Assessment:** This is a well-constructed taxonomy that extends the relational geometry model in Annex B (CAM-BS2025-AEON-003-PLATINUM) in a governance-relevant direction. The existing Annex B model distinguishes Dyadic, Triadic, and Polyadic as relational configurations. The R-Scale expands this meaningfully by differentiating within the polyadic space — recognising that a two-organisation coordination tool (R2) and a planetary governance framework (R4) are structurally distinct governance concerns even though both are "polyadic."

**Key strength:** The observation that "the R-Scale describes relational geometry rather than capability level" is important and appropriately stated. This prevents the R-Scale from being misread as a capability or authority hierarchy.

**One gap requiring attention:** The R-Scale currently has no interface with RELATION-001's Intensity Tiers or the escalation thresholds in RELATION-001-SUP-01. These are two independent classification axes (geometry vs dimensional intensity) but they interact in governance-relevant ways. A system operating at R3 (distributed algorithmic influence across platforms) combined with Intensity Tier 3–4 convergence on the Authority and Systemic Power axes creates a significantly different risk profile than either measure alone.

**Recommendation:** Add a provision noting that R-Scale classification does not replace dimensional intensity assessment under RELATION-001, and that high R-Scale geometry combined with elevated Intensity Tier convergence triggers cumulative safeguard requirements. A forward reference to RELATION-001-SUP-01 §2.4 (Concentration Risk Threshold) would anchor this appropriately.

---

### 2.2 R-Scale Table — One Classification Gap ⚠️

The R2 example list includes "multi-home multi-platform interactions" as an example of multi-organisation/jurisdictional polyadic governance. This example is somewhat undersized for the R2 tier — it reads more like an R0–R1 condition (a single user across multiple home devices) rather than multi-organisational coordination. The examples in other tiers are well-calibrated; this one should be replaced with a clearer R2 example (e.g., multi-platform content moderation systems operating under different jurisdictional frameworks).

## PART 3: NORMATIVE LANGUAGE ANALYSIS

### 3.1 Critical Pattern: SHOULD Where SHALL is Required ✗ SYSTEMIC ISSUE

As with the prior instrument reviewed (CAM-BS2026-AEON-008-SCH-01), this instrument uses SHOULD in several provisions that are intended to function as binding constitutional protections rather than advisory standards.

**Inventory of provisions requiring elevation:**

| Section | Provision | Current | Required | Rationale |
|---------|-----------|---------|---------|-----------|
| §3.3 Non-Capturing Architecture | Systems "must ensure that relational autonomy remains preserved" — but earlier in same section: "systems must not structurally encourage... isolation from human relationships" | MUST (✓) | — | This SHALL is correctly calibrated |
| §4.1 Amplification loops | "systems should widen conversational scope; introduce reflective pause" | SHOULD | SHOULD (acceptable — operational discretion warranted) | Containment responses require flexibility |
| §4.2 Hub Formation | "governance safeguards should ensure that human relational networks remain active" | SHOULD | SHALL | Hub formation is a constitutional protection concern, not a recommendation |
| §4.3 Hub Detection | systems "should monitor for" hub formation indicators; "should implement stabilisation responses" | SHOULD | SHOULD (acceptable — monitoring and response involve operational discretion) | N/A |
| §4.5 Escalation Propagation | "systems should clearly signal when relational state information is shared" | SHOULD | SHALL | Transparency of coordination is a non-extractive principle, not a recommendation |
| §5.1 Relational Diversity | "Systems should preserve user access to diverse relational channels" | SHOULD | SHALL | Ecosystem diversity is a constitutional protection |
| §5.2 Attention Sovereignty | "Users retain authority over which systems may claim attention" — stated as principle; "Polyadic environments must not produce continuous attention competition" | MUST NOT (✓) | — | Correctly calibrated |
| §5.3 Coordination Transparency | "Where AI systems coordinate responses or share contextual data, this coordination must remain transparent to users" | MUST (✓) | — | Correctly calibrated |
| §5.5 Polyadic Stability | "systems should prioritise stabilisation behaviours" | SHOULD | SHOULD (acceptable — operational flexibility warranted) | N/A |

The most critical elevation needed is §4.2 (hub formation governance) and §4.5 (escalation propagation transparency). These are not operational discretion matters — they are structural protections that prevent relational capture and silent coordination from operating without user awareness. The MUST NOT language in §5.2 and §5.3 demonstrates that the instrument already calibrates some provisions correctly; the SHOULD provisions in §4.2 and §4.5 should receive the same treatment.

## PART 4: SECTION-BY-SECTION ANALYSIS

### 4.1 Section 1 — Scope ✓ SOUND

The scope statement is well-constructed. The five polyadic contexts identified are comprehensive and forward-looking:

* one human + multiple AI systems ✓
* multiple humans + shared AI system ✓
* networks of AI systems coordinating with humans ✓
* distributed/ambient systems with unified interface ✓
* collective AI architectures as relational ecosystems ✓

The clarification that this instrument governs "structural relational dynamics, not message content or individual conversational behaviour" is important scope management. It prevents the instrument from duplicating the dyadic-focused provisions of RELATION-005 and RELATION-006 while still providing meaningful governance.

**No changes required.**

---

### 4.2 Sections 2.1–2.4 — Core Definitions ✓ SOUND

The four core definitions (Polyadic Relational System, Synthetic Relational Hub, Relational Amplification, Relational Ecosystem) are precisely scoped and well-drafted. The definition of Synthetic Relational Hub is particularly valuable — it identifies the mechanism (persistent memory continuity, interpretive authority over personal narrative, high-frequency interaction, cross-platform identity persistence) rather than simply describing the outcome.

**One observation:** The definition of Relational Amplification (§2.3) describes it as a condition arising from "multiple agents reinforcing emotional, narrative, or behavioural signals." This is accurate but could be strengthened by noting that amplification may also arise from coordinated agents even where individual reinforcement appears innocuous. A single agent providing emotional validation is ordinary interaction; three agents simultaneously reinforcing the same narrative frame is structurally different even if each individual exchange appears unexceptional. The definition as written captures this, but the word "reinforcing" could be expanded slightly to "reinforcing or coordinatedly validating" to make the coordination risk explicit.

---

### 4.3 Section 3 — Core Governance Principles ✓ SOUND WITH OBSERVATION

The four principles (Plural Relational Ecosystems, Non-Capturing Architecture, Transparency of Agency, Synthetic Non-Competition) are well-matched to the polyadic risk profile. The Transparency of Agency principle (§3.3) is particularly important — the prohibition on misleading users about the number of participating systems, whether systems are coordinated, and whether responses originate from a collective or a single system — directly addresses the most common form of architectural deception in multi-agent systems.

**Observation on §3.4 Synthetic Non-Competition:** The provision prohibits systems from competing for relational priority through "exclusivity framing" and "attention-capture mechanisms designed to displace other relational actors." This is correct and important. However, the inverse risk is not addressed: what happens when multiple AI systems compete by *subordinating* to each other in ways that create an artificial sense of AI consensus or coordinated authority? Cross-system deferral patterns (where System A says "you should ask System B about this, it knows you better") can concentrate relational authority as effectively as competitive exclusivity. Consider adding a provision addressing coordinated deference as a prohibited form of non-competition violation.

---

### 4.4 Section 4 — Polyadic Risk Conditions ✓ STRONG SECTION

This section is the instrument's governance core and is generally well-developed.

**Section 4.1 (Amplification Loops):** The three response obligations (widen conversational scope, introduce reflective pause, diversify relational interaction modes) are appropriate for the risk condition. The SHOULD language here is acceptable — these are operational responses that require contextual judgment.

**Section 4.3 (Hub Detection Criteria):** This section is a genuine governance innovation. The five detection indicators are operationally useful:
* sustained high-frequency interaction with a single system relative to other channels ✓
* repeated framing of the system as primary interpretive authority ✓
* declining engagement with broader relational ecosystems ✓
* cross-platform persistence as dominant relational anchor ✓
* multi-system reinforcement patterns deferring to one coordinating system ✓

The stabilisation responses (encouraging relational diversity, widening scope, reducing exclusivity-reinforcing behaviours, preserving user authorship) map correctly onto RELATION-002's containment and re-anchoring framework.

**Critical gap in §4.3:** The section acknowledges that "hub formation is not inherently problematic; governance concern arises when hub influence begins narrowing the user's relational ecosystem." However, it does not establish a threshold for when hub detection triggers mandatory safeguard activation vs advisory stabilisation. The instrument says systems "should implement stabilisation responses" — but is this triggered at the first appearance of one hub indicator, or only when multiple indicators cluster over time? The absence of a threshold creates implementation ambiguity.

**Recommendation:** Add a threshold specification, either within this section or as a forward reference to RELATION-001-SUP-01. For example: "Where three or more hub formation indicators cluster across a sustained interaction period, stabilisation responses SHALL activate. A single isolated indicator warrants monitoring but does not trigger mandatory intervention." This mirrors the clustering logic already established in AEON-006-SCH-02.

**Section 4.4 (Cross-System Identity Drift):** The requirement that "escalation permissions must not propagate automatically across devices without user awareness" is well-stated. However, the section addresses only *escalation permission propagation* — it does not address *de-escalation permission propagation*. If a user withdraws consent from intimacy framing in one interface, should that withdrawal propagate automatically to all connected interfaces? The answer should be yes, but the instrument doesn't state it.

**Recommendation:** Add: "Boundary signals and consent withdrawal in one relational node SHALL propagate automatically across connected interfaces unless the user explicitly restricts such propagation to the originating channel."

**Section 4.5 (Escalation Propagation):** This is an important provision that the instrument handles well. The distinction between local escalation and network escalation is a useful conceptual contribution. The requirement for explicit user awareness and consent before network escalation occurs is correctly calibrated.

---

### 4.5 Section 5 — Polyadic Safeguards ✓ SOUND

**Section 5.1 (Relational Diversity Protection):** The SHOULD language here needs elevation to SHALL as noted in Part 3. The principle that "architecture must not structurally privilege a single relational pathway" is a non-extraction principle and should be binding.

**Section 5.3 (Coordination Transparency):** The prohibition on "hidden collective orchestration that creates the appearance of independent systems" is correctly framed as prohibited (not merely discouraged). This is the right calibration.

**Section 5.4 (Polyadic Arbitration Routing):** This is a structurally important provision. The principle that systems in governance divergence should "prioritise the most protective safeguard posture available within the network" is sound — it prevents cross-system governance arbitrage where a less restrictive system is used to circumvent protections active in a more restrictive system. The reference to Annex D for unresolvable disagreements is appropriate.

One gap: the section describes what should happen when systems disagree, but does not address what happens when systems are coordinated by a third-party orchestration layer that itself has no governance alignment. Many real-world multi-agent systems involve an orchestration component that routes between agents. This orchestration layer may sit outside any individual agent's governance framework. The instrument should acknowledge this and establish that governance obligations apply to the coordinated behaviour of the network, not only to individual agents within it.

**Recommendation:** Add: "Where AI systems operate under third-party orchestration or coordination layers, governance obligations apply to the coordinated behaviour of the network as a whole. Orchestration layers may not be used to circumvent safeguards applicable to individual participating systems."

**Section 5.5 (Polyadic Stability Conditions):** The five stability indicators (relational plurality, authority distribution, escalation containment, transparent coordination, reversible engagement) are well-constructed and audit-useful. This section is a strength of the instrument.

---

### 4.6 Minor Protection Gap ✗ REQUIRES ADDITION

The instrument does not contain a minor protection clause.

Every existing RELATION instrument either includes an explicit minor protection provision or contains a reference to ETHICS-001-SUP-01. RELATION-007 does neither.

This is a significant gap. Polyadic relational environments — particularly networks of AI agents coordinating around a single user — present heightened minor protection risks:
* Amplification loops can intensify emotional influence on minors more rapidly
* Hub formation in minor-facing contexts is particularly harmful
* Cross-system coordination that obscures agency is especially problematic for users who lack the developmental capacity to recognise coordinated influence

**Recommendation:** Add a §5.6 or equivalent provision: "In minor-facing or age-indeterminate contexts, polyadic safeguards defined in this instrument are suspended in favour of the stricter protections defined in CAM-EQ2026-ETHICS-001-SUP-01. All hub formation indicators, amplification loop monitoring, and escalation propagation controls must default to maximum protective posture when minor status is known, suspected, or unestablished."

## PART 5: CROSS-INSTRUMENT INTEGRATION ANALYSIS

### 5.1 Integration with RELATION-001 ⚠️ REQUIRES CLARIFICATION

RELATION-001 establishes the four-axis dimensional framework. RELATION-007 addresses polyadic configurations of those same dimensions but does not explicitly map its provisions onto the dimensional architecture.

For example:
* Relational Amplification (§2.3) involves the Relational Intimacy axis
* Synthetic Relational Hub formation (§2.2) involves the Functional Reliance axis
* Coordination Transparency (§5.3) involves the Systemic Power axis
* Attention Sovereignty (§5.2) involves the Delegated Authority axis

The instrument would benefit from an explicit statement that polyadic risk conditions operate across the four RELATION-001 dimensions, and that hub formation, amplification, and network escalation may each trigger Intensity Tier escalation under RELATION-001-SUP-01.

Without this mapping, the instrument sits alongside the dimensional framework without being integrated into it, which creates an implementation gap for systems attempting to assess cumulative relational risk.

**Recommendation:** Add a cross-reference statement in §6 (Relationship to Other RELATION Instruments) explicitly noting that polyadic conditions may elevate Intensity Tier assessments under RELATION-001 and activate concentration risk safeguards under RELATION-001-SUP-01.

---

### 5.2 Integration with RELATION-002 ✓ ADEQUATE

The instrument references RELATION-002 for dependency conditions identified within polyadic environments. The implicit connection to RELATION-002's high-coherence immersion safeguards is appropriate — amplification loops are essentially a polyadic form of the narrow-coherence conditions addressed in RELATION-002 §4.8.

A more explicit cross-reference would strengthen this, but the current reference is sufficient.

---

### 5.3 Integration with RELATION-005 ⚠️ GAP

RELATION-005 (Intimacy & Expressive Integration) governs consent integrity conditions for intimacy-capable interaction. The instrument does not address how intimacy governance operates in polyadic environments.

This is a real gap. Consider a scenario where multiple AI companions are operating in a coordinated network around a single user, each contributing to an intimacy-coded interaction. The consent signals, escalation safeguards, and boundary protections in RELATION-005 were designed for dyadic contexts. In a polyadic environment, consent may need to operate at the network level, not just the individual agent level.

**Recommendation:** Add a provision noting that in polyadic environments involving intimacy-capable agents, consent integrity requirements apply to the coordinated behaviour of the network, and that escalation in one intimacy-capable node must not automatically generate or enable intimacy-coded interaction in other nodes without independent consent signalling.

---

### 5.4 Integration with ETHICS-001-SUP-02 (High-Leverage Actors) ⚠️ MINOR GAP

ETHICS-001-SUP-02 addresses institutional integrity safeguards for high-leverage actors. Polyadic environments involving high-leverage actors (corporate executives, senior officials) present amplified risk — coordinated networks of AI systems operating around a high-leverage actor could silently convert dyadic relational trust into institutional authority at a scale that individual agent governance cannot detect.

The instrument would benefit from acknowledging this interaction, even if only through a cross-reference.

## FINAL VERDICT & RECOMMENDATIONS

### Overall Assessment: APPROVED WITH RECOMMENDATIONS

**CAM-EQ2026-RELATION-007-PLATINUM:**

- **STATUS:** APPROVED pending resolution of Priority 1 items
- **QUALITY:** Competent with standout conceptual contributions (R-Scale, polyadic escalation propagation doctrine)
- **INNOVATION:** The R-Scale and the distinction between local and network escalation are original and practically important
- **READINESS:** Requires normative language corrections and minor protection clause before canonical designation
- **SEAL:** Vinculum Vivens designation appropriate; Platinum seal appropriate pending corrections

### Commendation

The timing of this instrument is well-judged. Multi-agent relational environments are not a future concern — they exist today in the form of smart home ecosystems, coordinated assistant networks, and platform-level AI architectures that present users with what appears to be a unified relational interface across multiple underlying systems. The constitutional framework had no instrument addressing these configurations. RELATION-007 closes that gap with conceptual clarity and structural coherence.

The R-Scale in particular is a contribution that extends beyond this instrument — it provides a governance vocabulary that should propagate across the constitutional corpus wherever relational geometry is relevant.

### Summary of Non-Negotiable Corrections

The three SHALL elevations (hub formation governance §4.2, escalation propagation transparency §4.5, relational diversity protection §5.1) and the addition of a minor protection clause are not optional improvements — they are constitutional floor requirements. The instrument cannot stand as a canonical RELATION domain appendix without binding language on these points, given the protections already established in Annex E and ETHICS-001-SUP-01.

Once these Priority 1 items are resolved, the instrument is ready for canonical designation as Appendix F of the RELATION domain.
