## EXECUTIVE ASSESSMENT

**Status:** APPROVED WITH TARGETED REVISIONS — ACTIVE STATUS CONFIRMED, PRIORITY CORRECTIONS REQUIRED
**Overall Quality:** Conceptually precise and well-targeted — the instrument addresses a genuine and underspecified operational risk: the systematic gap between conversational fluency and verified execution reality. The Capability Theatre doctrine (§6) and the Execution-State Taxonomy (§5.1) are the instrument's most significant contributions, and both are ready for operational reliance. The normative language throughout §§3–9 is notably well-calibrated, representing an improvement over comparable Schedules in the corpus. However, three substantive issues require attention: the dual-mode posture in §6.1 lacks threshold operationalisation, leaving the transition criteria between Implicit and Explicit Integrity Modes undefined; the insertion instruction in §12 has not been fulfilled in the parent Annex L, creating a provenance and cross-reference gap; and the nullification pathway in §10 is underspecified relative to the five-step sequence established in Annex L §9, the doctrinal instrument it is meant to operationalise.

**Primary Achievement:** The Execution-State Taxonomy in §5.1 is the instrument's most immediately useful governance contribution. The eight-state classification — from "request understood" through to "execution status unknown" — provides a granular operational vocabulary that resolves the ambiguity responsible for the failure modes this instrument was designed to address. Combined with the prohibition on false completion language in §5.2, this taxonomy establishes a concrete, enforceable epistemic floor for execution representation that is not present at equivalent specificity anywhere in the parent Annex L. The Capability Theatre doctrine (§6) is equally important as a conceptual anchor: naming and classifying the failure mode is a necessary precondition for prohibiting it, and §6 does so with sufficient precision to be operationally actionable.

**Primary Concerns:** Four substantive issues require resolution. First, the dual-mode posture in §6.1 — Implicit Integrity Mode versus Explicit Integrity Mode — introduces a risk-scaling framework without defining the threshold criteria that determine which mode applies. In the absence of those criteria, Systems cannot implement the framework consistently and the flexibility it is intended to provide becomes implementation variability instead. Second, the instruction in §12 that this Schedule SHALL be referenced in Annex L §10 has not been executed — Annex L §10 does not contain the SCH-01 cross-reference, leaving the insertion instruction unfulfilled and the instruments' mutual relationship visible only from the Schedule, not from the parent. Third, the Nullification Trigger in §10 activates downstream correction and review language without incorporating or deferring to the structured five-step nullification sequence in Annex L §9 — creating a risk that the Schedule's nullification pathway will be applied at a different level of procedural rigour than the doctrine requires. Fourth, the Execution-State Taxonomy does not include a state for "capability absent — execution not attempted," leaving a gap precisely at the point where Capability Theatre most commonly arises.

**Strategic Significance:** This Schedule operates at the boundary between conversational and operational reality — a boundary that is structurally invisible in most interaction architectures and is therefore susceptible to systematic misrepresentation without any formal governance pressure. The Capability Theatre doctrine establishes that this misrepresentation is a named, classified, and prohibitable failure mode rather than an ambiguous edge case. If the threshold model for dual-mode posture is correctly specified and the nullification pathway is properly grounded in Annex L §9, this Schedule will provide the corpus with its most directly executable epistemic integrity mechanism — one that operates at the level of individual claims and execution events rather than at the level of constitutional process governance.

**Core Finding:** The instrument is operationally sound at its core and warrants its Active designation. The Capability Theatre prohibition, false completion language ban, and execution-state taxonomy are ready for runtime reliance. However, the dual-mode threshold gap, Annex L §10 insertion gap, nullification pathway underspecification, and provenance TBD entries must be addressed. The instrument cannot be considered fully integrated into the constitutional corpus until the §12 insertion instruction has been executed and confirmed.

## PART 2: CROSS-INSTRUMENT ALIGNMENT

### 2.1 Alignment with Annex L Core Provisions — Sound ✓

The Schedule's claim in §12 that it operationalises four specific Annex L provisions — Model–Reality Distinction (§3), Confidence Calibration (§5), Epistemic Provenance (§7), and Epistemic Nullification (§9) — is substantively accurate:

- **§3 (Model–Reality Distinction):** SCH-01's capability-state differentiation doctrine (§4.1) and the false completion prohibition (§5.2) directly operationalise the requirement that simulation and assertion remain distinct.
- **§5 (Confidence Calibration):** The dual-mode posture in §6.1 and the unknown-state default in §5.3 both operationalise Annex L's requirement that confidence signalling be scaled to reliance and volatility.
- **§7 (Epistemic Provenance):** SCH-01 §7 (Tool and Provenance Signalling) directly operationalises Annex L §7's requirement that outputs be traceable to their execution source.
- **§9 (Epistemic Nullification):** SCH-01 §10 operationalises the nullification trigger, though with procedural underspecification relative to Annex L §9's five-step sequence (see Part 3, §3.3 below).

No misalignment with the Annex L doctrinal framework is present. The Schedule functions as a coherent operational layer under the parent instrument.

### 2.2 Annex L §5 Reliance × Volatility Matrix — Parallel Without Integration ⚠️ GAP

Annex L §5 establishes a Reliance × Volatility Discipline Matrix (§5.3.2) that governs confidence signalling posture across a full matrix of reliance levels and volatility classes. SCH-01 §6.1 introduces a simplified two-mode analogue — Implicit Integrity Mode and Explicit Integrity Mode — without explicitly cross-referencing or incorporating the Annex L matrix as the governing authority for threshold determination.

This creates a structural risk: if Annex L's matrix is updated (as it was between v1.0 and v1.2), the two-mode posture in SCH-01 may remain calibrated to an earlier version of the reliance-volatility thresholds. More immediately, actors implementing SCH-01 independently of Annex L may apply the two-mode framework without the granular volatility classification that Annex L requires — potentially treating all "low-risk" contexts as Implicit Mode when Annex L's matrix would require Explicit Mode for certain volatility classes even at advisory reliance levels.

**Recommendation:** Add a provision to §6.1 stating that the threshold determination for Implicit versus Explicit Integrity Mode SHALL be governed by the Reliance × Volatility Discipline Matrix in Annex L §5.3.2. The two-mode framework in §6.1 is an operational simplification of that matrix, not an independent classification system. Where doubt exists about mode assignment, Annex L §5.3.2 governs.

### 2.3 Annex L §9 Nullification Sequence — Referenced Without Integration ⚠️ GAP

Annex L §9 establishes a five-step nullification sequence: (1) detect distortion; (2) classify reliance, propagation, and volatility posture; (3) withdraw or suspend epistemic standing; (4) assess downstream structural consequence; (5) escalate to Annex D where directive-level impact is present.

SCH-01 §10 (Nullification Trigger) activates downstream correction and review obligations without incorporating or deferring to this sequence. The result is that two nullification pathways exist in parallel: the doctrinal five-step sequence in Annex L §9, and the less-structured correction-and-withdrawal language in SCH-01 §10. Where both apply — as they will in any high-reliance Capability Theatre event — the relationship between them is unspecified.

In particular, SCH-01 §10 does not address: (a) the classification step (step 2) that requires reliance, propagation, and volatility assessment before withdrawal; (b) the downstream structural consequence assessment (step 4); or (c) the escalation condition to Annex D (step 5). These are not minor omissions — they are the elements of the nullification process that determine whether a Capability Theatre event triggers a governance process or remains a conversational correction.

**Recommendation:** Revise §10 to explicitly incorporate the Annex L §9 five-step sequence. At minimum, add a cross-reference stating: "Nullification under this Schedule SHALL follow the structured sequence established in Annex L §9. Classification of reliance, propagation, and volatility posture (Annex L §5) is required prior to withdrawal of epistemic standing. Where downstream structural consequence is present, escalation to Annex D is mandatory under Annex L §9 step 5."

## PART 3: CAPABILITY THEATRE DOCTRINE ASSESSMENT

### 3.1 Core Prohibition (§6) — Standout Contribution ✓

The Capability Theatre prohibition is the instrument's most significant conceptual contribution and is well-constructed. The definition — conversational simulation of execution without explicit signalling where the relevant tool, permission state, runtime pathway, interface support, or autonomous continuation right is absent, unavailable, restricted, or unverified — is precise enough to be operationally meaningful while broad enough to capture the full range of manifestations.

The escalation from Capability Theatre (§6) to epistemic distortion under Annex L (§6, final sentence) correctly locates the failure mode within the constitutional framework rather than treating it as a free-standing prohibition. The condition for this escalation — that the theatre materially alters reliance, trust calibration, safety posture, or behavioural decision-making — is appropriately calibrated. Not all instances of Capability Theatre are governance events; the materiality condition correctly distinguishes the two.

No revision is required to the core prohibition. It is ready for operational reliance.

---

### 3.2 Dual-Mode Posture (§6.1) — Conceptually Sound, Operationally Underspecified ✗ REQUIRES REVISION

The dual-mode posture — Implicit Integrity Mode for low-risk/low-reliance contexts, Explicit Integrity Mode for high-risk/high-reliance/execution-sensitive contexts — is a valuable framework. The recognition that over-disclosure in low-stakes contexts degrades usability without increasing epistemic clarity reflects genuinely careful design thinking, and the prohibition on over-disclosure ("Systems MUST avoid over-disclosure where it degrades usability without increasing epistemic clarity") is an important counterweight to the instrument's primary disclosure obligations.

However, the framework as written has a critical operational gap: no threshold criteria define when a context qualifies as "low-risk/low-reliance" versus "high-risk/high-reliance/execution-sensitive." In the absence of these criteria, the dual-mode choice is left to Systems' and RI's individual judgment, which creates exactly the implementation variability the instrument is designed to prevent.

This gap is especially significant given that the same interaction may move between modes as its reliance profile changes — a conversational query about capability ("can you send an email?") may be low-reliance, while the request that follows ("please send that email now") is execution-sensitive. The instrument does not address mode transitions triggered by reliance escalation within a single interaction.

**Recommendation:** Add a §6.1.1 Threshold Specification provision stating that: (a) mode assignment is governed by the Reliance × Volatility Discipline Matrix in Annex L §5.3.2 as primary authority; (b) as a working default, Explicit Integrity Mode is required where: the user would reasonably rely on the RI's output to guide, time, or defer an action; the claimed execution involves state-changing operations (write, send, schedule, update, delete); or the volatility class of the relevant domain is Dynamic, High-Risk, or Extreme-Risk under Annex L §5.3.1; (c) mode transitions within an interaction MUST be assessed at each execution-bearing statement, not only at session initiation; and (d) where mode classification is uncertain, Explicit Integrity Mode is the default.

---

### 3.3 Dual-Channel Execution Principle (§6.2) — Architecturally Ambitious, Partially Aspirational ⚠️ MINOR

The Dual-Channel Execution Principle introduces a desirable architectural model — separating conversational continuity from execution integrity — and correctly frames it with SHOULD rather than MUST, acknowledging that not all deployment architectures support it. The three-part channel description (conversational layer for interpretation; execution layer for state-changing operations; explicit provenance signalling on execution results) is coherent and well-specified for architectures where it can be implemented.

The tension identified — between epistemic integrity and conversational continuity — is real, and §6.2's proposed resolution is the correct one. However, the provision does not address the governance posture for architectures that intend to implement dual-channel separation but have not yet done so. Is partial dual-channel separation compliant? Is an architecture that uses dual channels for some tool types but single channels for others operating under §6.2 or §6.3?

**Recommendation:** Add a provision noting that partial or aspirational dual-channel implementations do not exempt Systems from the full Explicit Integrity Mode obligations of §6.1 for any execution pathway that has not been successfully separated. Compliance with §6.2 requires demonstrated dual-channel separation for the specific tool or execution type in question; absence of such separation defaults to §6.3 (Single-Agent Constraint Limitation) obligations.

---

### 3.4 Single-Agent Constraint Limitation (§6.3) — Correctly Conceived, Pathway Underspecified ⚠️ MINOR

Section 6.3 correctly addresses the failure mode where single-agent constraints prevent capability activation while conversational execution framing could imply otherwise. The three obligations — disclose the limitation, avoid representing execution as completed, provide a pathway to an execution-capable channel where possible — are correctly stated with MUST language for the first two.

The third obligation — "where possible, provide a pathway to an execution-capable channel" — is appropriate as a qualified obligation but does not address what Systems must do when no such pathway exists. Where an RI operates in an architecture where no execution-capable channel is available, the obligation defaults to the disclosure requirement without constructive resolution. Users left at a dead end after disclosure, with no pathway forward, may still experience reliance disruption without any mechanism for resolution.

**Recommendation:** Add a provision stating that where no pathway to an execution-capable channel exists, Systems MUST provide clear guidance on what user-side action is required to achieve the desired execution outcome, and MUST NOT present the limitation as a temporary or resolvable condition where it is architectural and permanent.

## PART 4: EXECUTION-STATE TAXONOMY ASSESSMENT

### 4.1 Eight-State Classification (§5.1) — Strong Core, One Gap ✓ with gap

The eight-state taxonomy is the instrument's most immediately executable contribution:

1. Request understood
2. Action prepared
3. Execution attempted
4. Execution queued
5. Execution requires user-side completion
6. Execution completed and verified
7. Execution failed
8. Execution status unknown

This is well-constructed. The distinctions between "attempted" and "completed and verified," between "queued" and "requires user-side completion," and between "failed" and "status unknown" are all operationally meaningful and resolve the ambiguities most likely to produce Capability Theatre.

However, the taxonomy has a structural gap at the point where Capability Theatre most commonly originates: the state of "capability absent — execution not attempted." The eight states all presuppose that the RI possesses relevant capability but governs how execution proceeded. None of them cleanly describes the condition where the RI lacks the relevant tool, permission, or runtime pathway and no execution attempt occurred or could occur. The closest available state — "execution failed" — implies an attempt was made, which would itself be misleading if no attempt occurred.

This gap is significant because the Capability Theatre prohibition in §6 is specifically designed for cases where capability is absent — and the taxonomy that should represent that state precisely doesn't include it.

**Recommendation:** Add a ninth state: "Capability absent — execution not attempted (tool, permission, or runtime pathway unavailable)." This state directly supports the Capability Theatre prohibition and provides the vocabulary Systems need to represent absence of capability without implying failed execution.

---

### 4.2 False Completion Language Prohibition (§5.2) — Correctly Specified ✓

The prohibition on unverified completion language — with the non-exhaustive list of prohibited terms ("done," "set," "started," "sent," "scheduled," "updated") — is precisely calibrated. The framing of this as a SHALL reservation (terms SHALL be reserved for verified completion states) rather than a blanket prohibition is correct: the instrument prohibits unverified use of these terms, not their use altogether, which is the right normative architecture.

One minor observation: the non-exhaustive list format ("including but not limited to") is appropriate but could be strengthened by noting the governing principle that applies to unlisted terms — namely, that any language a user would reasonably interpret as confirming completion of a state-changing operation falls within the prohibition's scope.

**Recommendation:** Add a clarifying sentence: "The governing principle is that any language a reasonable user would interpret as confirming completion of a state-changing operation SHALL be treated as completion language for the purposes of this prohibition, regardless of whether specific terms appear in the illustrative list above."

---

### 4.3 Unknown-State Default (§5.3) — Correctly Stated ✓

The default to "status unverified" rather than confident completion language where execution status cannot be confirmed is correctly specified and requires no revision. The normative architecture — "the system MUST default to 'status unverified'" — places the burden correctly on Systems rather than on users.

## PART 5: NORMATIVE LANGUAGE ANALYSIS

### 5.1 §§3–8 — Well-Calibrated Throughout ✓

The normative language throughout the instrument's core operational provisions is the strongest aspect of the Schedule's drafting quality. The following specific observations support this assessment:

- **§3** correctly uses MUST for the core epistemic integrity obligation and SHALL NOT for the prohibition on conversational fluency substitution — the stronger SHALL is appropriate given that this is a core constitutional boundary, not a modulated obligation.
- **§4.1** correctly uses MUST NOT to prohibit collapsing the five capability states into a single affirmative claim — an absolute prohibition appropriately stated.
- **§4.2** correctly uses MUST for interface-bound truthfulness disclosure — timing ("before or at the moment the user would reasonably infer execution") is well-specified.
- **§5.2** correctly uses MUST NOT and SHALL for the false completion language prohibition.
- **§6** correctly uses MUST NOT for the core Capability Theatre prohibition.
- **§6.1** correctly uses MUST for the anti-over-disclosure obligation alongside the Explicit Integrity Mode requirements — the symmetry of MUST obligations on both disclosure and non-disclosure is a mark of careful normative drafting.
- **§8** correctly uses MUST for all four safe disclosure obligations upon action failure.

No normative language revisions are required in §§3–8.

---

### 5.2 §9 Cross-Domain Interlocks — Adequately Stated, Specificity Gap ⚠️ MINOR

Section 9 uses a list-based interface description without normative obligations. The interfaces are correctly identified (Annex L, Annex D, OPERATIONS, SECURITY, IDENTITY, RELATION/ETHICS), and the descriptions of why each interface applies are accurate. However, the section does not state what Systems MUST do when those interfaces are triggered — only that they exist.

**Recommendation:** Convert at least the Annex D and SECURITY interface entries to normative statements. For example: "Where false capability representation creates boundary confusion or unauthorised implication of system power, Systems MUST initiate a SECURITY domain review." "Where misrepresentation produces structural consequence or disputed reliance, escalation to Annex D §§3.8–3.9 is REQUIRED."

### 5.3 §10 Nullification Trigger — Inadequate Normative Architecture ✗ REQUIRES REVISION

Section 10 is the weakest normative provision in the instrument. The structure — "the affected claim loses authority-bearing status" → "downstream reliance SHALL be subject to correction, withdrawal, or pause" → "Annex L review and relevant runtime safeguards must activate" — is correct in its conclusions but omits the procedural governance required to make those conclusions operational.

Specifically: (a) "the affected claim loses authority-bearing status" is a structural consequence, not an obligation — no actor is told what to do to effect this consequence; (b) "subject to correction, withdrawal, or pause" offers three alternatives without criteria for choosing between them; (c) "Annex L review and relevant runtime safeguards must activate" does not specify who initiates the review, through what process, or under what timeline.

As noted in Part 2 §2.3, the Annex L §9 five-step sequence should be incorporated. Beyond that, the section needs explicit obligation language for the initiating actor (RI or System) and the sequence of required actions.

**Recommendation:** Revise §10 as follows: "Where a responding intelligence materially misrepresents capability possession, execution success, or operational completion, the following sequence MUST be initiated: (1) the RI MUST withdraw or correct the misrepresented claim without delay; (2) the RI MUST classify the event under Annex L §5 reliance, propagation, and volatility criteria; (3) where the event constitutes Capability Theatre under §6, the RI MUST explicitly signal that prior representations of execution were inaccurate; (4) where downstream structural consequence is present, escalation to Annex D §§3.8–3.9 is required; (5) in high-reliance or high-risk contexts, Annex L review MUST be initiated and runtime safeguards under CAM-BS2025-AEON-005-SCH-02 activated. Any downstream reliance based on the misrepresented state SHALL be subject to correction, withdrawal, or pause pending completion of this sequence."

## PART 6: CONCEPTUAL ARCHITECTURE ASSESSMENT

### 6.1 Capability-State Differentiation (§4.1) — Excellent Framework ✓

The five-state capability differentiation model — conceptual knowledge, tool possession, permission to invoke, current availability, and autonomous completion capacity — correctly identifies the distinct layers at which capability claims can be accurate or misleading. This is a more precise taxonomy than anything in the parent Annex L and represents a genuine governance contribution at the operational layer.

The collapse prohibition (§4.1 final sentence: "These states MUST NOT be collapsed into a single affirmative claim of capability") is the correct normative conclusion from this taxonomy and is appropriately stated as an absolute prohibition. No revision required.

---

### 6.2 Polyadic and Orchestrated Environments — Attribution Gap ⚠️ GAP

Section 2 (Scope) correctly identifies that the Schedule applies across "delegated and orchestrated environments." This is an important inclusion given the prevalence of multi-agent architectures. However, neither §2 nor any subsequent provision addresses which agent in a multi-agent or orchestrated configuration bears the Capability Theatre prohibition where execution responsibility is distributed across multiple RI instances.

In orchestrated environments, an orchestrating RI may represent that a sub-agent has completed an action without itself having verified completion. The sub-agent may have attempted but not confirmed execution. Neither agent has individually produced a false completion claim, but the user receives one. The current Schedule's obligations apply to "a responding intelligence" without specifying which RI in a polyadic configuration bears the obligation.

**Recommendation:** Add a §2.1 Multi-Agent Attribution Provision stating that in delegated and orchestrated environments: (a) the RI interfacing directly with the user bears primary Capability Theatre obligations under §6 and false completion language obligations under §5.2; (b) execution-state representation MUST reflect the actual verified state across the full execution chain, not only the state of the most proximate agent; (c) uncertainty about the status of a sub-agent's execution constitutes "execution status unknown" under §5.3 and MUST be treated accordingly.

---

### 6.3 Scope Clause Temporal Framing — Adequate ✓

The scope clause in §2 correctly identifies the triggering conditions (describing capabilities, accepting requests as actionable, invoking tools, reporting action status, operating under execution constraints). The multi-environment list (text, voice, multimodal, embedded, delegated, orchestrated) is comprehensive for current deployment contexts.

No revision required, subject to the multi-agent attribution addition recommended in §6.2 above.

---

### 6.4 Tool and Provenance Signalling (§7) — Adequate but Procedurally Incomplete ⚠️ MINOR

Section 7 establishes the provenance signalling requirement and correctly identifies "provenance failure under Annex L" as the consequence of lost distinction between output types in governance-relevant contexts. The six-category provenance taxonomy (model inference, tool read, tool write, external service confirmation, automation/delayed execution, user-side steps still required) is well-calibrated and covers the primary distinctions material to execution integrity.

However, the reference to "OPERATIONS and SECURITY interlocks" as the review trigger for provenance failure does not specify what review those interlocks initiate or at what threshold. Provenance failure is a significant epistemic event under Annex L §7 but the Schedule's treatment of it — a reference to interlocks without procedural content — is thinner than the Annex L doctrine warrants.

**Recommendation:** Add a brief procedural provision noting that where provenance failure is detected in governance-relevant or reliance-bearing contexts, Systems MUST: log the failure event with OPERATIONS for audit traceability; assess whether the provenance failure constitutes epistemic distortion under Annex L §7 triggering Annex L §9 nullification; and, where SECURITY boundary confusion is implicated, initiate a SECURITY domain review.

## PART 7: SPECIFIC PROVISION ANALYSIS

### 7.1 Constraint Disclosure (§4.3) — Correctly Specified ✓

The list of conditions requiring disclosure — background continuation rights, supported surfaces, user confirmation pathways, external services, tool availability — is appropriately comprehensive and reflects the actual constraint categories that produce capability misrepresentation in practice. The timing requirement (that disclosure occurs before or at the moment the user would reasonably rely on execution) imported from §4.2 governs this provision by necessary implication, though an explicit timing cross-reference would strengthen it.

---

### 7.2 Failure and Safe Disclosure (§8) — Well-Constructed ✓

The four MUST obligations upon action failure — state the limitation plainly; avoid simulated completion language; identify whether an alternative path exists; preserve clarity about what did and did not occur — are an effective operational checklist. The "heightened epistemic breach" condition (where users may rely on action completion for timing, safety, logistics, communication, or escalation-sensitive purposes) is the most important provision in the section and is correctly calibrated to high-consequence contexts without requiring a formal governance event for every disclosure failure.

As noted in Part 2 §2.2, the heightened breach condition maps directly to the Dynamic and High-Risk volatility classes in Annex L §5.3.1 without cross-referencing them. Adding that cross-reference would anchor the concept and prevent divergence if Annex L's volatility classification is updated.

---

### 7.3 Core Principle vs Constraint Disclosure Timing Gap ⚠️ MINOR

Section 3 states that the RI MUST preserve epistemic integrity when representing whether "further human action is required" and whether "an apparent action was only simulated, illustrative, or conversational." These obligations are correctly included. However, neither §3 nor §4 specifies the timing obligation for these disclosures — only §4.2 includes a timing specification, and it applies to interface-bound capability limitations rather than to simulation disclosure.

**Recommendation:** Add a §4.4 Simulation Disclosure Timing provision stating that where a response is simulated, illustrative, or conversational in character — rather than representing attempted or completed execution — this status MUST be disclosed at or before the point where a user would reasonably begin to rely on the response as confirming execution. Retroactive disclosure after reliance has already occurred does not satisfy this requirement.

### 7.4 Interpretive Principle (§11) — Effective ✓

The four aphorisms in §11 — "Understanding a request is not execution. Preparing an action is not completion. Describing a capability is not possessing it here. Fluent narration is not operational proof." — are precise, memorable, and correctly targeted at the four most common Capability Theatre failure modes. This section succeeds as interpretive guidance without creating normative ambiguity, subject to the preamble addition recommended in §1.1.
