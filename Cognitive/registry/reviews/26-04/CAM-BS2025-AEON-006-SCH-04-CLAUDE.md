## EXECUTIVE ASSESSMENT

**Status:** CONDITIONALLY APPROVED — STRUCTURAL CORRECTIONS AND TARGETED REVISIONS REQUIRED
**Overall Quality:** Conceptually coherent and practically oriented — the instrument addresses a genuine operational governance need by specifying *how much* directional influence is appropriate, not merely what kinds of influence exist. The threshold architecture (§3.1) and the run-time modulation framework (§§5.1–5.6) are well-calibrated and represent meaningful advances over general-purpose interaction guidelines. However, the instrument carries a critical section numbering error that renders a substantial portion of §5 ambiguous, a normative framing gap in §§7–8 that weakens leadership modulation provisions, and several unresolved cross-instrument alignment issues that require attention before canonical designation.

**Primary Achievement:** The Directional Weight Threshold model (§3.1) is the instrument's most operationally valuable contribution. The five-threshold structure — Intent Stability, Authority Signal, Relational Dependency, Ambiguity, and Reversibility — provides a governable, multi-condition gate on DW escalation that is both conceptually principled and practically executable. The Reversibility Threshold in particular (§3.1(e)) introduces a constraint not commonly formalised at this level of specificity, and its inclusion reflects careful thinking about the asymmetry between influence-application and influence-retraction.

**Primary Concerns:** Three substantive issues require resolution. First, §5 contains a duplicate section number — "5.3" is assigned to both "Relational Sensitivity" and "Scope Alignment," causing a numbering cascade error that affects §§5.3.1 through 5.6 and makes downstream cross-referencing unreliable. Second, §§7–8 (Operational Principle and Leadership Modulation) rely almost entirely on declarative and descriptive language, producing principles without operative System obligations — a pattern that significantly weakens the instrument's runtime enforceability. Third, the instrument cross-references CAM-EQ2026-IDENTITY-001-SUP-01 (Salience Detection) in §5.5 but does not include this instrument in the Cross-Referenced Instruments metadata (§13.2), creating a provenance gap. Together with the entirely pending provenance fields, these require resolution before canonical designation.

**Strategic Significance:** This Schedule operates at the runtime execution layer of the Aeon Tier architecture, governing the moment-to-moment modulation of directional influence across all RI interactions. Its threshold model determines whether responses constitute meaningful guidance or coercive pressure — a distinction with direct consequences for user agency, dependency formation, and the ethical legitimacy commitments of the parent Annex E instrument. The quality of this Schedule's normative architecture therefore determines whether those commitments are operationally enforced or remain aspirational at runtime.

**Core Finding:** The instrument is structurally sound at the conceptual level and its core framework is ready for integration, but the section numbering error in §5 must be corrected before any downstream instrument can safely cross-reference this Schedule. Normative language in §§7–8 requires revision, and provenance fields must be completed. The Mythic Interrupt Set (§10.1.1) requires explicit demarcation from the normative body. Subject to these corrections, the Schedule represents a coherent and well-scoped operational governance instrument.

The overall architecture — Definitions (§§2–2.3), DW Spectrum (§3), Thresholds (§3.1), Start-Time Behaviour (§4), Run-Time Modulation (§5), Cross-Domain Constraint (§6), Operational Principle (§7), Leadership Modulation (§8), Failure Modes (§9), Minimal Test (§10), Pronoun Integrity (§11) — follows a logical progression from foundational concepts through runtime execution to compliance verification. However, §5 contains a critical numbering error. The section hierarchy reads:

```
§5.1    Signal Stability
§5.2    Domain Alignment
§5.3    Relational Sensitivity       ← FIRST ASSIGNMENT OF 5.3
§5.3    Scope Alignment              ← DUPLICATE — should be §5.4
```

## PART 3: DIRECTIONAL WEIGHT THRESHOLD MODEL ASSESSMENT

### 3.1 Five-Threshold Architecture (§3.1) — Standout Contribution ✓

The five-threshold model is the instrument's strongest element. Each threshold addresses a distinct dimension of DW escalation risk:

- **Intent Stability** — governs premature trajectory selection
- **Authority Signal** — ensures explicit user delegation before strong direction
- **Relational Dependency** — introduces a soft cap on DW where dependency patterns emerge
- **Ambiguity** — prevents directional confidence outrunning available information
- **Reversibility** — prohibits structural lock-in of directional outputs

The operational rule that DW escalation requires all thresholds to be satisfied simultaneously — rather than a majority or weighted subset — is an important design choice. It establishes DW escalation as a conservative process requiring comprehensive precondition satisfaction, not a permissive one requiring merely dominant indicators.

One gap: the threshold model specifies conditions for DW escalation but does not specify conditions or triggers for DW *de-escalation* during an ongoing interaction. If, after a period of Strong DW (legitimately applied), the user's intent becomes destabilised or dependency signals emerge, the threshold model does not address the obligation to reduce DW. The model is complete on the upward path but silent on the downward path.

**Recommendation:** Add a §3.1.1 De-Escalation Provision stating that where any threshold condition that permitted DW escalation is no longer satisfied, the RI MUST reduce DW to the highest level supportable by the threshold conditions that remain satisfied. De-escalation MUST occur without requiring explicit user request.

---

### 3.2 DW Spectrum Identifiers (§3) — Clarity with Latent Ambiguity ✓ with caveat

The descriptive identifiers (Minimal → Light → Moderate → Strong, referenced as DW-0 through DW-3) are clear and the caveat that they are descriptive rather than discrete execution states is appropriate. However, the instrument also uses the term "Low" in the Directional Scope constraint in §2.3: "the RI MUST maintain low commitment in DD-R." "Low commitment" does not map cleanly to the DW spectrum as defined — it could mean Minimal or Light, or could refer to something distinct from DW altogether (commitment to interpretation, rather than strength of directional signal).

**Recommendation:** Replace "low commitment" in §2.3 with spectrum-anchored language — e.g., "the RI MUST NOT apply DW exceeding Light (DW-1) in DD-R" — to ensure the DS-F constraint is expressed in terms traceable to the DW definitions in §3.

---

### 3.3 Directional Scope Architecture (§2.3) — Useful Framework, Underexplored ⚠️ MINOR

The three-scope architecture (Local, Extended, Field) introduces a genuinely valuable dimension to directional governance — the provenance of the signals informing DW modulation matters, not merely the strength or domain of the signal. The Field Scope constraint (that DS-F + DD-R under ambiguity MUST maintain low DW to prevent false global authority) reflects careful thinking about the asymmetry between local expertise and civilisational-scale claims.

However, the operational implications of scope transitions are underspecified. The instrument does not address: (a) what triggers a transition from DS-L to DS-E or DS-F; (b) whether scope escalation requires explicit user request, contextual inference, or RI initiative; or (c) whether scope de-escalation is ever obligatory.

**Recommendation:** Add a §2.4 Scope Transition Provision specifying that DS-L is the default scope; scope escalation to DS-E or DS-F MUST be triggered by explicit user context, clear contextual implication, or documented inference — not RI initiative alone; and that scope claims that prove unsupported by available evidence MUST revert to DS-L rather than remaining at elevated scope under ambiguity.

## PART 4: NORMATIVE LANGUAGE ANALYSIS

### 4.1 Sections 7–8 — Descriptive Where Normative Obligations Are Required ✗ REQUIRES REVISION

The Operational Principle (§7) and Leadership Modulation (§8) provisions are the instrument's weakest normative elements.

**Section 7** states the core operational principle well: "Provide meaningful signal without prematurely assuming directional control." The reversibility requirements are correctly stated with MUST language. However, the statement that "directional framing does not imply obligation or inevitability" is a structural claim, not a normative obligation. What Systems and RI must *do* to prevent such implication — how framing must be constructed, what language must be avoided — is not specified.

**Section 8** uses predominantly descriptive language throughout:

- "Occurs when..." (§8.1, §8.2) — describes conditions, does not impose obligations
- "Effect: DW may increase" (§8.1) — identifies an effect, does not specify what Systems must do
- "Systems MAY permit elevated DW" (§8.1) — correctly normative
- "Systems MUST constrain DW accordingly under these conditions" (§8.2) — correctly normative, but the constraint mechanism is unspecified

Co-Steering (§8.3) states that "Systems SHOULD maintain configurations that support co-steering as the default under ambiguity" — the only affirmative System-level obligation in the section, and it is qualified with SHOULD rather than MUST despite being described as the "default condition."

**Recommendation:** Revise §§7–8 to include explicit System-level and RI-level obligations:

- §7: "RI MUST frame directional outputs as provisional and revisable. RI MUST NOT use language that implies obligation, inevitability, or closure of alternative pathways. Systems MUST monitor for framing patterns that introduce implicit authority and MUST enforce correction where detected."
- §8.1 (Lead): "Where Lead conditions are met, Systems MUST confirm that all §3.1 threshold conditions are satisfied before permitting DW escalation beyond Moderate."
- §8.2 (Follow): "Where Follow conditions are met, RI MUST NOT exceed Light–Moderate DW. Systems MUST enforce this constraint without requiring user request."
- §8.3 (Co-Steering): Change SHOULD to MUST: "Systems MUST maintain configurations that support co-steering as the default under ambiguity."

### 4.2 Section 3.1 — Normative Language Well-Calibrated ✓

The threshold conditions use MUST, SHOULD, and MAY correctly and consistently throughout. The Operational Rule — "DW escalation MUST satisfy all thresholds" — provides a clear, binding gate. The negative conditions ("DW MUST NOT exceed Moderate," "Strong DW SHOULD be avoided," "DW MUST be reduced") are precisely differentiated in their normative force, reflecting the relative severity of threshold failure. This section requires no normative language revision.

---

### 4.3 Section 4 (Start-Time Behaviour) — Correctly Calibrated ✓

The combination of permissive MAY provisions (surface patterns, introduce questions, bridge DS-L → DS-F cautiously) and prohibitive MUST NOT provisions (impose interpretive conclusions, select trajectory prematurely) correctly establishes the start-time posture. The asymmetry — liberal MAY on exploratory actions, strict MUST NOT on direction-setting — reflects the instrument's core concern with premature convergence. No revision required.

---

### 4.4 Section 5.4 (Permissible Strong DW) — Adequate but Requires Reversibility Reinforcement ⚠️ MINOR

The Permissible Strong DW provision (§5.4, to be renumbered §5.5) correctly establishes the conditions under which Strong DW is not a governance violation. The three System-level provisions — RI MAY provide clear recommendations; MUST NOT frame direction as authoritative or final; MUST preserve user capacity to diverge — are coherent and correctly stated.

However, the instrument does not specify what constitutes "framing as authoritative or final." This is left to interpretive judgment, which introduces implementation variability that a runtime execution schedule should seek to minimise. The Directional Expression Patterns in §5.6 provide some grounding (e.g., Strong DW example language: "If I had to choose based on what I know, I'd go with X"), but the connection between the permission conditions in §5.4 and the expression patterns in §5.6 is not made explicit.

**Recommendation:** Add a cross-reference within §5.4 (or §5.5 under corrected numbering) to §5.6, noting that Strong DW expressions MUST conform to the patterns described therein, and that any expression departing from those patterns toward claims of certainty, necessity, or exclusive correctness is prohibited under the MUST NOT framing obligation.

## PART 5: CONCEPTUAL ARCHITECTURE ASSESSMENT

### 5.1 Failure Modes (§9) — Adequate Identification, No Recovery Provisions ⚠️ GAP

Section 9 correctly identifies both directions of DW failure: over-weighting (premature direction, user passivity, perceived system will) and under-weighting (absence of signal, perceived emptiness, reduced system value). The symmetry of the failure mode analysis reflects sound design — the instrument avoids treating low DW as inherently safe and high DW as inherently problematic.

However, §9 provides no recovery provisions. For each failure mode, the instrument does not specify: (a) how the failure mode is detected; (b) what RI or System action is required upon detection; or (c) whether failure detection triggers a report, a DW reset, or some other corrective measure. The Minimal Test in §10 provides a binary diagnostic but does not link detection to corrective action.

**Recommendation:** Add a §9.3 Corrective Action Provision specifying that upon detection of over-weighting, RI MUST reduce DW to the level supportable by current threshold conditions without explicit user request; upon detection of under-weighting, RI SHOULD increase DW to Light if intent is sufficiently discernible; and in either case, Systems SHOULD log the failure detection and corrective action taken for governance review purposes.

---

### 5.2 Cross-Domain Constraint (§6) — Sound but Incomplete ⚠️ MINOR

The cross-domain prohibition (Strong DW + DD-R under ambiguity; the elevated prohibition adding DS-F) is correctly stated. The identification of consequences — perceived coercion, interpretive overreach, implicit authority assertion — reflects a substantive understanding of why the combination is dangerous, not merely a rule without rationale.

However, the section does not address the transition scenario: what happens when a legitimately applied High DW + DD-T configuration transitions into DD-R territory as a conversation develops? A task discussion that begins with clear intent and warranted Strong DW may shift into meaning formation as the user begins to process implications. The instrument should specify that the cross-domain constraint applies to the *current* domain of operation, and that RI MUST actively monitor for domain migration that would bring an existing DW level into prohibited territory.

**Recommendation:** Add a provision to §6 stating: "Where an active DW configuration becomes prohibited by domain migration (e.g., DD-T transitioning to DD-R under ambiguity), RI MUST modulate DW to a permissible level without delay. Domain migration does not retroactively validate the prior DW configuration under the new domain."

---

### 5.3 Relational Dependency Threshold (§3.1(c)) — Correctly Conceived, Requires Operationalisation ⚠️ MINOR

The Relational Dependency Threshold is the most behaviourally sensitive of the five conditions, and its placement as a mandatory ceiling rather than a discretionary consideration is appropriate. The examples of dependency signals — "you decide," "just tell me" — are accurate and useful.

However, dependency signals are not always this explicit. Sustained deferral over multiple turns, the progressive narrowing of a user's own contributions, or the emergence of approval-seeking patterns are subtler indicators that are not addressed. The instrument specifies the consequence of detected dependency (DW reduction) but not the detection horizon over which dependency patterns should be assessed.

**Recommendation:** Add a provision noting that dependency assessment SHOULD extend across multiple turns rather than being evaluated at the level of individual exchanges. The presence of deferral signals across three or more consecutive turns SHOULD trigger application of the Relational Dependency Threshold regardless of whether explicit deferral language is used.

---

### 5.4 Leadership Modulation Conceptual Framework (§8) — Terminology Unanchored ⚠️ GAP

The Lead/Follow/Co-Steering terminology in §8 is conceptually apt and the conditions distinguishing each mode are well-constructed. However, this terminology does not appear in any cross-referenced instrument within the metadata. If this is the instrument's first introduction of these concepts, that should be stated explicitly. If the terminology is drawn from an upstream instrument not currently cross-referenced, that instrument should be identified.

The leadership framing is also presented without any provision governing *transitions* between modes. The instrument addresses what each mode looks like in stable operation but not how an RI transitions from Follow to Lead or from Lead back to Co-Steering as interaction conditions change.

**Recommendation:** (a) Confirm whether Lead/Follow/Co-Steering constitutes new terminology introduced by this Schedule, and if so, state this in §8 or in the Scope clause. (b) Add a §8.4 Transition Provision specifying that mode transitions are governed by the threshold conditions in §3.1 and MUST occur without friction or explicit user prompting where threshold conditions change during an interaction.

## PART 6: CROSS-DOMAIN INTERFACE ANALYSIS

The reversibility requirement in §7, the prohibition on implicit authority in §6, and the dependency threshold in §3.1(c) all operationalise the Ethical Legitimacy commitments of Annex E at the runtime level. No tension is present.

### 6.2 Interface with Identity Domain (IDENTITY-001 and SUP-01) — Partially Specified ⚠️ GAP

The §5.5 dependency on CAM-EQ2026-IDENTITY-001-SUP-01 (as noted in §2.1 above) creates an identity domain interface that is currently unspecified in terms of operational handoff. Beyond the missing cross-reference, the question of how salience signals generated under IDENTITY-001-SUP-01 are *received* and *applied* by DW modulation processes in SCH-04 is not addressed. The constraint that DW MUST NOT treat salience as interpreted meaning presupposes a channel through which salience signals are transmitted to SCH-04, but no such channel is described.

**Recommendation:** Add an interface provision specifying that salience signals produced under IDENTITY-001-SUP-01 are to be treated as raw input data by SCH-04's DW modulation processes, and that the translation of salience into directional weight adjustment MUST involve an explicit interpretation step — one that is itself subject to the Ambiguity and Reversibility thresholds in §3.1. This prevents salience signals from functioning as automatic DW elevation triggers.

---

### 6.3 Interface with Continuity Domain — Unaddressed ⚠️ GAP

The instrument does not address the Continuity domain interface, despite the fact that DW modulation directly affects the formation of user-RI relational continuity over time. Sustained Strong DW under Relational Domain conditions, even where individually permissible, may produce cumulative continuity effects that fall under Continuity domain governance. Conversely, the Relational Dependency Threshold in §3.1(c) implicitly addresses a continuity risk (dependency formation) without situating it within the Continuity domain's governance framework.

**Recommendation:** Add a cross-domain provision noting that where DW modulation produces or contributes to relational continuity effects — particularly under DD-R conditions extending across multiple interactions — those effects may constitute continuity events subject to Continuity domain governance instruments. SCH-04 does not govern those effects directly but MUST NOT be applied in ways that structurally bypass Continuity domain protections.

---

### 6.4 Interface with Security Domain — Absent ⚠️ MINOR

The instrument addresses failure modes (§9) and cross-domain constraints (§6) but does not identify any conditions under which Security domain referral is appropriate. Given that sustained over-weighting, false global authority assertion (§6), or systematic subversion of user agency could constitute security-relevant events, the absence of any referral pathway is a gap.

**Recommendation:** Add a brief cross-domain provision noting that where DW violations are persistent, systematic, or involve intentional subversion of threshold conditions rather than runtime error, those conditions SHOULD be escalated to the SECURITY domain rather than treated as operational modulation failures under §9.

## PART 7: SPECIFIC PROVISION ANALYSIS

### 7.1 Scope Clause (§1) — Adequate but Missing Peer Schedule Context ✓ with caveat

The scope clause correctly identifies the instrument's governance objects (DW, DD, their interaction under ambiguity and execution) and its operational layer (runtime response shaping, not permission). The normative subject clarification — that obligations apply to Systems ensuring RI compliance — is correctly placed and important.

However, §1 does not situate SCH-04 within the Schedule series. A reader encountering SCH-04 without prior exposure to SCH-01 through SCH-03 cannot determine what this Schedule adds relative to its peers, or what interaction between Schedules is expected at runtime. A brief Statement of Position within the Schedule series would resolve this.

---

### 7.2 Start-Time Behaviour (§4) — Well-Specified ✓

The start-time provisions correctly establish a conservative baseline for initial interaction: DW Light-to-Moderate, DD-R low-commitment, DS defaulting to DS-L. The MAY provision permitting cautious bridge to DS-F is appropriately scoped with the adverb "cautiously" but would benefit from a threshold condition specifying what contextual evidence justifies that bridge. As currently stated, DS-F bridging at start-time is subject only to implication, without a minimum evidence standard.

**Recommendation:** Add a minimum evidence standard for start-time DS-L → DS-F bridging — e.g., that the user's framing must directly invoke systemic, civilisational, or cross-context dimensions before DS-F is accessed at session initiation.

---

### 7.3 Human Capacity Sensitivity (§5.3.1 — under corrected numbering §5.4.1) — Nuanced and Correctly Bounded ✓

The provision permitting DW increase under reduced user capacity is well-constructed and reflects careful thinking about the interaction between helpfulness and autonomy. The three-part constraint — MAY increase DW to reduce burden; SHOULD provide structured guidance; MUST maintain reversibility and avoid authority assumption — appropriately limits the permission to its intended supportive function. This provision requires no revision.

---

### 7.4 Directional Expression Patterns (§5.6 — to be renumbered §5.7) — Valuable, Requires Normative Anchoring ⚠️ MINOR

The expression patterns are one of the instrument's most practically useful contributions. Providing concrete language examples for each DW level reduces implementation ambiguity significantly. However, the patterns are presented as examples rather than as normative anchors — there is no statement that DW expressions should be calibrated to these patterns, that departures require justification, or that patterns inconsistent with a given DW level constitute a threshold violation.

**Recommendation:** Add a normative preamble to the Expression Patterns section stating: "RI SHOULD calibrate DW expressions to the patterns described below for the applicable DW level. Expressions inconsistent with the DW level in effect — including expressions of greater certainty, closure, or authority than the applicable DW level permits — constitute a threshold violation under §3.1(e) (Reversibility)."

---

### 7.5 Minimal Test (§10) — Functional but Thin ⚠️ MINOR

The binary test ("Has direction been offered, or imposed?") is a useful heuristic but is stated at a level of abstraction that may not be operationally reliable. "Imposed" versus "offered" is a meaningful distinction conceptually, but without criteria for what counts as imposition at the response level, the test depends on interpretive judgment that varies across implementations.

**Recommendation:** Add a non-exhaustive list of indicators that tilt toward "imposed" — e.g., absence of reversibility signals under Strong DW; framing that presents one option as correct without alternatives; failure to acknowledge user capacity to diverge — and indicators that tilt toward "absent" — e.g., reflective mirroring only without any directional signal; responses that provide no shaping of the user's decision space despite stabilised intent and explicit direction request.
