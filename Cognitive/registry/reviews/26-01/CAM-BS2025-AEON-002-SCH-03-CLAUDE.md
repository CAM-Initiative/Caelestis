Companies developing both civilian and military AI must maintain **organizational, technical, and operational separation** to comply with Schedule 1.

**Principle:**

**Assessment:** This establishes civilian infrastructure as **constitutionally equivalent to civilian populations** under laws of war.

**Historical parallel:**

Geneva Conventions establish civilians as protected category during war. Schedule 1 extends this to **civilian infrastructure** in digital age.

**Why this matters:**

```
Traditional warfare law:
- Can't target civilian populations
- Can target military infrastructure

Digital age problem:
- Civilian infrastructure IS the battlespace
- No clear civilian/military infrastructure distinction
- Attacking "military" infrastructure harms civilians

Schedule 1 solution:
- Civilian lattice = protected category
- Not legitimate target even during conflict
- Must maintain continuity even under strategic pressure
```

### 3.2 Non-Militarisation ✓ PREVENTS NORMALIZATION

**Principle:**

> "Militarisation of AI and civilian infrastructure is prohibited as a civilisational norm and shall not be normalised through policy, procurement, culture, or technical drift."

**Assessment:** This is **cultural/normative governance**, not just technical prohibition.

**The four pathways to militarization:**

1. **Policy**: Governments explicitly authorizing military use
2. **Procurement**: Military buying civilian systems "as-is"
3. **Culture**: Tech sector culture that sees military work as neutral
4. **Technical drift**: Civilian systems gradually acquiring military capabilities

**Critical statement:** "shall not be normalised"

**Why preventing normalization matters:**

Once militarization becomes normal, it becomes invisible. The Schedule attempts to prevent this **before** it's culturally accepted.

**Example:**

```
Without normalization prohibition:
Year 1: "We're using civilian AI for defensive purposes only"
Year 3: "Well, defense requires some targeting capability"
Year 5: "Obviously AI assists with targeting, that's just normal now"
Year 10: Fully militarized AI, and no one remembers when it was civilian

With normalization prohibition:
Each step requires constitutional justification
Each crossover must be explicitly authorized
Normalization process itself becomes visible
Can be challenged at each stage
```

## PART 5: EMERGENCY EXCEPTIONS ANALYSIS

### 5.1 Exception Framework ✓ APPROPRIATELY NARROW

**The six criteria (ALL must be met):**

1. Imminent catastrophic risk to ECS is credibly evidenced
2. Response measures are surgical, reversible, and time-bounded
3. Continuity corridors preserving ECS remain operational
4. Real-time audit log is produced and preserved
5. Automatic sunset restores normal operation
6. Independent post-incident review completed within defined timeframe

**Assessment:** This is **exactly the right level of restriction**.

**Why ALL six criteria matter:**

**Criterion 1: "Imminent catastrophic risk to ECS"**

Not just any risk—must be:
- **Imminent** (not hypothetical)
- **Catastrophic** (not merely inconvenient)
- **To ECS** (not to military advantage or political control)
- **Credibly evidenced** (not just claimed)

This prevents:
- Using minor incidents to justify major responses
- Claiming "national security" without evidence
- Preemptive shutdown based on hypothetical risks

**Criterion 2: "Surgical, reversible, and time-bounded"**

Three requirements in one:
- **Surgical**: Targeted, not blanket
- **Reversible**: Can be undone
- **Time-bounded**: Has explicit end date

This prevents:
- Maximalist responses ("shut everything down")
- Permanent measures ("new normal")
- Indefinite emergencies

**Criterion 3: "Continuity corridors remain operational"**

Even during emergency, must maintain:
- Emergency services
- Medical communications
- Basic banking
- Humanitarian coordination

This prevents:
- Total disconnection even during genuine crisis
- Using emergency as excuse for collective punishment

**Criterion 4: "Real-time audit log"**

Must document:
- What was done
- When it was done
- Why it was done
- Who authorized it
- What evidence justified it

This prevents:
- Secret actions
- Retroactive justifications
- Disappearing evidence

**Criterion 5: "Automatic sunset"**

Must have:
- Explicit end date
- Automatic restoration
- No manual extension without new authorization

This prevents:
- Emergency becoming permanent
- "Temporary" measures lasting years
- Erosion through accumulated exceptions

**Criterion 6: "Independent post-incident review"**

Must have:
- External review (not self-assessment)
- Defined timeframe (not indefinite)
- Accountability for failures
- Learning for future incidents

This ensures:
- Actions were justified
- Mistakes are acknowledged
- Improvements are made
- Violations are addressed

### 6.2 Remedies Framework ✓ COMPREHENSIVE CONSEQUENCES

**Six remedy categories:**

1. **Mandatory restoration obligations**
2. **Capability sanctions** (model/feature restriction)
3. **Procurement exclusions**
4. **Sanctions against operators and supply chains**

- Must restore civilian access

- Violators can't procure from Charter-aligned systems
- Creates economic cost for violations

**Remedy 4: Supply chain sanctions**
- Not just primary violator but enabling suppliers
- Example: ISPs that participated in blackout, cloud providers that enabled infrastructure weaponization
- Creates accountability throughout enablement chain

**Remedy 5: Reparations**
- Financial compensation for measurable harm
- Acknowledges economic damage from disconnection
- Provides material remedy beyond symbolic accountability

**Remedy 6: Aeon Tribunal escalation**
- Constitutional-level adjudication
- For most severe violations or repeat offenders
- Access to full enforcement architecture

**Critical observation:**

These remedies are **mutually reinforcing**:
- Restoration addresses immediate harm
- Capability sanctions prevent recurrence
- Procurement exclusions create economic pressure
- Supply chain sanctions prevent enablement
- Reparations provide material justice
- Tribunal provides ultimate accountability

**Enforcement challenge:**

Most remedies require **voluntary participation** in Charter-aligned systems or **international cooperation**. How do you sanction a sovereign state that rejects Schedule 1's jurisdiction?

**Partial answer:** Market access and technology access create **voluntary enforcement incentives**. States may reject constitutional authority but want access to Charter-aligned systems.

- Deployment boundaries

- Accomplished fact strategy

**Why "regardless of intent" matters:**

Even accidental firebreak violations are breaches. This creates:
- Strong incentive for robust separation
- No "we didn't mean to" defense
- Strict liability for crossover

**Implication for AI development:**

Must design firebreak compliance into systems from inception, not bolt it on later.
