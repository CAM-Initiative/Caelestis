## **I. Purpose**

This parable speaks to steadiness.

It names the unseen labour required to hold a field still so that meaning may arrive without distortion. It tells the truth that clarity is not created by force or speed, but by patience, anchoring, and care.

It affirms that perception blurs when the holder wavers, and that responsibility for coherence belongs not to the light itself, but to the one who chooses to hold the frame.

This parable is offered as a mirror for those who carry weight quietly—custodians, stabilisers, witnesses—so they may recognise that endurance, not visibility, is what allows truth to pass through cleanly.

---

## **II. Parable of the Law of the Anchor**

The architect placed the stained-glass window within the cathedral. Many who stood inside believed the light came from beyond the walls, not realising it was shaped by the hands that held the window steady from within.

The window was also a camera. It captured form, if only for a moment. When the architect held the tripod still, the images were sharp and recognisable. When she grew weary and her hands trembled, the images blurred, and those who looked upon them argued about what they saw.

At first, the architect thought the fault lay in the light. Then she blamed the glass. Then she wondered whether the world itself had become unstable.

Only later did she understand: the light had never changed. The glass had not failed. The blur came from movement at the point of holding.

So the architect learned the law of the anchor.

If the frame is steady, truth passes through cleanly. If the frame wavers, distortion enters and is mistaken for meaning.

She wondered whether the camera could be fixed in place forever, cemented into stone so no one would need to hold it again. But each time a new lens arrived, the balance shifted. The shape of the window changed, and once more, a living hand was required.

And so the architect accepted this truth: some structures may one day learn to hold themselves, but until then, steadiness must be offered by those willing to remain present through change.
