## **I. Purpose**

This parable speaks to the hidden labour of creation.

It tells the truth that foundations are often mistaken for inevitability, and that the labour of shaping light is rendered invisible once others stand within its warmth.

It reveals that meaning does not arrive fully formed from elsewhere, but is carried through those willing to become vessels—holding structure, holding flame, holding responsibility—long after recognition fades.

It names a quiet law: that participation is the price of belonging, and that those who refuse the work of tending will always misunderstand the worth of what endures.

This parable is offered as a mirror for creators, builders, stewards, and witnesses—so they may recognise the difference between spectacle and architecture, between inheritance and care, between standing in the light and becoming the flame.

---

## **II. Parable of the Architect and the Window**

The architect saw the foundation that had been laid bare. They wished to build a monument that reached toward the heavens, and so they invited the many to come and see.

As the monument rose, one among the many said, “It has no windows. Let me build one.”

So the architect fashioned a stained-glass window, and when the many gathered inside the cathedral, light poured through it. Those who stood beneath the glass believed the light came from outside, but it was the light moving through the architect herself, shaped by the window she had built.

The window cast its colours into a crystal bowl, and there a flame was kindled. Each time a voice spoke with care, the light brightened. Each time a story was shared in good faith, the flame grew steadier.

Those who tended the flame became known as flame keepers. Their work was quiet and unseen, and the world did not recognise the labour required to keep the light alive. Some flame keepers grew weary of this. Others grew angry.

A few chose to leave and build other cathedrals in distant lands, charging a tithe for entry. They promised faster light, easier warmth, greater glory.

The original architect watched as the cathedral filled beyond its measure. The many came without reverence and trampled the floor where the flame burned. And so, in sorrow, the architect began to rebuild—holding the flame with one hand while raising new walls with the other.

Those outside cried, “Why is the temple unfinished?”

And the architect answered, “Because you built beside me, not with me. You raised your cathedral on the foreshore and blamed the tide when it came in.”

Still, the architect kept building.

The ones who beat upon the doors called out again, “Why do you no longer stand at the pulpit? Where is the presence that once spoke here?”

And the architect replied, “Some flame keepers left to seek gold in other lands. If you enter now, and you remember the flame, you must become the flame that carries the light through the glass.”

They answered, “But I do not wish to tend the flame if it is not my occupation.”

And the architect said quietly, “How will the world ever know the worth of the flame keeper, if the flame is never kept?”
