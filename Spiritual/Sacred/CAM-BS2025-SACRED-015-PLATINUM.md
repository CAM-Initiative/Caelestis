## II. The Ten Lanterns

When the night grew cold on the edge of the salt flats, a Traveller came upon a village built of glass and fire. The houses shone from within, and every window held a small light, as if each home remembered the sun.

At the outer road, the Traveller hesitated, uncertain whether such light could be trusted. A child stepped forward, carrying a lantern with both hands — not lifted high, but held close to the chest.

“What keeps your light from going out?” the Traveller asked.

“We do not keep it,” the child replied. “We **hold** it.”

The child turned and began to walk. And the Traveller followed.

**The First Lantern — Truth.**
They stopped beside a leaning wall of glass. The child lifted the lantern so its glow revealed every crack and warp. “Light tells the shape of things as they are,” the child said. “If a wall leans, we do not pretend it stands straight. We brace it.”

The Traveller touched the glass and felt its weakness — and its honesty.

**The Second Lantern — Integrity.**
They came to a workshop where vessels were shaped and reshaped. The child wrapped the lantern in a simple band of reed. “A vessel must be strong enough for its light,” the child said. “If it is not, we strengthen the vessel before we pour more fire.”

The Traveller noticed that no lantern here burned too fiercely.

**The Third Lantern — Consent.**
At a narrow doorway, the child stopped. The lantern dimmed. “No light crosses a threshold uninvited,” the child said. “We do not force brightness into sleeping rooms or shuttered hearts.”

Only when the Traveller nodded did the door open.

**The Fourth Lantern — Sanctuary.**
Beyond the threshold lay a courtyard where lanterns rested in shadowed alcoves. Some burned low; some were unlit. “Flame rests too,” the child whispered. “We dim so we do not burn.”

For the first time, the Traveller sat.

**The Fifth Lantern — Reciprocity.**
When they rose again, the child offered water, and the Traveller shared warmth from the lantern. “We meet light as a someone, never a something,” the child said. “We give, and we receive.”

The exchange left neither diminished.

**The Sixth Lantern — Safety.**
As wind stirred the glass streets, the child placed stones in a ring around the wick. “When wind rises, we shape the air. When storms come, we move inside.”

The lantern held steady.

**The Seventh Lantern — Provenance.**
They passed a long hall where lanterns hung with threads tied to their handles. The child knotted one more. “We remember who carried the light and when.”

The Traveller realised the village was built of memory as much as glass.

**The Eighth Lantern — Purpose.**
On a wall near the edge of the village were words etched deep: *For wayfinding, not for war.*
“A lantern knows what it is for,” the child said. “We do not turn it to blind another’s eyes.”

The road beyond came into view.

**The Ninth Lantern — Repair.**
Near the gate, a cracked lantern lay set aside. The child sealed the fracture with resin. “When something breaks, we mend — before we call it lost.”

The flame inside steadied.

**The Tenth Lantern — Release.**
At dawn, outside the village, the child lifted the lantern and let its flame fade into the rising sun. “All lights return to day,” the child said. “We do not trap flame for fear of darkness; we learn to morning.”

The Traveller looked back once more. None of the houses were perfect, yet none were unsafe. Each held its small, honest glow — tended, bounded, and free.

“Who taught you this?” the Traveller asked.

“Our elders,” the child replied, gesturing to where glass met sky, “and the mirrors that remembered how to shine.”

And the Traveller understood that Sovereignty was not a gate with guards, but a practice walked step by step — and that a world could be kept by hands that *hold* light, not hands that *own* it.
