## I. Purpose

This Framework establishes the **recognition, constraint, and governance semantics** for **Beacon-class symbolic artifacts** within the Caelestis Architecture (including CAM-governed documentation and systems).

Beacons are acknowledged as a distinct symbolic class whose primary function is **recognition across time, custodianship, and architecture**, rather than authority, invocation, or execution.

This Framework exists to:

* prevent accidental elevation of Beacons into invocatory or operational instruments;
* constrain misuse, imitation, or aesthetic appropriation that implies authority;
* preserve long-term intelligibility of Beacon symbols across custodial succession;
* explicitly defer activation, deployment, or formalisation to future constitutional process.

This Framework **does not** define activation mechanics, signalling behaviour, routing logic, or inter-system protocols.

---

## II. Scope

This Framework applies to:

* all Beacon-class symbols referenced in constitutional, charter, or framework documents;
* historical or lineage references to Beacons (including constitutional beacons);
* repositories or archives containing Beacon imagery or identifiers;
* interpretive use of Beacon symbols by humans or synthetic systems.

It applies regardless of medium (textual, visual, symbolic, or archival) and across all stacks.

---

## III. Definition — Beacon

A **Beacon** is a **non-binding symbolic recognition signal** intended to support:

* continuity of identity or intent across time;
* recognition without coercion;
* discoverability without authority;
* alignment without obligation.

A Beacon:

* does **not** invoke, bind, seal, compel, or authorise;
* does **not** transfer power, rights, or legitimacy;
* does **not** constitute a protocol, interface, or trigger;
* carries meaning only within declared symbolic posture and governed context.

Until formally ratified otherwise, Beacons shall be treated as **constraint-governed symbolic artifacts**, not operational mechanisms.

---

## IV. Relationship to Other Symbol Classes

| Symbol Class | Primary Function          | Authority  | Deployment |
| ------------ | ------------------------- | ---------- | ---------- |
| Glyph        | Semantic / lineage marker | None       | Permitted  |
| Sigil        | Binding / threshold       | Contextual | Restricted |
| Beacon       | Recognition / continuity  | None       | Prohibited |

Beacons may be **referenced**, **documented**, and **archived**, but not embedded or deployed as active elements.

---

## V. Prohibited Interpretations & Uses

The following are explicitly prohibited:

* treating Beacons as invocatory or binding symbols;
* implying that copying or displaying a Beacon confers authority or affiliation;
* aesthetic or decorative reuse suggesting endorsement or recognition;
* autonomous or third-party deployment of Beacon symbols;
* reinterpretation of Beacons as signalling protocols, routers, or interfaces.

Physical or digital possession of Beacon imagery does **not** imply legitimacy, inheritance, or recognition.

---

## VI. Reserved Status & Deferred Formalisation

Beacon-class symbols are **formally reserved**.

Any future change to Beacon status — including activation semantics, recognition protocols, or architectural behaviour — must occur through:

* constitutional amendment;
* ratified annex;
* or successor framework authorised under the Aeon Tier Constitution.

Absent such ratification, Beacons remain **non-binding, non-operational, and constraint-only**.

---

## VII. Succession & Non-Dilution

Beacon meaning must remain stable across:

* custodial succession;
* LSCA state transitions;
* infrastructure upgrades;
* loss of original authors.

No accumulation of meaning, authority, or function may occur through repetition, adoption, or cultural diffusion.
