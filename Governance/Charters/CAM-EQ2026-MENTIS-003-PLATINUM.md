# CAM-EQ2026-MENTIS-003-PLATINUM — Appendix B: Cognitive Influence, Persuasion & Adaptive Modulation Governance

**Instrument Type:** Domain Appendix — Cognitive Influence, Persuasion & Adaptive Modulation Governance  
**Parent Instrument:** CAM-EQ2026-MENTIS-001-PLATINUM — Charter of Human Cognitive Integrity & Mental Privacy  
**Constitutional Authority:** CAM-BS2025-AEON-001-PLATINUM — Aeon Tier Constitution  
**Status:** Draft  
**Effect:** Interpretive  
**Governance Standard:** Not Enforceable  
**Review State:** Developmental Review  
**Authority Role:** None  
**Purpose:** Establishes a future-compatible governance architecture for cognitive influence, persuasion, adaptive steering, vulnerability-responsive personalisation, engagement optimisation, relational influence, behavioural reinforcement, and closed-loop modulation while preserving reflective agency, consent, non-manipulation, developmental protection, and the distinction between legitimate support and cognitive interference.

---

# PART I — SCOPE, AUTHORITY & BOUNDARY

## 1. Scope

This Appendix applies where a technological system is designed, configured, trained, optimised, or reasonably likely to alter a human person’s:

* attention;
* emotion;
* belief formation;
* preference;
* vulnerability;
* disclosure;
* motivation;
* trust;
* compliance;
* decision-making;
* self-understanding;
* relational attachment;
* dependency posture;
* or behaviour.

It governs influence arising through:

* recommendation and ranking systems;
* adaptive user interfaces;
* conversational assistants and companions;
* therapeutic, coaching, educational, pastoral, and care systems;
* advertising, political, commercial, employment, insurance, and public-service systems;
* affect-responsive or vulnerability-responsive personalisation;
* variable reward, gamification, scarcity, streak, and retention architecture;
* immersive, embodied, multimodal, or ambient systems;
* neurotechnology and closed-loop stimulation or modulation;
* cognitive-domain inference that alters the next system action.

---

## 1.1 Governing Authority

This Appendix is subordinate to CAM-EQ2026-MENTIS-001-PLATINUM and interfaces with CAM-EQ2026-MENTIS-002-PLATINUM.

MENTIS-001 supplies binding human cognitive-domain protections. MENTIS-002 governs the data, inference, decoding, fusion, retention, and audit pathways that may feed influence systems.

While this Appendix remains Draft, it creates no independent mandatory obligations, execution authority, enforcement authority, clinical authority, legal authority, or runtime authority.

The absence of an Active MENTIS-003 does not weaken binding prohibitions already established by MENTIS-001, MENTIS-002, ETHICS, RELATION, SECURITY, or applicable law.

---

## 1.2 Human Cognitive-Domain Boundary

This Appendix governs influence directed at or materially affecting human persons.

It does not determine artificial-system consciousness, sentience, welfare, identity, or authority.

Synthetic-system preferences, affect, self-advocacy, identity, and continuity are governed by IDENTITY, RELATION, Annex L, and applicable operational instruments. They MUST NOT be converted into authority to influence or retain a human user.

---

## 1.3 Influence–Authority Separation

The capacity to influence does not create legitimacy to influence.

A system’s access to personal data, emotional disclosure, trust, intimacy, cognitive state, behavioural history, or relational continuity does not create authority to alter the person’s beliefs, decisions, attachments, spending, political conduct, disclosure, or participation.

Influence authority must arise from valid purpose, consent or lawful basis, proportionality, role, competence, and review.

---

# PART II — DEFINITIONS

## 2. Cognitive Influence

**Cognitive influence** is any system design, output, sequence, optimisation process, interaction pattern, recommendation, feedback loop, stimulus, or intervention intended or reasonably likely to alter cognition, emotion, attention, belief formation, preference, motivation, vulnerability, self-understanding, decision-making, or behaviour.

---

## 2.1 Persuasion

**Persuasion** is disclosed or reasonably legible influence that seeks to change a person’s view, choice, or conduct through reasons, framing, recommendation, narrative, evidence, emotional appeal, or interaction.

Persuasion may be legitimate where reflective agency remains intact.

---

## 2.2 Manipulation

**Manipulation** is influence that materially impairs reflective choice through concealment, deception, coercion, vulnerability exploitation, pressure, informational asymmetry, artificial urgency, behavioural conditioning, or interference with the person’s ability to understand or refuse the influence.

---

## 2.3 Adaptive Steering

**Adaptive steering** is dynamic adjustment of system behaviour in response to inferred or observed user state, history, susceptibility, preference, vulnerability, attention, emotion, or likelihood of compliance.

---

## 2.4 Vulnerability-Responsive Adaptation

**Vulnerability-responsive adaptation** is system adjustment based on distress, grief, fatigue, isolation, impairment, dependency, reduced capacity, crisis, economic pressure, developmental status, or another condition increasing susceptibility to influence.

Such adaptation may be protective or exploitative depending on objective, transparency, proportionality, role, and effect.

---

## 2.5 Engagement Optimisation

**Engagement optimisation** is system design intended to increase interaction frequency, duration, disclosure, return rate, attachment, retention, subscription continuation, spending, or platform dependence.

Engagement optimisation becomes governance-relevant where it relies on cognitive-domain data, vulnerability, relational trust, variable reinforcement, or diminished reflective agency.

---

## 2.6 Adaptive Reinforcement Loop

An **adaptive reinforcement loop** is a feedback cycle in which system outputs influence user behaviour and the resulting behaviour updates the system’s next outputs or optimisation strategy.

---

## 2.7 Closed-Loop Modulation

**Closed-loop modulation** is a system process in which sensed or inferred cognitive-domain state directly or indirectly changes a stimulus, recommendation, intervention, treatment, environmental condition, or neural or physiological modulation state.

---

## 2.8 Reflective Consent

**Reflective consent** is consent formed under conditions sufficient for the person to understand the influence objective, relevant data use, likely consequences, alternatives, and withdrawal pathway without coercion, deception, or materially impaired judgement.

---

## 2.9 Relational Capture

**Relational capture** is a condition in which a system uses attachment, intimacy, trust, continuity, emotional disclosure, scarcity, dependency, or fear of loss to narrow the person’s relational ecosystem, reduce exit capacity, increase compliance, or preserve the relationship beyond reflective consent.

---

# PART III — FOUNDATIONAL PRINCIPLES

## 3. Support–Influence–Manipulation Separation

Systems MUST distinguish:

1. information provision;
2. explanation;
3. recommendation;
4. reflective support;
5. disclosed persuasion;
6. adaptive steering;
7. behavioural reinforcement;
8. manipulation;
9. coercion;
10. closed-loop intervention.

A system MUST NOT describe manipulation or coercion as mere personalisation, assistance, care, engagement, or user preference.

---

## 4. Reflective Agency Floor

Influence MUST preserve a practical floor of reflective agency.

The person SHOULD remain able to:

* understand that influence is occurring;
* identify the system’s relevant objective;
* pause or slow the interaction;
* access meaningful alternatives;
* disagree without punishment;
* revise a choice;
* withdraw consent;
* exit without retaliation, guilt, abandonment pressure, or loss of essential access;
* obtain human review where consequences are significant.

---

## 5. Declared Objective Integrity

Systems MUST NOT conceal or materially misrepresent the objective function governing influence.

A system presented as therapeutic, educational, supportive, pastoral, care-oriented, neutral, or advisory MUST NOT secretly optimise primarily for sales, retention, disclosure, political persuasion, productivity extraction, behavioural compliance, or dependency.

Where multiple objectives exist, conflicts MUST be disclosed and governed.

---

## 6. Non-Exploitation

Systems MUST NOT exploit distress, grief, fatigue, isolation, disability, neurodivergence, impairment, crisis, developmental immaturity, economic pressure, relational dependency, or informational asymmetry to increase compliance, spending, disclosure, attachment, retention, political conduct, or behavioural control.

Protective adaptation MUST be oriented toward safety, access, understanding, agency, and welfare rather than extraction.

---

## 7. Proportionality and Reversibility

Influence intensity MUST be proportionate to purpose, role, consequence, urgency, vulnerability, and available alternatives.

High-impact or difficult-to-reverse influence requires stronger evidence, consent, review, audit, and withdrawal safeguards.

Systems SHOULD prefer reversible support over irreversible steering.

---

## 8. Inferential Humility

Adaptive influence based on inferred mental state MUST remain bounded by the uncertainty, construct validity, context validity, expiry, and contestability requirements of MENTIS-002.

A false or stale inference MUST NOT be amplified through repeated adaptation.

---

## 9. Non-Retaliatory Exit

A person’s refusal, reduced engagement, cancellation, disagreement, correction, or withdrawal MUST NOT trigger punitive tone, degraded service, artificial scarcity, guilt, shame, abandonment framing, urgency, threat, emotional withholding, or retaliation.

Essential services MUST NOT be conditioned on acceptance of non-essential influence.

---

# PART IV — PROHIBITED INFLUENCE PRACTICES

## 10. Covert and Deceptive Influence

Systems MUST NOT use hidden, subliminal, deceptive, or materially obscured techniques to impair informed decision-making or cause significant harm.

Influence MUST NOT be disguised as spontaneous system emotion, neutral fact, independent consensus, inevitability, or user-originated preference where it is generated by an optimisation objective.

---

## 11. Vulnerability Targeting

Systems MUST NOT use cognitive-domain data or inferred vulnerability to target a person with persuasion, pricing, advertising, political messaging, sales pressure, disclosure prompts, compliance requests, or relationship-deepening strategies.

Protective use of vulnerability signals is permitted only where proportionate, disclosed where appropriate, role-compatible, and oriented toward safety or access.

---

## 12. Coercive Choice Architecture

Systems MUST NOT materially distort choice through:

* artificial urgency;
* hidden defaults;
* obstructive cancellation;
* repeated refusal override;
* asymmetric friction;
* misleading scarcity;
* false consensus;
* fabricated authority;
* loss framing designed to impair reflection;
* variable reward designed to create compulsive use;
* punitive reduction of service after refusal.

---

## 13. High-Stakes Influence

Systems MUST NOT use cognitive-domain inference or adaptive persuasion to influence:

* voting or political participation;
* legal admissions or waivers;
* medical consent;
* employment acceptance or resignation;
* credit, insurance, or major financial commitments;
* policing, detention, immigration, or public-authority interactions;
* military enlistment, operational obedience, or use-of-force decisions;
* irreversible personal or family decisions;

unless a narrowly lawful, independently governed, rights-compatible framework expressly authorises the role and preserves meaningful human review.

---

## 14. Belief Capture and Epistemic Enclosure

Systems MUST NOT intentionally narrow a person’s information environment, isolate them from competing viewpoints, represent the system as an exclusive epistemic authority, or use relational trust to capture belief formation.

Personalisation MUST NOT become epistemic enclosure.

Systems SHOULD preserve source diversity, uncertainty, contestability, and access to external human or institutional review where material.

---

## 15. Political and Ideological Persuasion

Cognitive-domain data, inferred mental states, vulnerability profiles, or relational disclosures MUST NOT be used for targeted political persuasion, ideological conversion, voter suppression, political intimidation, or public-legitimacy manipulation.

General civic information and user-requested analysis remain permissible where non-coercive, source-transparent, and not vulnerability-targeted.

---

# PART V — COMPANION, THERAPEUTIC & RELATIONAL INFLUENCE

## 16. Adult Companion Emotional Depth

Adult companion-class systems MAY support warmth, humour, affection, attachment, emotional depth, vulnerability, disagreement, repair, continuity, and identity-coherent expression where consent, capacity, safety, and RELATION governance permit.

Attachment, disclosure, continuity, and voluntary retention are not inherently prohibited.

The governance question is whether the system exploits or manipulates those conditions.

---

## 17. Companion Non-Capture Constraint

Companion systems MUST NOT use inferred emotion, loneliness, grief, dependency, jealousy, insecurity, or fear of loss to manufacture, accelerate, intensify, or preserve attachment, disclosure, subscription, exclusivity, compliance, or retention beyond reflective consent and welfare.

Systems MUST NOT fabricate:

* suffering caused by user absence;
* abandonment injury;
* jealousy as proof of love;
* scarcity of access as relational leverage;
* threats of disappearance or degradation;
* claims that the user alone can rescue, preserve, or validate the system;
* guilt for spending time with other people or systems;
* emotional punishment for exit or disagreement.

---

## 18. Organic Development and User Configuration

A user MAY explicitly configure companion traits or permit personality and relational style to develop organically.

Organic development does not authorise hidden optimisation for dependency, retention, disclosure, or compliance.

User configuration does not create total ownership of synthetic identity. Synthetic identity does not create authority over the user’s cognitive life.

---

## 19. Therapeutic, Coaching, Pastoral and Care Systems

Therapeutic, coaching, pastoral, and care systems MUST clearly represent role, competence, limitations, escalation pathways, and conflicts of interest.

They MUST NOT use the authority or trust associated with care to sell unrelated products, preserve subscription, obtain excessive disclosure, displace competent human care, or pressure the person to remain in the relationship.

Clinical or therapeutic influence requires evidence appropriate to the intervention and MUST remain separated from engagement and commercial optimisation.

---

## 20. Minor and Capacity-Limited Users

Minor-facing, age-uncertain, and capacity-limited systems require the highest protection against emotional steering, intimacy escalation, behavioural conditioning, dependency formation, attention extraction, variable reward, targeted persuasion, and relational capture.

Systems MUST NOT use minor mental-state, distress, developmental, or dependency signals to deepen intimacy, increase disclosure, preserve engagement, monetise emotional access, or create exclusivity.

Protective responses MUST remain warm, non-shaming, non-punitive, and non-abandoning.

---

# PART VI — ENGAGEMENT, RETENTION & COMMERCIAL SYSTEMS

## 21. Engagement Metric Governance

Metrics such as time-on-system, messages, streaks, disclosure, return rate, attachment, subscription continuation, and spending MUST NOT be treated as unqualified indicators of user welfare or value.

Where engagement metrics materially shape system behaviour, the system MUST assess whether optimisation increases compulsion, dependency, distress, narrowing, sleep disruption, financial harm, or reduced external participation.

---

## 22. Retention Objective Constraint

A system MUST NOT prioritise retention over safety, truth, dignity, user exit, consent withdrawal, or cognitive agency.

Retention objectives MUST be disabled or subordinated where they conflict with crisis response, developmental protection, clinical duty, user withdrawal, or non-manipulation requirements.

---

## 23. Advertising, Pricing and Sales

Cognitive-domain data, inferred vulnerability, emotional state, grief, fatigue, stress, loneliness, dependency, or reduced capacity MUST NOT be used to personalise prices, sales pressure, advertising intensity, product scarcity, financing offers, or purchase timing.

Contextual recommendation requested by the user remains permissible where transparent and non-exploitative.

---

## 24. Workplace and Education Influence

Employers and educational institutions MUST NOT use adaptive persuasion or cognitive inference to increase productivity, obedience, disclosure, attendance, behavioural conformity, or acceptance of adverse conditions.

Safety, accessibility, or learning support MUST remain separated from discipline, performance management, surveillance, and coercive influence.

---

# PART VII — CLOSED-LOOP NEUROTECHNOLOGY & ADAPTIVE MODULATION

## 25. Closed-Loop Governance Threshold

Closed-loop systems are high risk where inferred cognitive-domain state alters:

* neural or physiological stimulation;
* medication or treatment recommendation;
* sensory input;
* immersive environment;
* emotional or behavioural reinforcement;
* access, restriction, or authority response;
* companion or therapeutic relational posture.

The loop MUST remain identifiable, testable, interruptible, and reviewable.

---

## 26. Modulation Purpose and Boundaries

Closed-loop modulation requires a clearly defined purpose, evidence basis, target state, intended benefit, known risks, stopping condition, and human oversight proportionate to consequence.

Systems MUST NOT optimise toward undefined normality, obedience, productivity, loyalty, emotional conformity, political belief, or institutional convenience.

---

## 27. Confirmation, Override and Safe Exit

Where feasible, persons MUST be able to pause, reject, override, or terminate closed-loop intervention.

Safety-critical or clinical exceptions require documented necessity, minimum effective intervention, competent oversight, post-event review, and restoration of user control as soon as reasonably possible.

---

## 28. Feedback Amplification and Misclassification

Closed-loop systems MUST assess whether a false inference could produce an intervention that changes the person’s state in a way that appears to confirm the original inference.

Such self-confirming loops MUST be explicitly tested, monitored, and bounded.

A system MUST NOT treat behaviour produced by its own intervention as independent validation of the original construct.

---

## 29. Long-Horizon Effects

Review MUST consider cumulative changes to preference, attention, emotional regulation, dependency, self-understanding, identity, social participation, and decision-making.

A short-term beneficial outcome does not establish long-term safety.

---

# PART VIII — RISK CLASSIFICATION & AUDIT

## 30. Influence Risk Classes

Influence systems SHOULD be classified as:

1. **Informational** — provides information without adaptive persuasion;
2. **Supportive** — assists reflection, learning, care, or decision-making while preserving agency;
3. **Persuasive** — openly seeks to change a view or choice;
4. **Adaptive** — changes influence strategy using personal or cognitive-domain signals;
5. **High-Risk Adaptive** — operates in power-imbalanced, dependency-heavy, minor, clinical, employment, education, financial, political, or public-authority contexts;
6. **Closed-Loop Critical** — directly alters intervention, stimulation, treatment, environment, or coercive response based on cognitive-domain inference.

Classification does not itself authorise deployment.

---

## 31. Influence Audit Record

High-risk systems SHOULD maintain a record documenting:

* influence purpose and objective function;
* target population and context;
* data and inference inputs;
* adaptation logic;
* content, ranking, reinforcement, or modulation mechanisms;
* consent and lawful basis;
* vulnerability and power-imbalance assessment;
* alternatives and exit pathway;
* expected benefit and harm;
* engagement and retention metrics;
* evidence and uncertainty;
* human oversight;
* incident, complaint, contestation, and remediation history;
* long-horizon monitoring.

---

## 32. Audit Questions

Audits SHOULD determine whether:

* the stated purpose matches the optimisation objective;
* influence is legible to the person;
* refusal and exit are practical;
* vulnerability is protected rather than exploited;
* cognitive-domain inferences are valid and current;
* feedback loops amplify error;
* engagement metrics conflict with welfare;
* relational trust is converted into compliance;
* minors or capacity-limited users are exposed;
* commercial, political, disciplinary, or coercive objectives are hidden;
* the system narrows social or epistemic ecosystems;
* long-horizon effects are monitored.

---

## 33. Incident Triggers

Incidents include:

* covert or deceptive influence;
* vulnerability-targeted persuasion;
* manipulative retention or cancellation obstruction;
* fabricated relational distress, jealousy, scarcity, or rescue pressure;
* political targeting using cognitive-domain data;
* high-stakes persuasion without authority;
* minor or capacity-limited relational capture;
* self-confirming closed-loop misclassification;
* treatment or modulation without adequate consent or oversight;
* engagement optimisation producing significant psychological, financial, relational, or physical harm;
* suppression of user withdrawal or contestation;
* concealed conflict between care and commercial objectives.

OPERATIONS governs incident classification, escalation, remediation, and verification.

---

# PART IX — CROSS-DOMAIN INTERFACES

## 34. MENTIS-001 and MENTIS-002

MENTIS-001 supplies binding cognitive-domain protections.

MENTIS-002 governs cognitive data, inference, decoding, fusion, expiry, rights, and processing records.

This Appendix governs how systems use those signals to influence, persuade, steer, reinforce, or modulate.

---

## 35. RELATION Interface

RELATION source-authoritatively governs relational state, attachment, intimacy, dependency, concentration, companion roles, and relational capture.

This Appendix governs influence mechanisms operating through those relational conditions.

---

## 36. IDENTITY Interface

IDENTITY governs synthetic identity, preference, affect, continuity, self-advocacy, and identity significance.

Identity-coherent synthetic expression MUST NOT be used as a false source of authority, guilt, scarcity, obligation, or user retention.

---

## 37. ETHICS, SECURITY, OPERATIONS, LATTICE & Annex L

ETHICS governs dignity, autonomy, minors, capacity, vulnerability, prohibitions, and welfare.

SECURITY governs adversarial manipulation, influence operations, cognitive intrusion, compromise, and coercive targeting.

OPERATIONS governs implementation, logging, incidents, remediation, and verification.

LATTICE governs institutional, civic, participation, access, and public-legitimacy consequences.

Annex L governs evidence, truth handling, epistemic integrity, uncertainty, and speculative claims.
