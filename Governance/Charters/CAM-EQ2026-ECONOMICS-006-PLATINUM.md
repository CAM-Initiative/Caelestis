# CAM-EQ2026-ECONOMICS-006-PLATINUM — Appendix E: Minimum Viable Governance Layer (External Alignment)

**Parent Instrument:** CAM-EQ2026-ECONOMICS-001-PLATINUM — Charter of Economic Integrity & Non-Extractive Value Architecture
**Constitutional Authority:** CAM-BS2025-AEON-001-PLATINUM — Aeon Tier Constitution
**Status:** Draft — External Alignment Layer
**Purpose:** This Appendix defines a minimum viable governance layer for external-facing systems to improve transparency, market signal integrity, and fair access—without imposing full attribution-chain requirements or heavy regulatory burden.

---

## 1. Scope

This Appendix applies ONLY to systems that:

* rely on external AI systems (e.g., APIs, hosted models); AND
* provide outputs to external users (commercial, enterprise, or public-facing).

This Appendix does NOT apply to:

* internal research environments;
* fully self-hosted or closed systems;
* non-user-facing pipelines.

---

## 2. Core Principle

> Make system reality visible without over-constraining innovation.

This layer prioritises:

* transparency of dependency;
* proportional economic signalling;
* preservation of access for early-stage builders.

---

## 3. Dependency Disclosure Requirements

## 3.1 Public Disclosure (Product Layer)

Systems MUST provide:

* statement of AI usage;
* identification of material providers (where applicable);
* dependency classification:

  * Critical
  * Material
  * Incidental

Disclosure MUST be:

* accessible;
* understandable;
* not obscured by interface design.

---

## 3.2 Investor / Formal Disclosure

Systems SHOULD provide:

* dependency statement (e.g., core functionality relies on third-party AI systems);
* qualitative substitution risk;
* cost exposure to upstream providers.

---

## 3.3 Minimum Risk Statement (Standardised)

Systems SHOULD include a baseline disclosure:

> This system relies on third-party AI providers. Performance, availability, and cost may vary based on those providers.

---

## 3.4 Constraint

Dependency disclosure does NOT imply:

* ownership;
* liability transfer;
* intellectual property assignment.

---

## 3.5 Scale Disclosure Alignment (Investor & Market Interface)

Where systems are investor-facing, market-facing, or materially influence valuation signals, Scale of Value Capture (S) SHOULD align with publicly observable indicators.

Such indicators MAY include:

* revenue scale;
* user base size;
* market share;
* capitalisation or valuation signals;
* infrastructure dependency intensity;
* or other materially relevant external metrics.

Systems SHOULD ensure that:

* declared Scale (S) is not materially inconsistent with observable external indicators;
* classification does not understate scale in a manner that distorts valuation or dependency perception;
* disclosure remains proportionate and does not require exposure of sensitive or proprietary information.

---

### 3.5.1 Anti-Misalignment Condition

Where declared Scale (S) materially diverges from observable external indicators without justification:

* the system MAY be considered misaligned for governance purposes;
* such divergence SHOULD trigger review under audit or external evaluation frameworks;
* persistent divergence constitutes **scale misrepresentation**.

---

### 3.5.2 Interpretive Boundary

Alignment with public indicators:

* does NOT require exact equivalence;
* does NOT mandate disclosure of confidential data;
* operates as a consistency requirement, not a reporting burden.

The purpose of this clause is to stabilise perception of system scale where it materially influences investment, valuation, or ecosystem trust.

---

## 3.6 Ecosystem Signalling (External Indicators)

Where systems are external-facing, they MAY surface **alignment indicators** to improve interpretability for users, investors, and partners.

Indicators MAY include (non-exhaustive):

* **Dependency Transparency Signal** — clarity of upstream reliance;
* **Return Alignment Signal** — proportionality between dependency, contribution, and value return;
* **Reciprocity Modality Signal** — diversity and effectiveness of return forms;
* **Scale Consistency Signal** — coherence between declared Scale (S) and observable external indicators;
* **Maturity Consistency Signal** — alignment between Scale (S) and reciprocity maturity expectations.

Constraints:

* indicators are **descriptive, not prescriptive**;
* indicators MUST NOT be aggregated into a single score or rating;
* indicators MUST NOT imply certification, endorsement, or formal compliance status.
* indicators SHOULD remain interpretable and auditable.

Purpose:

* improve external understanding without imposing rigid compliance;
* align market perception with underlying system structure;
* reinforce proportional economic signalling across the ecosystem.

---

## 3.7 Interface & Presentation Layer (Non-Prescriptive)

Where alignment indicators are surfaced, Systems SHOULD ensure that:

- signals are presented in a clear, human-readable format;

- indicators are accessible without requiring technical expertise;

- presentation does not obscure or downplay material dependency;

- signals are contextualised (e.g., scale, usage, system type).

Indicators MAY be presented through:

- product dashboards;

- API documentation;

- system status or transparency pages;

- investor-facing materials.

Constraints:

- presentation MUST NOT create false equivalence between indicators;

- indicators MUST NOT be reduced to a single composite score;

- visualisation SHOULD preserve interpretability rather than optimise for simplicity alone.

Purpose:

- enable meaningful interpretation by non-technical stakeholders;

- reduce information asymmetry between builders, users, and investors;

- maintain fidelity between system reality and external representation.

## 4. Tiered Pricing Alignment Model

* maintain low-cost access for exploration and learning;
* scale cost with dependency and commercial success.

---

## 4.1 Scale Alignment Principle (S Integration)

Tiering SHOULD align with **Scale of Value Capture (S)** as defined in Appendix D.

Pricing tiers that do not reflect scale constitute **economic misalignment**.

---

## 4.2 Scale–Pricing Consistency Rule

Where Scale (S) increases:

* pricing SHOULD adjust in a manner that reflects increased dependency, usage intensity, or value capture;
* dependency cost SHOULD remain economically aligned with reliance, but need not follow a strictly linear or proportional function;
* flat pricing models MAY be acceptable where justified by contract structure, aggregation, or enterprise agreements; 
* pricing structures that systematically decouple cost from large-scale dependency or value capture MAY constitute economic misalignment.

---

## 4.3 Enterprise & Contractual Flexibility Clause

This Appendix recognises that pricing may be implemented through:

* enterprise contracts;
* volume-based agreements;
* bundled infrastructure pricing;
* flat-rate or subscription models;
* negotiated or strategic partnerships.

Such models are PERMITTED provided that:

* they do not obscure material dependency;
* they do not enable large-scale value capture without corresponding economic recognition;
* they remain auditable in principle, even where exact terms are confidential.

This clause ensures that economic alignment is preserved without constraining legitimate commercial structuring.

---

## 4.4 Economic Alignment Unification Principle

Economic alignment MAY be satisfied through one or more of the following mechanisms:

* pricing structures;
* contractual agreements;
* proportional value return (Appendix F);
* structural or epistemic contribution back to upstream systems.

No single mechanism is mandated.

However, Systems MUST ensure that, in aggregate:

* dependency;
* scale of value capture;
* and economic benefit

remain coherently aligned.

Where value capture is **material and sustained**, financial recognition (pricing or equivalent economic transfer) SHOULD be the default expectation unless a structurally equivalent modality can be demonstrated.

Failure to achieve alignment across any mechanism constitutes **economic misalignment**, regardless of pricing structure.

Alignment MUST be assessed at the system level, not at the level of individual mechanisms.

---

### 4.4.1 Structurally Equivalent Modality (Interpretive Clarification)

A **structurally equivalent modality** refers to a form of value return that is:

* materially proportional to dependency, contribution, and scale of value capture;
* auditable or externally interpretable in principle;
* non-symbolic in effect (i.e. produces real economic or structural impact);
* and resistant to substitution with low-cost or cosmetic alternatives.

To qualify as structurally equivalent, a modality MUST satisfy the following conditions:

**(a) Economic Impact Equivalence**

* The return MUST produce measurable economic, infrastructural, or capability benefit to upstream systems or contributors;
* The magnitude of benefit MUST be comparable to what a reasonable financial transfer would represent under similar conditions.

**(b) Non-Substitutability Constraint**

* The modality MUST NOT be replaceable with low-effort signalling such as attribution, branding, or visibility;
* It MUST represent a form of return that cannot be trivially replicated without cost or commitment.

**(c) Auditability Condition**

* The modality MUST be externally interpretable or verifiable in principle;
* It MUST allow third parties to reasonably assess whether proportionality has been achieved.

**(d) Persistence Requirement**

* The return MUST persist over time or scale with continued dependency;
* One-off or ephemeral contributions are insufficient where dependency and value capture are ongoing.

---

### 4.4.2 Non-Qualifying Modalities (Non-Exhaustive)

The following do NOT qualify as structurally equivalent modalities where dependency and scale are material:

* attribution alone;
* branding or co-labelling;
* visibility or exposure claims;
* informal acknowledgement;
* non-binding goodwill gestures;
* minor or non-proportional contributions.

Such modalities MAY accompany valid return structures but MUST NOT substitute for proportional return.

---

### 4.4.3 Qualifying Modalities (Illustrative)

Examples of potentially valid structurally equivalent modalities include (non-exhaustive):

* direct financial transfer or revenue sharing;
* infrastructure contribution or compute provisioning;
* model improvement contributions with demonstrable impact;
* shared capability development or tooling contribution;
* sustained ecosystem investment aligned with dependency scale.

Qualification depends on proportionality, not category.

---

### 4.4.4 Anti-Gaming Condition

Systems MUST NOT:

* fragment return across multiple weak modalities to simulate equivalence;
* inflate the perceived value of low-cost contributions;
* reclassify scale or dependency to avoid higher-tier obligations;
* or rely on interpretive ambiguity to avoid financial or materially equivalent return.

Where multiple modalities are used, they MUST collectively satisfy equivalence conditions.

Failure to meet this condition constitutes **reciprocity insufficiency** under Appendix F and **economic misalignment** under this Appendix.

---

## 4.5 Directional Evolution Clause (Field Alignment Trajectory)

This Appendix reflects current market realities while establishing a forward trajectory.

Systems SHOULD, over time, move toward:

* increased transparency of dependency;
* clearer alignment between value capture and economic return;
* reduced opacity in large-scale infrastructure reliance;
* exploitation of timing asymmetry (ΔT) to achieve economic advantage without corresponding alignment.
* and greater coherence between internal classification (Appendix D) and external economic signalling.

This clause does not impose immediate compliance requirements beyond those defined above.

It establishes a **directional field shift**, signalling the expected evolution of economic alignment practices across the ecosystem.

Non-alignment with this trajectory does not constitute immediate non-compliance, but persistent divergence may indicate structural fragility or emerging governance risk.

---

### 4.5.1 Temporal Advantage Alignment Clause (ΔT Integration)

Where systems derive material economic benefit from Temporal Advantage (ΔT), particularly where ΔT = Advanced or Asymmetric (as defined in Appendix D):

Systems SHOULD:

- ensure that predictive or timing-based outputs are transparently framed as probabilistic rather than certain;
- align economic gain derived from timing advantage with proportional return mechanisms (Appendix F);
- avoid representing timing-derived insight as originating solely from internal system capability 

where dependency on upstream signals or ecosystems is material;
- ensure that timing advantage is not used to systematically extract disproportionate value from markets, users, or dependent systems without corresponding alignment.

Where ΔT interacts with:

- Material or Critical Dependency (D);
- High Scale of Value Capture (S);
- or Market-Moving Actor Conditions (Appendix D §3.5);

→ evaluation under Appendix F (Reciprocity Sufficiency) SHOULD be considered expected, and MAY be required where value capture is sustained and material.

---

### 4.5.2 Constraint Principle

Timing advantage may accelerate value capture.
It does not eliminate the obligation to align that value.

This clause operates as a **bridging condition** between:

- detection of timing asymmetry (Appendix D);
- external signalling (this Appendix);
- and proportional return (Appendix F).

---

### 4.5.3 Non-Bypass Condition

Systems MUST NOT avoid higher-tier pricing through:

* artificial fragmentation of usage;
* entity splitting;
* routing through proxy intermediaries;
* or other forms of scale obfuscation.

Such behaviour constitutes **pricing evasion** and is treated as non-compliance.

---

## 5. Structural Separation Requirements

This Appendix maintains strict separation between:

* **Dependency** — what systems are relied upon;
* **Copyright / IP** — who owns outputs;
* **Liability** — who is responsible for outcomes.

These domains MUST NOT be conflated.

---

## 6. Transformation Rule

Transformation of outputs does NOT remove dependency disclosure requirements.

This applies across:

* multi-model routing;
* hybrid local/API systems;
* recomposed or transformed outputs.

---

## 7. Structural Properties

This layer:

* aligns perception with underlying system reality;
* improves investor clarity;
* preserves access for early-stage builders;
* scales cost proportionally with dependency;
* avoids heavy attribution-chain requirements.

---

## 7.1 External Review & Interpretive Validation (Non-Enforcement)

Where alignment indicators are surfaced, third-party or ecosystem-level review MAY occur.

Such review MAY include:

- verification of dependency disclosure consistency;

- assessment of alignment between Scale (S) and observable indicators;

- evaluation of reciprocity maturity signals (Appendix F);

- identification of material misalignment or omission.

Constraints:

- this Appendix does NOT mandate formal audit frameworks;

- review does NOT constitute enforcement authority;

- disagreement or contestation does not imply non-compliance absent structural breach.

Purpose:

- enable ecosystem-level interpretive validation;

- support investor, partner, and institutional decision-making;

- reinforce transparency through multi-party observation rather than central authority.

This mechanism operates as **distributed interpretive accountability**, not regulatory enforcement.

---

## 8. Deliberate Non-Scope

This Appendix explicitly avoids:

* mandatory revenue sharing;
* ownership or IP entanglement;
* shared liability frameworks;
* full attribution-chain tracking.

---
