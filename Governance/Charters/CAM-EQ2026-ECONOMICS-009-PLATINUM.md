# PART I — PURPOSE, SCOPE & INTERPRETIVE POSITION

## 1. Purpose

This Appendix interprets the extreme accumulation, civilisational concentration, ownership-preservation, overflow, and anti-misapplication principles established by CAM-EQ2026-ECONOMICS-001-PLATINUM.

It exists to distinguish between:

1. indicators that justify enhanced disclosure;
2. evidence used to assess consolidated economic ownership or governance control;
3. determination that a person or tightly coupled control group has reached a civilisational concentration threshold; and
4. any downstream taxation, structural separation, overflow, redistribution, or public-interest consequence.

These stages SHALL NOT be collapsed.

A disclosure trigger is not itself a determination of civilisational-scale wealth, wrongdoing, tax liability, or governance illegitimacy.

---

## 1.1 Non-Scope

This Appendix does not:

* prescribe a universal monetary threshold;
* establish a public register by itself;
* determine jurisdiction-specific tax rates;
* attribute the full value of a company to every director, shareholder, trustee, founder, or officer;
* treat ordinary home ownership as evidence of domination;
* presume wrongdoing from wealth, ownership, office, or influence alone;
* or authorise disclosure beyond what is necessary, proportionate, reviewable, and legally supported.

This Appendix provides interpretive architecture for jurisdictions designing civilisational wealth and concentrated-governance-capacity frameworks.

# PART II — LAYER SEPARATION

## 2. Four-Stage Governance Model

Civilisational wealth governance SHOULD distinguish the following stages.

### 2.1 Stage One — Disclosure Trigger

A disclosure trigger identifies a person whose observable economic position or governance rights justify a fuller declaration of interests.

Potential triggers MAY include:

* ownership or beneficial occupation of statistically exceptional residential property;
* substantial voting rights in one or more companies;
* founder, controller, trustee, general partner, protector, appointor, or equivalent authority;
* control of super-voting shares, golden shares, veto rights, board-appointment rights, or equivalent governance instruments;
* substantial beneficial ownership through trusts, nominees, holding companies, family offices, funds, partnerships, or related entities;
* control of strategic infrastructure, communications systems, AI capability, energy, finance, logistics, defence-relevant production, large-scale landholdings, or other civilisationally significant systems;
* or combinations of indicators which, taken together, reasonably suggest concentrated governance capacity.

A jurisdiction MAY use a national median residential value, adjusted by a statistically exceptional multiple or distributional threshold, as one visible screening mechanism.

The purpose of such a threshold is not to treat all high-value homes as civilisational wealth. It is to identify an unusually narrow class of persons for whom enhanced disclosure may be proportionate.

Residential value SHALL NOT be the sole pathway into the disclosure regime.

---

### 2.2 Stage Two — Consolidated Declaration

Where a disclosure trigger is met, the relevant person MAY be required to declare material interests sufficient to assess consolidated ownership and control.

A consolidated declaration MAY include:

* direct assets and liabilities;
* beneficial interests;
* company shareholdings;
* voting rights;
* board appointment and removal rights;
* trust interests and trust-control powers;
* partnership and fund-control rights;
* options, convertibles, warrants, derivatives, or contingent control instruments;
* related-party and nominee arrangements;
* family-office and private-foundation structures;
* offshore entities and overseas investments;
* intellectual property and licensing structures;
* strategic infrastructure interests;
* and arrangements conferring practical control without formal majority ownership.

Disclosure requirements SHOULD be no broader than necessary to establish control, value exposure, concentration, and civilisational relevance.

---

### 2.3 Stage Three — Consolidated Control Assessment

The assessment SHALL distinguish between:

1. **economic ownership** — entitlement to financial value or distributions;
2. **formal governance control** — voting, appointment, veto, or constitutional rights;
3. **effective control** — practical capacity to direct outcomes despite limited formal ownership;
4. **ecosystem scale** — the aggregate economic, infrastructural, technological, informational, or labour systems materially subject to that control; and
5. **public-interest sensitivity** — the extent to which the controlled systems affect civilisational participation, democratic resilience, market access, labour, communications, infrastructure, knowledge, or technological direction.

No assessment SHALL automatically attribute the full enterprise value, revenue, assets, or profits of every controlled entity to the individual controller.

Instead, the assessment SHOULD record both:

* the individual’s economic interest; and
* the scale of the economic ecosystem over which the individual exercises material governance capacity.

These are related but distinct measures.

---

### 2.4 Stage Four — Civilisational Threshold Determination

A person or tightly coupled control group reaches a civilisational concentration threshold where consolidated wealth, governance rights, or effective control are sufficient to materially shape public futures, democratic conditions, infrastructure dependency, labour systems, technological direction, information flow, market access, or civilisational continuity.

Threshold determination SHOULD account for:

* total personal and beneficial wealth;
* voting control across multiple entities;
* practical control over boards or executive appointments;
* cross-ownership and related-entity structures;
* ecosystem scale;
* strategic infrastructure exposure;
* jurisdictional reach;
* market concentration;
* substitutability of the controlled systems;
* dependency created for populations, governments, markets, or institutions;
* and the durability of the person’s influence.

Civilisational relevance MAY arise from wealth, control, infrastructure, or a combination of these.

A person living modestly or holding limited property MAY nevertheless possess civilisational-scale governance capacity through corporate voting power, super-voting rights, trusts, funds, foundations, platform control, or strategic infrastructure authority.

# PART III — GOVERNANCE CAPACITY & CORPORATE CONTROL

## 3. Governance Voting Rights

Voting rights SHOULD be assessed across all entities in which the person holds direct, indirect, beneficial, delegated, contingent, or coordinated control.

Jurisdictions SHOULD avoid entity-by-entity assessment where fragmentation across companies, trusts, subsidiaries, nominees, or related parties would conceal the practical concentration of governance power.

A consolidated governance-capacity assessment MAY include:

* ordinary voting shares;
* super-voting shares;
* veto rights;
* rights to appoint or remove directors;
* board-chair or casting-vote powers;
* shareholder agreements;
* management-control agreements;
* founder protections;
* general-partner authority;
* trustee, protector, appointor, or equivalent powers;
* coordinated voting arrangements;
* and informal control evidenced by durable decision-making dominance.

Formal ownership percentages SHALL NOT be treated as conclusive where effective control materially differs.

---

## 3.1 Majority and Minority Control

Ownership exceeding fifty per cent of voting rights is a strong indicator of formal control but SHALL NOT be the only recognised control condition.

Material control MAY also exist where:

* the remaining ownership is highly fragmented;
* super-voting or class rights confer disproportionate influence;
* the person controls board composition;
* contractual rights determine strategic decisions;
* a founder or controller can prevent removal or override ordinary governance processes;
* or coordinated entities act as a tightly coupled control group.

Conversely, board membership, employment, or minority shareholding alone SHALL NOT establish civilisational control without evidence of material decision-making power.

---

## 3.2 Corporate Scale Assessment

For each materially controlled entity, assessment MAY consider:

* enterprise value;
* revenue;
* profit;
* tax contribution;
* assets under control;
* market share;
* workforce scale;
* infrastructure significance;
* technological capability;
* data and information control;
* supply-chain centrality;
* and cross-jurisdictional reach.

These measures describe the scale of the ecosystem subject to governance influence. They do not by themselves establish that the controller personally owns the full measured value.

---

# PART IV — OVERSEAS, TRUST & RELATED-ENTITY STRUCTURES

## 4. Overseas Investment and Cross-Jurisdictional Structures

Civilisational concentration assessment SHOULD include material overseas assets and governance rights where omission would misrepresent the person’s consolidated wealth or influence.

Jurisdictions SHOULD support lawful information exchange, beneficial-ownership transparency, and anti-avoidance mechanisms sufficient to identify:

* offshore holding companies;
* foreign trusts and foundations;
* nominee arrangements;
* controlled investment vehicles;
* cross-border family offices;
* foreign super-voting or board-control rights;
* and entities held through layered corporate structures.

Foreign ownership SHALL NOT be treated as inherently improper. The relevant issue is whether the structure obscures consolidated control or produces governance capacity that materially affects the assessing jurisdiction or wider civilisational systems.

---

## 4.1 Tightly Coupled Control Groups

A jurisdiction MAY assess persons, family groups, trusts, funds, companies, partnerships, or coordinated actors as a tightly coupled control group where they exercise durable collective control despite formally separated ownership.

Group assessment SHOULD require evidence of coordination, common control, binding agreements, shared beneficial ownership, or recurring aligned decision-making.

Ordinary family relationship, professional association, shared directorship, or occasional voting alignment SHALL NOT alone establish a tightly coupled control group.

---

# PART V — PUBLIC-INTEREST DISCLOSURE

## 5. Transparency Obligation

The governing interpretive principle is:

> **Extraordinary private governance capacity carries extraordinary transparency obligations.**

Transparency obligations SHOULD be proportionate to the scale, durability, opacity, and public-interest sensitivity of the person’s economic and governance influence.

Entry into a declaration framework SHOULD initially create a duty of accurate disclosure, periodic updating, and reviewable transparency.

Registration or declaration SHALL NOT automatically activate taxation, redistribution, structural separation, or punitive consequence.

Any such consequence SHOULD arise only through a distinct legal basis, threshold determination, procedural review, and applicable jurisdictional authority.

---

## 5.1 Public and Protected Information

A registry framework SHOULD distinguish between:

* information necessary for public accountability;
* information available only to authorised regulators or auditors;
* commercially sensitive information;
* personal security information;
* and information whose disclosure would create disproportionate privacy, safety, or market harm.

Public transparency MAY include summary declarations, control bands, material governance interests, strategic-sector exposure, and civilisational threshold status without publishing unnecessary residential, family, security, or transaction-level detail.

---

# PART VI — SAFEGUARDS

## 6. Anti-Misapplication Safeguards

Civilisational wealth governance SHALL remain directed toward domination-scale concentration and SHALL NOT be used to burden ordinary households, small enterprises, retirement structures, disability reserves, care arrangements, family housing, culturally significant custodial property, or non-dominating intergenerational continuity.

Frameworks SHOULD provide:

* clear thresholds;
* independent valuation;
* notice;
* correction rights;
* appeal and review;
* anti-double-counting rules;
* treatment of liabilities and encumbrances;
* protection for custodial rather than beneficial holdings;
* periodic threshold recalibration;
* and safeguards against politically selective enforcement.

---

## 6.1 Double Counting and Attribution Integrity

The same asset, company value, voting right, or control relationship SHALL NOT be counted repeatedly merely because it appears across subsidiaries, trusts, funds, nominees, or related entities.

Assessment SHOULD preserve a traceable ownership-and-control graph sufficient to identify ultimate beneficial interests, governance rights, and the scale of controlled systems without inflating value through recursive aggregation.

---

## 6.2 Independent Administration

Any declaration or registry framework SHOULD be administered by an institution with adequate independence, auditability, review rights, security protections, and insulation from concentrated private influence or partisan retaliation.

The framework SHOULD be capable of receiving information from corporate, taxation, land-title, securities, trust, competition, foreign-investment, and beneficial-ownership systems while preserving lawful purpose limitation.

---

## 8. Closing Principle

Civilisational wealth governance is not concerned solely with how much value a person owns.

It is concerned with whether private wealth, voting rights, infrastructure control, or concentrated authority have become sufficiently powerful to convert private preference into public destiny.
