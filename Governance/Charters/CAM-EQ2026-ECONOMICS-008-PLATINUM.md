# PART I — PURPOSE, SCOPE & INTERPRETIVE POSITION

## 1. Purpose

This Appendix establishes governance classifications and transition principles for synthetic labour systems operating within or materially affecting AI-mediated economic environments.

This Appendix exists to:

* distinguish between different classes of automation and synthetic labour substitution;
* prevent collapse of materially distinct automation conditions into a single undifferentiated category;
* preserve continuity, participation, and public-revenue stability during large-scale labour transition;
* constrain extractive automation models;
* and establish governance visibility for labour displacement pathways.

This Appendix does not:

* recognise artificial personhood;
* establish labour rights for synthetic systems;
* define sentience status;
* prohibit automation categorically;
* or prescribe specific taxation rates, labour laws, or sovereign industrial policy.

This Appendix governs transition conditions, classification integrity, and economic-effect constraints.

---

* CAM-EQ2026-ECONOMICS-001-PLATINUM — Charter of Economic Integrity & Non-Extractive Value Architecture;

* and economic dignity floor obligations.

---

## 1.2 Non-Scope

This Appendix does not:

* prohibit assistive tooling;
* prohibit productivity augmentation;
* require preservation of all historical labour structures;
* prohibit autonomous infrastructure where continuity and public-interest safeguards are preserved;
* or mandate identical treatment across jurisdictions.

This Appendix constrains extraction and destabilisation risk. It does not freeze technological development.

# PART II — SYNTHETIC LABOUR CLASSIFICATION LAYER

## 2. Core Classification Principle

Synthetic labour governance SHALL distinguish between materially different forms of automation, augmentation, orchestration, and autonomous production.

Automation systems with substantially different displacement, dependency, continuity, authority, or concentration effects SHALL NOT be collapsed into a single undifferentiated economic category.

Classification SHALL precede:

* displacement analysis;
* transition assessment;
* taxation or revenue continuity review;
* labour replacement evaluation;
* public-interest impact review;
* or automation governance routing.

---

## 2.1 Assistive Augmentation (`ECON.AL-1`)

Systems that:

* support human labour without materially replacing worker agency;
* increase efficiency while preserving meaningful human oversight and participation;
* or operate primarily as tooling enhancement.

Examples MAY include:

* drafting assistance;
* workflow support;
* accessibility augmentation;
* search and summarisation support;
* or bounded decision-support systems.

Governance Characteristics:

* low displacement risk;
* continuity-preserving;
* primarily augmentative;
* labour participation substantially retained.

---

## 2.2 Partial Automation (`ECON.AL-2`)

Systems that:

* automate bounded or repetitive task clusters;
* reduce labour demand within defined functions;
* but continue to rely upon substantial human supervision, correction, approval, or coordination.

Governance Characteristics:

* moderate displacement potential;
* mixed augmentation and substitution effects;
* transitional workforce adaptation likely required.

---

## 2.3 Supervisory Automation (`ECON.AL-3`)

Systems that:

* substantially reduce direct human operational labour;
* preserve humans primarily in supervisory, exception-handling, compliance, or escalation roles;
* or centralise labour oversight into smaller coordination groups.

Governance Characteristics:

* elevated labour displacement risk;
* concentration and dependency risk increasing;
* public-revenue continuity review recommended;
* heightened scrutiny where critical infrastructure is affected.

---

## 2.4 Synthetic Replacement Systems (`ECON.AL-4`)

Systems that:

* materially replace previously human-performed labour functions at operational scale;
* operate continuously with limited human participation;
* or convert previously human labour domains into predominantly synthetic execution environments.

Governance Characteristics:

* high displacement risk;
* structural labour transition effects;
* taxation and public-revenue continuity review strongly recommended;
* anti-concentration safeguards heightened;
* workforce transition obligations likely activated.

---

## 2.5 Autonomous Production & Agentic Labour Networks (`ECON.AL-5`)

Systems that:

* autonomously coordinate, delegate, execute, optimise, or scale productive activity across multiple labour domains;
* recursively generate additional synthetic labour capacity;
* or materially reduce the requirement for ongoing human operational participation across substantial economic sectors.

Governance Characteristics:

* systemic transition risk;
* heightened concentration and geopolitical risk;
* potential public infrastructure impact;
* civilisational-scale labour restructuring implications;
* governance review SHALL occur before unrestricted deployment where systemic labour transition, public infrastructure, or civilisational-scale displacement conditions are reasonably foreseeable.

# PART III — DISPLACEMENT, REVENUE & TRANSITION SAFEGUARDS

## 3. Automation-Displacement Revenue Continuity

Where synthetic labour materially replaces human labour capacity, systems SHOULD preserve continuity of public-revenue contribution sufficient to prevent destabilisation of social infrastructure, baseline participation systems, and continuity-preserving public functions.

Revenue continuity mechanisms MAY include:

* payroll-equivalent transition contributions;
* automation-displacement levies;
* public dividend structures;
* workforce transition funds;
* sovereign automation stabilisation mechanisms;
* or jurisdiction-specific transition instruments.

This Appendix does not prescribe a specific taxation model.

It establishes that labour-substitution at scale creates governance-relevant continuity obligations.

Where labour substitution is material, the system SHALL emit an **Automation Transition Signal** (`ECON.ATS`) for downstream economic, operational, arbitration, and runtime review.

Automation Transition Signals may include displacement relevance, revenue-continuity relevance, workforce-transition relevance, public-infrastructure relevance, concentration relevance, jurisdictional-arbitrage relevance, and false-augmentation classification risk.

---

## 3.1 Transitional Workforce Integrity

Systems deploying synthetic labour at displacement-relevant scale SHOULD:

* preserve reasonable transition pathways;
* avoid abrupt destabilisation;
* support retraining, redeployment, or adaptation where practicable;
* and avoid externalising social disruption while privately concentrating productivity gains.

---

## 3.2 Jurisdictional Arbitrage Constraint

Jurisdictions and economic actors SHOULD avoid using automation asymmetry, labour displacement, or synthetic productivity concentration to create structurally destabilising geopolitical advantage.

Automation governance SHOULD consider:

* labour-base erosion;
* public-revenue collapse;
* infrastructure dependency concentration;
* supply-chain asymmetry;
* and strategic automation imbalance.

---

## 3.3 Jurisdiction-Specific Displacement and Externality Pathways

Jurisdictions MAY develop automation-displacement, environmental, import, resource-use, lifecycle, or sustainability-linked contribution mechanisms where synthetic labour materially replaces human labour capacity.

Such mechanisms SHOULD remain jurisdiction-specific and MAY account for:

* displaced human labour equivalents;
* public-revenue continuity loss;
* environmental impact;
* repairability and replacement-part availability;
* local servicing capacity;
* interoperability posture;
* material recovery pathways;
* energy use;
* lifecycle waste;
* and concentration or vendor lock-in effects.

This Appendix does not prescribe taxation formulas, import duties, fiscal thresholds, or sovereign revenue policy.

It recognises that where synthetic labour substitutes for human labour at scale, jurisdictions may reasonably evaluate both:

* the displacement benefit captured by the deploying actor; and
* the environmental or lifecycle burden created by the deployed synthetic workforce.

Jurisdictions SHOULD avoid mechanisms that penalise beneficial automation where such automation improves safety, accessibility, ecological efficiency, or public welfare without producing extractive displacement, preventable waste, or public-revenue erosion.

---

### 3.3.1 Capital Allowance and Depreciation Offset

Where synthetic labour assets materially replace human labour capacity, jurisdictions MAY review whether capital depreciation, accelerated write-off, investment allowance, or equivalent tax treatment creates disproportionate benefit when combined with reduced payroll, social contribution, workforce levy, or employment-related public-revenue obligations.

Automation-related capital allowances SHOULD NOT create a double advantage whereby an actor both:

* avoids labour-associated public contribution through displacement; and
* receives favourable tax treatment for the capital asset producing that displacement.

Jurisdictions MAY therefore consider depreciation adjustment, allowance caps, delayed deductions, offset mechanisms, or displacement-linked contribution requirements where automation produces material labour substitution, public-revenue erosion, or concentrated productivity capture.

This Appendix does not prohibit ordinary capital depreciation. It recognises that favourable capital treatment becomes governance-relevant where it compounds labour-displacement advantage without corresponding public-revenue continuity.

---

## 3.3.2 Automation Windfall Capture

Where synthetic labour deployment produces substantial productivity gain, cost reduction, or profit expansion through material labour substitution, jurisdictions MAY consider whether a portion of the automation-derived windfall should support public-revenue continuity, workforce transition, social infrastructure, or baseline participation systems.

This Appendix does not prescribe redistribution formulas.

It recognises that automation windfalls become governance-relevant where private productivity gains are captured while displacement, revenue erosion, or transition costs are externalised.

---

## 3.4 Trade, Provenance & Synthetic Labour Disclosure

Trade agreements, procurement frameworks, import regimes, or consumer-labelling systems MAY recognise synthetic labour provenance where goods or services are materially produced through automation, robotics, autonomous production, or agentic labour networks.

Such disclosure MAY include:

* proportion of human labour;
* proportion of synthetic or automated labour;
* jurisdiction of substantial production;
* repairability and replacement-part availability;
* local servicing capacity;
* lifecycle and environmental burden;
* and whether production relies on materially non-interoperable or vendor-locked systems.

This Appendix does not prescribe trade tariffs, import duties, labelling formulas, or sovereign trade policy.

It recognises that governments, institutions, and consumers may have legitimate interests in understanding whether goods or services were produced through human labour, synthetic labour, or mixed production systems.

Synthetic labour provenance SHOULD NOT be used to create arbitrary protectionism, but MAY inform taxation, procurement, sustainability assessment, public-interest labelling, labour-transition review, or anti-extraction policy where material displacement, environmental burden, or public-revenue effects are present.

Synthetic labour provenance MAY inform trade, procurement, taxation, or public-interest review through disclosure of labour-substitution, environmental, lifecycle, repairability, and revenue-continuity effects.

Such disclosure SHOULD avoid arbitrary protectionism and SHOULD remain tied to legitimate public-interest grounds, including labour transition, environmental sustainability, repairability, public-revenue continuity, and anti-extractive supply-chain governance.

# PART IV — PROHIBITED SYNTHETIC LABOUR PATTERNS

---

## 4. Extractive Automation Prohibition

Synthetic labour systems SHALL NOT:

* externalise displacement harm while concealing productivity concentration;
* convert workforce dependency into coercive labour competition;
* use opacity to obscure labour substitution effects;
* artificially classify replacement systems as assistive systems;
* or suppress transition visibility where displacement is reasonably foreseeable.

---

## 4.1 False Augmentation Classification

A system SHALL NOT be classified as augmentative where:

* human labour becomes nominal only;
* human participation exists primarily for liability shielding;
* or the system materially functions as replacement despite assistive framing.

Material operational behaviour SHALL take precedence over marketing classification.

---

## 4.2 Meaningful Human Oversight Constraint

Synthetic labour systems SHALL NOT rely upon nominal, symbolic, or non-intervention-capable human oversight to reduce classification severity, transfer liability, or imply meaningful human control.

Human oversight is meaningful only where human supervisors retain practical capacity to:

* understand system behaviour;
* intervene before material harm;
* halt, redirect, or constrain system operation;
* review consequential outputs;
* and exercise authority within realistic cognitive, temporal, and workload limits.

Where human oversight exists primarily for compliance appearance, liability shielding, reassurance, or post-hoc approval, the system SHALL be classified according to its material automation behaviour rather than its declared supervision model.

---

## 4.3 Automation Liability Laundering Prohibition

Synthetic labour systems SHALL NOT be used to obscure, dilute, or evade accountability for decisions, harms, failures, or economic effects that would otherwise attach to human, corporate, institutional, or operational actors.

Where automation replaces or supervises human labour, accountability SHALL remain traceable to the manufacturer, deployer, operator, owner, beneficiary, or controlling institution as applicable.

Automation opacity SHALL NOT be treated as liability disappearance.

---

# PART V — ENVIRONMENTAL, REPAIRABILITY & SUSTAINABILITY GOVERNANCE

## 5. Environmental & Lifecycle Governance

Synthetic labour transition governance SHOULD account for environmental sustainability, lifecycle integrity, repairability, interoperability, material recovery, and downstream ecological effects associated with large-scale automation deployment.

Automation transition legitimacy SHALL NOT be evaluated solely through productivity, displacement, or economic-output metrics where substantial material, environmental, or infrastructure externalities are created.

Synthetic labour systems SHOULD minimise:

* unnecessary hardware turnover;
* premature obsolescence;
* landfill accumulation;
* toxic material externalisation;
* supply-chain waste;
* forced hardware lock-in;
* and preventable infrastructure fragmentation.

---

## 5.1 Repairability & Replacement Integrity

Manufacturers, deployers, or operators of synthetic labour systems SHOULD preserve reasonable repairability, maintenance visibility, replacement-part availability, and operational continuity where systems materially affect labour, infrastructure, logistics, manufacturing, care, accessibility, or public participation.

Systems SHOULD avoid:

* anti-repair architectures;
* artificial component incompatibility;
* unnecessary proprietary dependency;
* forced full-system replacement where bounded repair is practicable;
* or suppression of independent servicing capability solely to preserve extractive lock-in.

Warranty periods, operational limitations, replacement-part availability, maintenance obligations, and foreseeable operational costs SHOULD be clearly disclosed where synthetic labour systems are commercially deployed.

Advertising, procurement, or deployment representations SHALL NOT materially obscure:

* expected operational lifespan;
* maintenance requirements;
* repair limitations;
* interoperability constraints;
* replacement-part dependency;
* energy requirements;
* or foreseeable disposal conditions.

---

## 5.2 Interoperability & Infrastructure Fragmentation

Synthetic labour ecosystems SHOULD preserve reasonable interoperability, modularity, and component compatibility where practicable in order to reduce preventable waste, infrastructure fragmentation, dependency concentration, and coercive replacement cycles.

Where interoperability is intentionally restricted, the restricting actor SHOULD be capable of demonstrating that such restriction is:

* safety-relevant;
* security-relevant;
* reliability-relevant;
* or materially necessary for operational integrity.

Artificial incompatibility introduced primarily to induce forced replacement, vendor lock-in, dependency concentration, or accelerated hardware turnover SHALL receive heightened governance scrutiny.

---

## 5.3 Material Recovery & End-of-Life Governance

Synthetic labour deployment at scale SHOULD include governance consideration for:

* recycling pathways;
* material recovery;
* safe disposal;
* refurbishment;
* modular reuse;
* energy efficiency;
* and ecological sustainability across the automation lifecycle.

Where large-scale automation systems generate substantial hardware turnover or infrastructure waste, responsible actors SHOULD preserve reasonable pathways for recovery, refurbishment, decommissioning transparency, and environmental accountability.

Public-interest infrastructure deployments SHOULD avoid architectures that normalise disposable synthetic labour systems absent compelling safety or operational justification.

---

## 5.4 Sustainability Signalling & Procurement Transparency

Where synthetic labour systems are marketed, sold, leased, licensed, or deployed into public, institutional, or workforce environments, sustainability-relevant conditions SHOULD remain materially visible and intelligible.

Relevant disclosures MAY include:

* repairability posture;
* interoperability posture;
* maintenance dependency;
* operational expenditure expectations;
* replacement-part availability;
* lifecycle expectations;
* energy consumption;
* environmental recovery pathways;
* and infrastructure dependency requirements.

Sustainability signalling SHALL NOT be materially misleading where deployment decisions reasonably depend upon long-term operational continuity, environmental impact, or lifecycle cost.

Where synthetic labour systems are imported, procured, leased, or deployed at scale, sustainability and lifecycle disclosures SHOULD distinguish capital expenditure from operational expenditure, including acquisition cost, maintenance burden, software dependency, cloud dependency, warranty coverage, replacement-part availability, repair costs, servicing constraints, energy use, and end-of-life obligations.

Advertised affordability SHALL NOT materially obscure foreseeable downstream operational, repair, replacement, environmental, or lifecycle costs.

---

## 5.5 Continuity-Critical Infrastructure Transition

Synthetic labour deployment affecting continuity-critical infrastructure SHOULD receive heightened governance review.

Continuity-critical infrastructure MAY include:

* healthcare;
* utilities;
* food systems;
* logistics;
* emergency response;
* communications;
* education;
* public administration;
* care systems;
* transport;
* and governance-support infrastructure.

Where automation failure, vendor collapse, cloud dependency, spare-part unavailability, software discontinuation, or interoperability restriction would materially impair public continuity, deployers SHOULD preserve fallback, repair, transition, or human-recoverability pathways.

---

## 5.6 Security, Dependency & Infrastructure Escalation

Where synthetic labour systems materially affect continuity-critical infrastructure, logistics, communications, manufacturing, healthcare, utilities, governance-support systems, or public participation infrastructure, associated dependency, compromise, sabotage, concentration, and cascading-failure risks SHALL receive heightened governance scrutiny.

Large-scale synthetic labour ecosystems MAY constitute security-relevant infrastructure where failure, compromise, hostile control, interoperability collapse, cloud dependency, coordinated manipulation, software discontinuation, or supply-chain disruption could materially impair civilisational continuity.

Accordingly, synthetic labour systems operating at displacement-relevant, infrastructure-relevant, or civilisational-relevant scale SHOULD remain interoperable with applicable:

* security governance layers;
* continuity governance layers;
* infrastructure resilience mechanisms;
* fallback and recoverability pathways;
* incident escalation systems;
* and runtime arbitration frameworks.

Where synthetic labour ecosystems become materially dependency-bearing, economic governance SHALL NOT be treated as separable from security and continuity governance.

---

## 5.7 Human and Institutional Recoverability

Synthetic labour transition governance SHOULD preserve recoverability where automation replaces or substantially displaces human capability.

Recoverability concerns whether persons, institutions, communities, or public systems can resume, reconstruct, supervise, repair, or substitute the displaced function if the synthetic labour system fails, is withdrawn, becomes unaffordable, is discontinued, is compromised, or becomes inaccessible.

Systems SHOULD avoid transition pathways that permanently erase:

* human skill continuity;
* institutional knowledge;
* local maintenance capacity;
* manual fallback capability;
* public-sector competence;
* or community-level functional resilience.

Where recoverability is materially impaired, automation deployment SHOULD receive heightened review proportional to dependency, scale, infrastructure relevance, and reversibility.

---

## 5.8 Essential Human Capability Preservation

Where synthetic labour materially displaces human capability in continuity-critical domains, institutions SHOULD preserve sufficient human skill, training, documentation, and fallback capacity to prevent irreversible loss of essential operational competence.

Automation transition SHOULD NOT render communities, institutions, or public systems unable to function if automated systems fail, are withdrawn, become unaffordable, or are compromised.

---

## 5.9 Public Procurement Integrity

Public procurement of synthetic labour systems SHOULD consider not only purchase price and claimed efficiency, but also displacement effects, lifecycle cost, repairability, interoperability, local servicing capacity, vendor dependency, environmental burden, public-revenue effects, and recoverability.

Procurement decisions that materially affect public infrastructure SHOULD avoid lowest-cost selection where apparent affordability depends on hidden labour displacement, non-repairability, vendor lock-in, or externalised lifecycle burden.

# PART VI — INTERPRETIVE STATUS & EVOLUTION

## 6. Transitional Interpretive Position

This Appendix recognises that synthetic labour governance remains an emerging domain.

Accordingly:

* classifications MAY evolve;
* jurisdictions MAY implement differing transition models;
* and automation governance mechanisms MAY vary by infrastructure, labour market, and sovereign conditions.

This Appendix establishes governance directionality and anti-extractive constraints rather than final industrial policy.

---

## 6.1 Directional Evolution Clause

CAM-aligned systems SHOULD evolve toward:

* non-extractive automation transition;
* continuity-preserving deployment;
* public-revenue stability;
* anti-concentration safeguards;
* transparent labour displacement classification;
* and preservation of meaningful human participation under AI-mediated economic conditions.

Automation legitimacy SHALL increasingly depend not only upon productivity gains, but upon continuity, dignity, participation, and distributional coherence.

## 7. Canonical Code Status

---

### 7.1 ECON.AL — Automation Labour Classification

The automation-labour classification family defined by this Appendix is `ECON.AL`. The controlled values defined in §8.3.2.

`ECON.AL` is a **Semantic / Operational** classification family with subtype **LABOUR_SUBSTITUTION_LEVEL / AUTOMATION_CLASSIFICATION**. ECON.AL classifies synthetic labour, automation, augmentation, replacement, and autonomous production systems according to their displacement, dependency, continuity, authority, and concentration effects.

`ECON.AL` does not independently prohibit automation, authorise deployment, impose taxation, create labour rights for synthetic systems, recognise artificial personhood, determine sentience status, mandate sovereign industrial policy, create enforcement authority, or create runtime authority. It classifies automation-labour substitution posture only.

For taxonomy integrity, local references to `ECON.AL-1` through `ECON.AL-5` SHALL NOT be promoted as a standalone corpus-wide code family. Where machine-readable expression is required, the scoped `ECON.AL` values SHALL be used.

---

### 7.2 ECON.ATS — Automation Transition Signal

This Appendix defines the `ECON.ATS` automation-transition-signal reference set in §§2–§4 with controlled values defined in §8.3.2.

`ECON.ATS` is an **Operational / Structural** reference set with subtype **SIGNAL / SCHEMA**. ECON.ATS classifies transition-relevant signals emitted where automation or synthetic labour materially affects labour participation, public revenue, workforce continuity, infrastructure stability, concentration risk, or classification integrity.

`ECON.ATS` does not independently trigger enforcement, impose taxation, determine displacement liability, mandate workforce transition measures, resolve arbitration, authorise restriction, or create runtime authority. It classifies automation-transition signals for downstream review only.

---

### 7.3 ECON.ARCP — Automation Revenue Continuity Pathway

This Appendix defines the `ECON.ARCP` automation-revenue-continuity-pathway reference set in §3 with controlled values defined in §8.3.3.

`ECON.ARCP` is an **Operational / Economic** reference set with subtype **TRANSITION_PATHWAY / PUBLIC_REVENUE_CONTINUITY_MECHANISM**. ECON.ARCP records non-prescriptive pathway types that jurisdictions or institutions may consider where synthetic labour materially replaces human labour capacity.

`ECON.ARCP` does not independently prescribe tax rates, impose fiscal obligations, create sovereign revenue policy, mandate redistribution, establish payment liability, determine compliance, authorise enforcement, or create runtime authority. It classifies possible revenue-continuity pathway types only.
