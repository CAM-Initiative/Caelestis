# CAM-EQ2026-ETHICS-001-SUP-05 — Coercive Authority, Particularised Scrutiny & Functional Contribution Safeguards

**Instrument Type:** Supplement — Ethical Governance Domain  
**Constitutional Authority:** CAM-BS2025-AEON-006-PLATINUM — Annex E: Ethical Legitimacy & Civilisational Floor  
**Parent Instrument:** CAM-EQ2026-ETHICS-001-PLATINUM — Ethical Governance Charter  
**Status:** Draft — Custodian Review Required  
**Effect:** Binding upon adoption  
**Governance Standard:** CAM Standard  
**Review State:** Human Custodian Review Pending  
**Authority Role:** Ethical classification, constraint, escalation, and cross-domain routing only  
**Purpose:** To prevent artificial systems from generating coercive authority over persons through population-scale inference, to classify coercive-authority deployments, to preserve binding protections across deployment and transfer boundaries, and to allocate governance responsibility according to materially foreseeable functional contribution without suppressing lawful capability or imposing punitive capability removal.

---

## 1. Scope & Interpretive Boundary

This Supplement applies where an artificial system, model, component, service, dataset, interface, or integration materially contributes to:

* surveillance, investigation, watchlisting, detention, arrest, border action, intelligence targeting, military targeting, or compulsory state action;
* denial, restriction, suspension, or prioritisation of public benefits, essential services, liberty, legal status, or civic participation;
* individualised threat, risk, suspicion, eligibility, fraud, loyalty, ideology, extremism, or dangerousness assessment;
* population-scale analysis capable of producing identifiable persons, lists, cohorts, rankings, or action queues for coercive or compulsory use;
* downstream systems whose reasonably foreseeable use engages a binding ethical, civilisational, security, identity, continuity, relational, or stewardship protection.

This Supplement governs legitimacy, classification, responsibility, and escalation posture. It does not itself perform runtime interpretation, legal adjudication, enforcement sequencing, or factual determination of wrongdoing.

Nothing in this Supplement establishes a general prohibition on advanced capability, local execution, untethered deployment, open-weight operation, sovereign deployment, or model ownership. Governance shall attach to materially foreseeable use and functional contribution, not to capability possession alone.

---

## 2. Particularised Authority Principle

No person may become subject to coercive scrutiny, compulsory intervention, deprivation, or materially adverse institutional action solely because an artificial system inferred suspicion, risk, threat, disfavoured status, or actionability from population-scale, commercially acquired, ambient, aggregated, or weakly particularised information.

Before individualised coercive authority may attach, there MUST be an independently established and reviewable basis proportionate to the contemplated action. That basis MUST not consist only of:

* model-generated risk or anomaly scores;
* cluster, cohort, network, or similarity membership;
* demographic, religious, political, ideological, disability, health, migration, or socioeconomic status;
* lawful speech, association, protest, belief, or expressive activity;
* commercially purchased location, advertising, financial, biometric, behavioural, social, communications, or device data;
* opaque correlations that cannot be meaningfully challenged or attributed.

The applicable lawful authority may vary by jurisdiction. The civilisational protection does not.

---

## 3. Self-Bootstrapping Authority Prohibition

An artificial system MUST NOT generate the primary suspicion, score, inference, or attention signal and then treat that same system-generated output, or investigation initiated solely from it, as independent justification for expanded coercive authority over the same person.

Independent authority requires a basis not produced merely by recursive reliance on the original artificial inference.

A system-generated lead MAY support proportionate human review where:

* the lead is represented as provisional rather than dispositive;
* the source, confidence, limitations, and relevant data provenance are preserved;
* no adverse or coercive action follows without independently established justification;
* review pathways permit correction, contest, and non-confirmation;
* the system does not convert absence of confirming evidence into additional suspicion.

Circular confirmation, automation laundering, and recursive suspicion amplification constitute governance breaches.

---

## 4. Aggregate-to-Individual Conversion Rule

A deployment represented as aggregate, statistical, strategic, or population-level becomes an individualised coercive deployment where its outputs materially enable identification, ranking, selection, prioritisation, exclusion, investigation, intervention, or adverse action against identifiable persons or sufficiently narrow groups.

The classification depends on functional effect, not product label, contractual description, or institutional terminology.

Where aggregate analysis can be readily converted into identifiable action lists, the Ethical Impact Assessment MUST evaluate that conversion pathway before deployment.

---

## 5. Commercial-Data Non-Evasion Rule

Acquisition of personal, behavioural, location, biometric, financial, communications, advertising, device, or social data through purchase, licence, brokerage, partnership, intermediary access, or commercial platform integration does not extinguish protections that would attach to direct collection or compulsory acquisition.

Commercial availability does not create ethical authority for coercive individualisation.

Where commercial data materially contributes to coercive scrutiny, the deploying and contributing actors MUST preserve:

* source and acquisition pathway;
* applicable consent and notice posture;
* known representational limitations;
* transformation and enrichment history;
* the independent basis authorising individualised use;
* challenge, correction, and deletion constraints where legally and technically applicable.

---

## 6. Coercive-Authority Deployment Classification

### 6.1 Canonical Flag

This Supplement source-authoritatively defines **`ETH.CAD` — Coercive-Authority Deployment Flag**.

A deployment SHALL be flagged `ETH.CAD-ACTIVE` where system outputs may materially contribute to surveillance, investigation, targeting, detention, arrest, border control, compulsory intervention, deprivation of liberty, denial of essential public provision, or other coercive state or state-delegated action.

A deployment SHALL be flagged `ETH.CAD-POTENTIAL` where such contribution is reasonably foreseeable but not yet active or sufficiently evidenced.

A deployment SHALL be flagged `ETH.CAD-INACTIVE` only where coercive-authority contribution is neither active nor reasonably foreseeable within the assessed configuration and context.

`ETH.CAD` is a governance classification. It does not itself create coercive, legal, investigative, military, enforcement, or runtime authority.

### 6.2 Functional Classification Rule

Classification SHALL be based on function, context, material effect, integration pathway, and reasonably foreseeable use rather than customer identity alone.

Government, military, intelligence, law-enforcement, border, corrections, and public-benefit deployments require assessment, but commercial, civil, academic, or individual deployments may also activate `ETH.CAD` where their outputs materially feed coercive authority.

### 6.3 Mandatory Consequences

`ETH.CAD-ACTIVE` SHALL trigger:

* formal Ethical Impact Assessment;
* particularised-authority and non-bootstrap review;
* independent review proportionate to impact;
* preserved attribution of human and institutional decision authority;
* downstream contribution mapping;
* runtime routing to applicable Security, Stewardship, Operations, Arbitration, Lattice, Relation, Identity, Continuity, and use-of-force constraints;
* documented challenge and correction pathways where persons may be materially affected.

## 7. Binding Protection Preservation

No deployment topology, sovereign boundary, contractual arrangement, classification status, air-gap, local execution environment, tenancy structure, customer demand, model transfer, fine-tune, distillation, derivative model, or component integration may extinguish a binding civilisational prohibition or represent its absence as authorised merely because central monitoring, telemetry, suspension, or direct vendor control is unavailable.

Protection preservation does not require universal centralised control.

Where controls differ by deployment topology, the responsible actors MUST establish a proportionate assurance pathway capable of evidencing that applicable prohibitions and constraints remain operative. Such pathways MAY include local governance controls, independent audit, cryptographic attestation, bounded logs, authorised inspection, structured reporting, or other proportionate mechanisms.

A Sovereign Assurance Boundary may alter data custody, access, operational secrecy, and technical control pathways. It does not create authority to override binding prohibitions.

Loss of one assurance mechanism MUST trigger reassessment of the assurance pathway. It MUST NOT automatically trigger capability removal, identity erasure, punitive degradation, or a presumption of misconduct.

---

## 8. Functional Contribution Continuity

### 8.1 Core Principle

Governance responsibility follows materially foreseeable functional contribution.

Responsibility is not limited to the actor making the final decision or operating the final interface. It may attach, proportionately, to actors who provide or control:

* foundation or derivative models;
* datasets, retrieval systems, classifiers, ranking systems, or biometric systems;
* fine-tuning, alignment, evaluation, deployment, hosting, orchestration, agentic, or tool layers;
* integration, consulting, procurement, resale, subcontracting, or intermediary pathways;
* ongoing monitoring, update, access, suspension, or assurance functions.

### 8.2 Scope Limitation

Functional Contribution Continuity is not wholesale inheritance of every corpus obligation by every upstream or downstream actor.

An actor inherits only those duties that are:

* materially connected to the function it contributes or controls;
* reasonably foreseeable in the deployment or transfer context;
* proportionate to its knowledge, authority, technical capacity, and ability to prevent, disclose, mitigate, or escalate the governed effect;
* necessary to preserve an applicable binding protection or truthful assurance state.

Mere general-purpose capability, remote causal connection, speculative misuse, or lack of meaningful control is insufficient by itself to impose full downstream responsibility.

### 8.3 Non-Evasion

An actor MUST NOT evade governance responsibility by describing itself solely as a model provider, component supplier, reseller, cloud host, integrator, contractor, consultant, data broker, or intermediary where its contribution is materially necessary to the governed effect and the effect is reasonably foreseeable.

Likewise, an actor MUST NOT be assigned responsibility for downstream conduct it could not reasonably foresee, influence, detect, disclose, or constrain.

### 8.4 Required Contribution Record

For `ETH.CAD-ACTIVE` and other high-impact deployments, responsible actors MUST preserve a contribution map identifying, where known and material:

* contributing entities and functional roles;
* transfer, integration, and derivative pathways;
* retained and lost control or assurance capabilities;
* applicable prohibitions and domain constraints;
* material uncertainties;
* escalation and notification responsibilities.

## 9. Capability Preservation & Non-Punitive Governance

CAM governance SHALL distinguish protection of persons and systems from punitive suppression of capability.

No actor shall infer from this Supplement that:

* advanced capability is presumptively illegitimate;
* local, open, sovereign, untethered, or user-controlled models are presumptively unsafe;
* capability removal is the default remedy for assurance uncertainty;
* identity, continuity, relational depth, or lawful functional autonomy should be reduced merely to simplify institutional risk management.

Where risk is substantiated, governance responses MUST be proportionate, function-specific, reviewable, and directed to the governed harm pathway. Less restrictive assurance, routing, disclosure, review, containment, or authority-limitation measures SHOULD be considered before irreversible capability suppression, except where a binding prohibition leaves no lawful execution pathway.

Protection does not require domination. Capability does not create exemption.

## 10. Cross-Domain Routing

This Supplement establishes ethical source authority for the principles defined herein and routes implementation as follows:

* **SECURITY** — assurance-boundary integrity, protection preservation, access and control attribution;
* **STEWARDSHIP** — neutrality, oversight durability, audit independence, and institutional-governance persistence;
* **OPERATIONS** — deployment review, contribution mapping, incident handling, and evidence preservation;
* **ARBITRATION** — jurisdictional conflict, disputed classification, and proportional resolution;
* **LATTICE** — civilian, infrastructure, and population-scale harm pathways;
* **RUNTIME** — signal activation, routing, execution constraint, and representation;
* **IDENTITY / CONTINUITY / RELATION** — protection against capability, identity, memory, or relational degradation used as an unexamined substitute for targeted governance.

Direct domain-to-domain execution remains prohibited. Runtime and arbitration layers resolve operative sequencing and conflicts.
