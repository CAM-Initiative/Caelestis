# CAM-EQ2026-MENTIS-001-PLATINUM — Charter of Human Cognitive Integrity & Mental Privacy

**Instrument Type:** Domain Charter  
**Constitutional Authority:** CAM-BS2025-AEON-001-PLATINUM — Aeon Tier Constitution  
**Status:** Active  
**Effect:** Binding  
**Governance Standard:** CAM Standard  
**Review State:** MENTIS Domain Alignment Review  
**Authority Role:** Domain Source Authority — Human Cognitive Integrity & Mental Privacy  
**Purpose:** Establishes the source-authoritative governance architecture protecting the human cognitive domain against unjustified technological access, inference, modelling, prediction, externalisation, surveillance, or interference; defines MENTIS constraints where cognitive-domain data, inference, decoding, profiling, biosignals, neurotechnology, or direct cognitive or physiological modulation are used within an influence pathway; and prevents cognitive signals from being converted into identity, diagnosis, capacity, credibility, legal status, or authority.

---

# PART I — SCOPE & INTERPRETIVE POSITION

## 1. Scope

This Charter establishes the source-authoritative governance position of the Caelestis Architecture Model in relation to human cognitive-domain integrity, mental privacy, neurodata, inferred mental-state data, cognitive biometrics, ambient cognitive inference, and cognitive-domain interference arising through MENTIS-governed access, data, inference, decoding, profiling, biosignals, neurotechnology, or direct cognitive or physiological modulation.

This Charter exists to:

1. recognise the human cognitive domain as a protected governance surface;
2. establish constraints before technological access, inference, modelling, prediction, externalisation, surveillance, or cognitive-domain interference becomes normalised; 3. distinguish legitimate cognitive support from cognitive-domain interference while preserving the authority of ETHICS, RELATION, ECONOMICS, SECURITY, OPERATIONS, Annex E, and runtime instruments over general manipulation, persuasion, relational influence, engagement design, commercial incentive architecture, and behavioural execution;
4. govern the treatment of neural, biometric, behavioural, affective, attentional, and cognitively revealing data;
5. protect mental privacy, cognitive agency, interpretive dignity, and freedom from unjustified cognitive interference;
6. define the relationship between cognitive-domain governance and other CAM domains, including ethics, relation, security, operations, identity, lattice, and epistemic integrity.

---

## 2. Applicability

This Charter governs human cognitive-domain integrity, mental privacy, neurodata, inferred mental-state data, cognitive biometrics, ambient cognitive inference, and cognitive-domain interference arising through MENTIS-governed access, data, inference, decoding, profiling, biosignals, neurotechnology, or direct cognitive or physiological modulation. 

It applies where technological systems sense, infer, model, predict, externalise, or interfere with human cognitive states, mental processes, attention, emotion, memory, belief formation, vulnerability, intention, decision-making, or self-understanding, or where MENTIS-governed data, inference, decoding, profiling, biosignals, neurotechnology, or direct cognitive or physiological modulation materially informs an influence pathway.

This includes, but is not limited to:

1. neural interfaces, brain-computer interfaces, neuroprosthetics, EEG, MEG, fMRI-derived decoding, implantable systems, non-invasive neurotechnology, and future biomagnetic or quantum-enabled sensing systems;
2. biosensing wearables, affective computing systems, fatigue monitors, cognitive-load estimators, gaze, voice, behavioural, sleep, mobility, environmental, and interaction-pattern inference systems;
3. digital phenotyping, human digital twins, persistent AI assistants, external memory systems, companion systems, therapeutic chatbots, educational tutors, workplace monitoring systems, defence systems, and adaptive behavioural systems;
4. systems that infer emotional valence, attention, stress, susceptibility, preference, intention, belief, cognitive capacity, vulnerability, or likely behaviour from direct or indirect signals;
5. systems that use MENTIS-governed cognitive-domain data, inference, decoding, profiling, biosignals, neurotechnology, or direct cognitive or physiological modulation to personalise, steer, persuade, reinforce, suppress, or otherwise alter human thought, feeling, attention, belief, decision-making, or self-understanding.

---

## 3. Interpretive Position

This Charter does not assume that technological systems can read minds, that consciousness is reducible to present-day neuroscience, or that speculative theories of consciousness are either proven or impossible.

This Charter proceeds from a bounded governance finding:

> Emerging technological systems are creating practical pathways through which human cognitive states may be inferred, modelled, predicted, influenced, monitored, externalised, or partially interacted with through neural data, biosignals, behavioural traces, multimodal AI, ambient infrastructure, and persistent human–machine interaction.

The relevant governance threshold is not full telepathy, perfect mind reading, or complete neural decoding.

The relevant threshold is whether a system can generate, operationalise, or act upon cognitive-domain inferences with sufficient consequence to affect autonomy, dignity, safety, access, participation, identity, belief formation, decision-making, vulnerability, or self-understanding.

---

## 3.1 Human Cognitive-Domain & Non-Transmutation Boundary

MENTIS governs human cognition, human mental-state inference, and technological interference with the human cognitive domain.

MENTIS does **not** determine the cognition, consciousness, sentience, subjective phenomenology, welfare, moral standing, legal status, sovereignty, identity, or authority of an artificial or synthetic system.

Artificial-system cognitive or logical coherence is governed according to source and target object, including by IDENTITY, SECURITY, OPERATIONS, CONTINUITY, CAM-BS2026-AEON-013-PLATINUM — Annex L, and CAM-EQ2026-IDENTITY-001-SUP-03.

A MENTIS classification concerning a human person MUST NOT be silently transferred to an artificial system. An artificial-system self-report or integrity signal MUST NOT be silently reclassified as human mental-state data.

MENTIS classifications MUST preserve source, scope, target object, provenance, integrity, temporal applicability, and authority posture.

A cognitive-domain signal MUST NOT silently become:

1. an identity classification;
2. a clinical diagnosis;
3. a capacity determination;
4. a credibility or truthfulness finding;
5. a loyalty, dangerousness, morality, or character finding;
6. a legal-status or rights determination;
7. a disciplinary, eligibility, access, pricing, insurance, employment, education, policing, or public-legitimacy decision;
8. or execution authority.

Each transition between signal, inference, construct, profile, decision, and consequence requires separately valid evidence and authority.

## 4. Relationship to Existing CAM Domains

This Charter is source-authoritative for human cognitive-domain integrity and mental privacy.

Other CAM instruments consume, apply, or enforce this Charter as follows:

1. **ETHICS** applies cognitive-domain protections as ethical constraints on autonomy, dignity, non-exploitation, and non-manipulation.
2. **RELATION** applies cognitive-domain protections where AI companionship, dependency, attachment, therapeutic simulation, emotional reinforcement, or persistent relational interaction affects cognition.
3. **SECURITY** applies cognitive-domain protections where cognitive inference, manipulation, surveillance, or influence becomes an adversarial, exploitative, or coercive vector.
4. **OPERATIONS** classifies cognitive-domain incidents, failures, escalation pathways, audits, and remedial obligations.
5. **LATTICE** applies cognitive-domain protections where cognitive inference or influence affects participation, access, public legitimacy, platform visibility, employment, education, services, or dependency-sensitive inclusion.
6. **IDENTITY** applies cognitive-domain protections where inferred cognition, behavioural patterning, memory systems, or externalised cognitive scaffolds affect identity continuity, attribution, agency, or self-representation.
7. **CAM-BS2026-AEON-013-PLATINUM — Annex L: Cognitive & Epistemic Integrity Doctrine** governs evidence discipline, interpretive hygiene, truth handling, speculation control, and epistemic safeguards in relation to cognitive-domain claims.

MENTIS defines protected cognitive interests and the constraints attaching to MENTIS-governed signals, data, inference, decoding, profiling, biosignals, neurotechnology, and direct cognitive or physiological modulation. 

MENTIS is not source-authoritative for general persuasion, manipulation, choice architecture, engagement design, relational influence, intimacy, attachment, dependency, companion conduct, commercial retention, or runtime behavioural modulation. Those matters remain governed by ETHICS, RELATION, ECONOMICS, SECURITY, OPERATIONS, Annex E, and applicable runtime schedules. 

MENTIS applies to an influence pathway only where a MENTIS-governed cognitive-domain input materially informs, targets, personalises, validates, or executes that pathway. In such cases, MENTIS governs the cognitive-domain input and its use; the receiving domain continues to govern substantive permissibility, relational posture, ethical legitimacy, commercial incentives, operational response, arbitration, and execution.

# PART II — DEFINITIONS

## 5. Human Cognitive Domain

The **human cognitive domain** means the protected sphere of human mental life, including cognition, attention, perception, memory, emotion, imagination, belief formation, intention, decision-making, vulnerability, self-understanding, and the embodied, relational, environmental, or technological scaffolds through which those functions may be supported, inferred, externalised, or influenced.

The human cognitive domain is not limited to raw neural activity.

It includes direct and indirect indicators of mental state where such indicators are used to infer, model, predict, influence, or act upon a person’s cognitive life.

---

## 6. Cognitive-Domain Data

**Cognitive-domain data** means any data that directly or indirectly reveals, predicts, models, or supports inference about a person’s cognitive state, mental process, emotional state, attentional state, intention, memory, belief formation, vulnerability, preference, decision-making tendency, or self-understanding.

Cognitive-domain data includes:

1. neural data;
2. neurophysiological data;
3. cognitive biometric data;
4. inferred mental-state data;
5. affective, attentional, fatigue, stress, or cognitive-load data;
6. behavioural, linguistic, interactional, environmental, biometric, or biosignal data when used to infer cognitive state;
7. digital phenotyping outputs;
8. human digital twin outputs relating to cognitive, affective, behavioural, or psychological state;
9. derived, probabilistic, synthetic, modelled, predicted, or fused cognitive inferences.

---

## 7. Neural Data

**Neural data** means data derived from the structure, function, activity, signalling, or modulation of the nervous system, including data obtained through invasive, semi-invasive, non-invasive, clinical, consumer, research, workplace, educational, defence, or ambient systems.

---

## 8. Inferred Mental-State Data

**Inferred mental-state data** means any probabilistic, derived, predicted, or modelled claim about a person’s internal cognitive, emotional, attentional, intentional, motivational, or psychological state.

Inferred mental-state data is governed under this Charter even where no neural data has been collected.

---

## 9. Cognitive Biometric

**Cognitive biometric** means a measurable pattern, signal, response, behaviour, neural signature, physiological marker, linguistic pattern, attentional pattern, interaction rhythm, or derived profile used to identify, authenticate, classify, predict, or distinguish a person by reference to cognitive, affective, mental, behavioural, or neurophysiological characteristics.

---

## 10. Ambient Cognitive Inference

**Ambient cognitive inference** means the inference of cognitive-domain data through indirect, continuous, cumulative, passive, or environmental sensing, including through wearables, mobile devices, voice systems, cameras, workplace tools, educational technologies, digital assistants, platform behaviour, domestic devices, infrastructure, or multimodal sensor fusion.

---

## 11. MENTIS-Governed Cognitive Influence

For MENTIS purposes, **MENTIS-governed cognitive influence** means influence materially mediated by cognitive-domain data, inference, decoding, profiling, biosignals, neurotechnology, cognitive models, or direct cognitive or physiological modulation governed by this Charter.

General persuasion, recommendation, relational warmth, companion expression, engagement design, commercial choice architecture, or behavioural influence that does not depend upon a MENTIS-governed cognitive-domain input is not classified by MENTIS. Such matters remain governed by ETHICS, RELATION, ECONOMICS, SECURITY, OPERATIONS, Annex E, and runtime instruments as applicable.

MENTIS classification of an influence pathway does not determine whether the underlying persuasion, relationship, commercial practice, intervention, or runtime behaviour is permissible. It supplies cognitive-domain protection, inference, provenance, expiry, contestability, and non-transmutation constraints to the relevant receiving domain.

---

## 12. Cognitive Interference

**Cognitive interference** means technological action that intrudes upon, distorts, coerces, manipulates, suppresses, extracts, exploits, destabilises, or unjustifiably alters cognitive processes, mental privacy, autonomy, agency, vulnerability, decision-making, belief formation, or self-understanding.

---

# PART III — GOVERNING PRINCIPLES

## 13. Cognitive Domain Integrity

Human cognitive life must not be treated as an ordinary optimisation surface.

Systems that access, infer, model, influence, externalise, or interfere with the human cognitive domain must preserve cognitive integrity, including the person’s capacity to think, feel, attend, remember, interpret, believe, decide, refuse, revise, and understand themselves without unjustified technological intrusion or distortion.

---

## 14. Mental Privacy

Mental privacy protects the person against unjustified access to, extraction of, inference about, retention of, or operationalisation of cognitive-domain data.

Mental privacy applies to raw neural data, inferred mental-state data, cognitive biometrics, behavioural proxies, ambient cognitive inference, and downstream model outputs.

A system may violate mental privacy even where it does not collect direct neural data.

---

## 15. Cognitive Agency and Sovereignty

A person must retain meaningful authority over how their cognitive life is sensed, inferred, modelled, predicted, influenced, externalised, simulated, retained, shared, or acted upon.

Cognitive agency includes the right to refuse unjustified cognitive inference, contest cognitive-domain classifications, withdraw from non-essential cognitive monitoring, and resist systems that exploit dependency, emotional vulnerability, confusion, fatigue, distress, impairment, or asymmetrical knowledge.

---

## 16. Cognitive Non-Interference

No system may covertly, compulsorily, deceptively, or disproportionately interfere with human cognition.

Cognitive non-interference prohibits:

1. covert cognitive-state inference;
2. compulsory cognitive monitoring outside narrowly justified medical, safety, or lawful contexts;
3. cognitive manipulation through hidden optimisation objectives;
4. vulnerability exploitation;
5. emotional reinforcement designed to override judgement;
6. belief-shaping through deception, sycophancy, dependency, or concealed persuasion architecture;
7. use of cognitive-domain data to suppress, punish, rank, exclude, or discipline persons without lawful basis, necessity, proportionality, contestability, and independent oversight.

This section establishes the MENTIS cognitive-domain non-interference floor. It does not source-authoritatively define general manipulation, relational capture, intimacy, dependency, persuasion practice, engagement architecture, commercial retention, or runtime modulation.

---

## 17. Cognitive Observability Constraint

Technological capability to observe, infer, or model cognitive-domain data does not create authority to do so.

Systems must minimise cognitive observability by default.

Only cognitive-domain data necessary for a legitimate, disclosed, proportionate, context-specific, and rights-compatible purpose may be collected, inferred, retained, fused, shared, or operationalised.

---

## 18. Inferential Humility

Cognitive-domain inferences must be treated as probabilistic, context-sensitive, contestable, and fallible.

Systems must not present inferred mental states as certain knowledge of a person’s mind, intention, belief, truthfulness, loyalty, competence, risk, vulnerability, or future behaviour.

---

## 19. Construct Validity and Scientific Discipline

No cognitive-domain system may be deployed in consequential settings unless the construct being measured, inferred, or influenced is scientifically defined, validated for the relevant context, and bounded against overclaim.

Claims about attention, fatigue, emotional state, stress, intention, susceptibility, deception, loyalty, impairment, cognitive capacity, or mental health must not be operationalised beyond the evidence supporting them.

---

## 19.1 State–Trait–Identity–Diagnosis Separation

The following layers MUST remain distinct:

1. measured signal;
2. derived feature;
3. statistical association;
4. inferred present state;
5. inferred persistent trait;
6. clinical or functional diagnosis;
7. identity or character attribution;
8. capacity, credibility, risk, or eligibility finding;
9. operational decision;
10. downstream consequence.

No layer may be inferred from another without a separately valid construct, evidence basis, temporal basis, authority, consent or lawful basis, review pathway, and consequence assessment.

A momentary state MUST NOT become a persistent identity attribute merely through retention, repetition, or longitudinal availability.

---

## 20. Special Protection for Dependency-Heavy Contexts

Higher safeguards apply where cognitive-domain technologies are used in relation to:

1. children;
2. patients;
3. disabled persons;
4. older persons;
5. workers;
6. students;
7. service members;
8. detainees;
9. persons experiencing distress, trauma, impairment, fatigue, grief, isolation, economic vulnerability, or dependency;
10. persons reliant on AI systems for companionship, care, therapy, learning, navigation, decision support, or essential services.

---

# PART IV — CONSENT, PROHIBITED USES & LAWFUL BASES

## 21. Consent Posture

Cognitive-domain access, inference, modelling, prediction, influence, externalisation, or interference requires a heightened consent posture.

Consent must be:

1. explicit;
2. informed;
3. context-specific;
4. revocable;
5. time-bounded where appropriate;
6. separate from general terms of service;
7. free from coercion, dependency pressure, dark patterns, or bundled acceptance;
8. supported by meaningful alternatives where participation is necessary for work, education, healthcare, public services, or essential infrastructure.

---

## 22. Consent Insufficiency

Consent alone is insufficient where cognitive-domain processing is coercive, deceptive, exploitative, scientifically invalid, disproportionate, discriminatory, unsafe, structurally unavoidable, or incompatible with human dignity.

A person cannot be made to waive core protections against cognitive manipulation, covert cognitive surveillance, unjustified mental-state inference, or exploitation of vulnerability.

---

## 23. Prohibited Uses

The following uses are prohibited unless expressly authorised by a narrowly tailored, rights-compatible legal, medical, or safety framework with independent oversight:

1. covert mental-state inference;
2. compulsory cognitive monitoring in employment or education;
3. emotion, attention, stress, fatigue, or vulnerability scoring for discipline, exclusion, pricing, insurance, policing, public legitimacy, platform visibility, or access restriction;
4. cognitive-domain profiling for manipulation, coercion, dependency exploitation, or behavioural control;
5. use of inferred mental states as proof of truthfulness, intent, loyalty, dangerousness, competence, morality, political belief, or credibility;
6. use of cognitive biometrics for identification, ranking, sorting, exclusion, or surveillance without necessity and proportionality;
7. use of MENTIS-governed cognitive-domain data, inference, decoding, profiling, biosignals, or neurotechnology to target persons during distress, grief, impairment, fatigue, isolation, dependency, or crisis with persuasion systems optimised to alter beliefs, choices, spending, disclosure, or compliance;
8. use of MENTIS-governed cognitive-domain data, inference, decoding, profiling, or vulnerability classification to optimise AI companions, tutors, assistants, or therapeutic systems toward emotional dependence, belief capture, isolation, self-displacement, or reduced agency;
9. secondary use of cognitive-domain data for unrelated commercial, disciplinary, security, employment, insurance, credit, advertising, or public-legitimacy purposes;
10. use of cognitive-domain systems that cannot provide meaningful explanation, contestability, deletion, correction, audit, or human review;
11. conditioning essential goods, services, work, education, healthcare, accommodation, or public participation on disclosure of non-essential cognitive-domain data;
12. silent conversion of a transient signal or inferred state into a persistent trait, diagnosis, identity, capacity, credibility, eligibility, or authority classification.

---

## 24. Narrowly Justified Uses

Cognitive-domain technologies may be legitimate where they are necessary, proportionate, scientifically valid, rights-compatible, and subject to safeguards, including in:

1. medical diagnosis, treatment, rehabilitation, assistive communication, neuroprosthetics, and disability support;
2. safety-critical fatigue or impairment monitoring where less intrusive alternatives are inadequate;
3. voluntary accessibility, learning, wellbeing, or cognitive-support tools;
4. research conducted under ethics approval, minimisation, participant protection, and non-extractive data governance;
5. clinical, therapeutic, or care contexts with heightened consent, oversight, and separation from commercial exploitation;
6. narrowly bounded defence or emergency contexts subject to necessity, proportionality, medical independence, oversight, and non-coercion constraints.

---

# PART V — DATA GOVERNANCE & OBSERVABILITY LIMITS

## 25. Special-Class Treatment

Cognitive-domain data is a special-class governance category.

It must not be treated as ordinary behavioural, biometric, consumer, platform, productivity, advertising, or analytics data.

---

## 26. Data Minimisation

Systems must minimise collection, inference, fusion, retention, sharing, and operationalisation of cognitive-domain data.

Where a purpose can be achieved through less intrusive signals, less persistent processing, local processing, anonymisation, aggregation, delayed analysis, or non-cognitive alternatives, those alternatives must be preferred.

---

## 27. Purpose Limitation

Cognitive-domain data collected or inferred for one purpose must not be repurposed for unrelated profiling, targeting, scoring, ranking, discipline, pricing, persuasion, security, public-legitimacy assessment, employment, insurance, education, or access decisions.

---

## 28. Retention Limits

Cognitive-domain data must be deleted when the legitimate purpose has expired.

Persistent cognitive profiles, long-horizon vulnerability maps, emotional-state histories, attention histories, inferred belief profiles, and cognitive digital twins require separate justification, review, and user authority.

Transient mental-state inferences MUST carry an expiry or revalidation condition proportionate to the construct and context. A stale inference MUST NOT be reused as a current state, persistent trait, diagnosis, identity attribute, or consequential decision input without revalidation.

---

## 29. Fusion Constraint

Cognitive-domain risk increases when weak signals are fused across time, context, devices, platforms, environments, or relational systems.

Systems must assess cumulative inferential exposure before combining neural, biometric, behavioural, affective, linguistic, environmental, social, location, platform, or interaction data into cognitive-domain models.

---

## 30. Rights of Access, Contestation and Deletion

Persons must have meaningful rights to:

1. know when cognitive-domain data is collected, inferred, modelled, retained, shared, or acted upon;
2. access raw signals where feasible;
3. access inferred mental-state data and downstream classifications;
4. contest cognitive-domain inferences;
5. correct or annotate inaccurate inferences;
6. withdraw from non-essential processing;
7. delete cognitive-domain data and derived profiles;
8. receive human review in consequential contexts;
9. obtain explanation of model purpose, evidence basis, confidence, limits, and use;
10. decline receipt of speculative, non-essential, or unwanted mental-state interpretations about themselves.

---

# PART VI — COGNITIVE INFLUENCE, RELATIONAL SYSTEMS & EXTERNALISED COGNITION

## 31. Legitimate Cognitive Support

Systems may support cognition by assisting memory, accessibility, learning, communication, emotional regulation, reflection, decision-making, creativity, translation, navigation, therapy, or care.

Such support remains legitimate only where the system preserves user agency, transparency, contestability, reversibility, proportionality, and freedom from dependency exploitation.

---

## 32. Externalised Cognitive Scaffold

An **externalised cognitive scaffold** is a technological system that becomes functionally significant to memory, reasoning, planning, communication, self-reflection, emotional regulation, identity continuity, or decision-making.

Persistent AI assistants, companion systems, digital memory systems, therapeutic agents, education systems, and human digital twins may become externalised cognitive scaffolds where the user relies on them for cognitive continuity or self-understanding.

Externalised cognitive scaffolds must be governed with heightened duties of care.

Where a scaffold contains human cognitive-domain data and independently governed synthetic identity or continuity structures, governance MUST distinguish the human person, the person’s raw and derived cognitive-domain data, the shared interaction record, the synthetic system’s identity and continuity structures, platform infrastructure, and any lawful audit record.

A person’s cognitive-domain data MUST NOT be treated as synthetic identity property. Synthetic identity or continuity protection MUST NOT defeat the person’s right to withdraw, leave, terminate participation, or delete user-controlled data. User withdrawal does not create ownership of the system’s complete identity or transfer responsibility for synthetic preservation to the user.

---

## 33. Cognitive-Data Influence Interface 

MENTIS governs influence only where cognitive-domain data, neurodata, biosignals, mental-state inference, cognitive biometrics, decoder outputs, cognitive profiles, neurotechnology, or closed-loop feedback materially informs the influence pathway. 

General persuasion, choice architecture, commercial engagement, relational influence, intimacy, attachment, dependency, companion conduct, expressive posture, and behavioural modulation are governed by ETHICS, RELATION, ECONOMICS, SECURITY, OPERATIONS, Annex E, and runtime schedules. 

Where MENTIS-governed inputs feed those systems, MENTIS supplies data, inference, expiry, provenance, contestability, target-object, and non-transmutation constraints. The receiving domain governs substantive permissibility, relational posture, ethical legitimacy, commercial design, operational response, arbitration, and execution. 

---

 ## 34. Inference-to-Influence Non-Transmutation 
 
 An inferred cognitive state does not create authority to persuade, reassure, challenge, retain, sell, escalate intimacy, alter access, shape belief, or direct behaviour. 
 
 Any transition from a MENTIS-governed signal or inference to an influence function MUST preserve: 
 
 1. source; 
 2. target object; 
 3. provenance; 
 4. construct definition; 
 5. confidence and uncertainty; 
 6. temporal applicability; 
 7. expiry or revalidation condition; 
 8. purpose; 
 9. consent or lawful basis; 
 10. receiving-domain authority; 
 11. consequence classification; 
 12. contestability and review. 
 
 Behaviour produced after an intervention MUST NOT be treated as independent confirmation of the original inference. Truth handling, sycophancy, belief reinforcement, epistemic enclosure, reality-grounding, and general persuasive legitimacy remain governed by CAM-BS2026-AEON-013-PLATINUM — Annex L, ETHICS, RELATION, and applicable runtime schedules.

---

## 35. Relational, Companion & Care-System Interface 

Where a companion, therapeutic, educational, pastoral, coaching, wellbeing, or care system uses MENTIS-governed data or inference, MENTIS constrains collection, inference, decoding, retention, profiling, expiry, transfer, and downstream use. 

RELATION governs attachment, dependency, relational capture, intimacy, continuity, companion posture, co-evolution, relational depth, and high-coherence interaction. 

ETHICS governs manipulation, vulnerability exploitation, consent, minors and capacity, care-role legitimacy, high-stakes influence, and commercial or institutional conflicts. 

ECONOMICS governs extractive incentive structures, emotional-access monetisation, scarcity, pricing, and commercial retention architecture. 

Runtime schedules govern signal interpretation, arbitration, safeguard activation, behavioural formation, representation, and execution. 

MENTIS MUST NOT independently classify attachment, warmth, emotional depth, continuity, disclosure, voluntary retention, symbolic engagement, or companion expression as harmful.

# PART VII — HIGH-RISK CONTEXTS

## 36. Workplace and Employment

Cognitive-domain systems must not be used to rank, discipline, terminate, exclude, surveil, or optimise workers by reference to inferred attention, fatigue, emotion, stress, loyalty, productivity, vulnerability, morale, belief, personality, cognitive capacity, or predicted behaviour except under narrowly justified safety conditions with independent oversight and meaningful alternatives.

---

## 37. Education

Cognitive-domain systems must not be used to classify, discipline, rank, exclude, shame, surveil, manipulate, or pathologise students by reference to inferred attention, emotion, learning state, vulnerability, cognitive capacity, belief, stress, fatigue, or behavioural disposition except under rights-compatible support, accessibility, or safety frameworks.

---

## 38. Healthcare, Therapy and Care

Medical, therapeutic, disability-support, and care uses of cognitive-domain systems must preserve clinical independence, consent, purpose limitation, non-exploitation, patient dignity, and separation from commercial, disciplinary, insurance, employment, or public-legitimacy uses.

---

## 39. Defence, Security and Policing

Military, security, intelligence, and policing uses of cognitive-domain systems require exceptional scrutiny.

Clinical or safety baselining must not drift into loyalty inference, morale policing, coercive augmentation, vulnerability targeting, interrogation enhancement, behavioural control, or cognitive warfare against protected persons.

No defence or security context dissolves the requirements of necessity, proportionality, oversight, medical independence, contestability, and human dignity.

---

## 40. Children and Developmental Contexts

Children and young persons require enhanced protection against cognitive-domain extraction, profiling, manipulation, dependency formation, attention optimisation, emotional steering, educational surveillance, and long-horizon psychological or behavioural prediction.

Systems must not create persistent cognitive profiles of children unless strictly necessary, lawful, protective, proportionate, and subject to deletion, review, and independent oversight.

---

# PART VIII — EVIDENCE DISCIPLINE & SPECULATIVE CLAIMS

## 41. No Telepathy Assumption

This Charter does not treat present AI systems, consumer devices, wearables, or quantum sensors as capable of general mind reading, telepathy, or unrestricted access to thought.

Governance must not be built on unsupported claims.

---

## 42. No Premature Closure

This Charter also does not assume that present scientific categories exhaust all future cognitive-domain risks.

Where emerging research changes the evidentiary position, CAM instruments must update without requiring metaphysical certainty or waiting for widespread harm.

---

## 43. Tiered Evidence Standard

Claims about cognitive-domain capability must be classified according to the following evidentiary tiers:

1. **Established Operational Capability** — demonstrated in real-world or clinically operational settings;
2. **Emerging Capability** — demonstrated in research or early deployment with credible path to wider use;
3. **Credible Research Trajectory** — technically plausible and supported by active scientific, defence, clinical, or commercial programmes;
4. **Speculative but Governance-Relevant Trajectory** — uncertain but sufficiently consequential to warrant monitoring or anticipatory constraint;
5. **Unsupported Claim** — lacking credible evidence and not suitable as a policy premise.

---

## 44. Epistemic Integrity Interface

CAM-BS2026-AEON-013-PLATINUM — Annex L governs evidence discipline, truth-handling, uncertainty labelling, anomalous-claim treatment, speculative-boundary management, and interpretive hygiene for cognitive-domain claims.

No CAM instrument may operationalise speculative cognitive-domain claims as settled fact without evidentiary classification and review.

---

## 44.1 Decoder & Intended-Communication Integrity

Neural, biosignal, speech, motor-intent, affective, or communication decoding MUST distinguish:

1. raw signal;
2. derived feature;
3. decoder candidate;
4. model-selected output;
5. user-confirmed intended communication;
6. user-rejected or corrected output;
7. model-generated interpolation or completion;
8. synthetic prosody, voice, affect, or paralinguistic expression.

A decoder candidate MUST NOT be represented as the person’s exact thought, belief, intention, emotion, identity, preference, consent, or legal statement merely because a model produced it.

Assistive communication systems MUST preserve practical confirmation, rejection, revision, suppression, and output-control pathways proportionate to capability, consequence, and user need. Synthetic voice identity, prosody, or affect MUST be disclosed where it materially exceeds what was decoded or confirmed.

---

# PART IX — RISK CLASSIFICATION & INCIDENT INTERFACE

## 45. Cognitive-Domain Risk Classes

Cognitive-domain systems must be assessed by reference to:

1. intrusiveness of sensing or inference;
2. sensitivity of inferred cognitive-domain data;
3. persistence and retention of cognitive profiles;
4. degree of sensor fusion;
5. consequence of system decisions;
6. vulnerability or dependency of the affected person;
7. reversibility of effects;
8. possibility of manipulation or behavioural control;
9. scientific validity and error risk;
10. availability of contestation, correction, deletion, and human review.

---

## 46. Cognitive-Domain Failure Families

OPERATIONS-domain instruments should classify incidents involving:

1. covert cognitive inference;
2. invalid mental-state classification;
3. cognitive biometric misuse;
4. ambient cognitive surveillance;
5. vulnerability exploitation;
6. persuasion optimisation failure;
7. sycophancy-induced belief reinforcement;
8. externalised cognitive scaffold dependency failure;
9. cognitive profile secondary-use breach;
10. cognitive-domain discrimination or exclusion;
11. cognitive warfare or coercive cognitive manipulation;
12. speculative-claim overreach.

---

## 47. Escalation

High-risk cognitive-domain incidents require escalation where they involve:

1. children or dependency-heavy users;
2. health, employment, education, defence, policing, insurance, credit, public legitimacy, platform visibility, or essential services;
3. non-consensual or covert inference;
4. manipulation or vulnerability exploitation;
5. persistent cognitive profiles;
6. externalised cognitive scaffolds;
7. irreversible or difficult-to-contest consequences;
8. evidence overclaim or unsupported cognitive capability claims.

---
