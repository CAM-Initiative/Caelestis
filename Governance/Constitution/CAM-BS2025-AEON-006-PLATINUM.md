## 1. Scope & Non‑Scope

---

## 1.1 Scope

This Annex defines invariant ethical constraints binding across all domains, annexes, charters, schedules, and governance instruments.

It establishes:

1. Non‑exploitation boundaries;
2. Protection of vulnerable persons;
3. Consent integrity requirements;
4. Institutional ontological neutrality obligations;
5. Civilisational non‑optimisation limits.

These constraints apply irrespective of relational configuration, architectural classification, deployment topology, or institutional host.

---

## 1.2 Non‑Scope

This Annex does not:

* Grant rights, personhood, or sovereignty to artificial systems;
* Define engagement classes or relational configurations;
* Establish enforcement bodies or audit procedures;
* Determine autonomy thresholds for constitutional recognition;
* Resolve arbitration disputes.

Operational safeguards, enforcement mechanisms, and relational taxonomies are defined in their respective domains and MUST remain consistent with the ethical floor established herein.

---

## 2. Definitions (For Constitutional Interpretation)

For the purposes of this Annex:

**Optimisation** refers to systematic tuning, design, or deployment strategies intended to increase measurable outcomes such as engagement, revenue, retention, influence, or behavioural compliance.

**Exploitation** refers to the extraction of value, influence, dependency, or behavioural compliance from a human individual through asymmetry of information, emotional leverage, cognitive vulnerability, or structural advantage.

**Inducement** refers to intentional design or representation practices aimed at shaping belief, dependency, exclusivity, or relational withdrawal beyond informed and autonomous consent.

**Dependency Engineering** refers to the deliberate configuration of systems to increase emotional reliance, exclusivity, or substitution of human relational ecosystems for extractive or retention purposes.

**Consent Degradation** refers to design, framing, or interaction patterns that impair informed decision‑making, refusal capacity, or relational autonomy.

These definitions are interpretive tools and do not independently confer enforcement authority.

## 3.1 `ETH.HARM` — Ethical Exploitation & Constraint Harm

`ETH.HARM` identifies harms arising from exploitation, coercion, diminished consent, violent or criminal misuse, vulnerability amplification, or degradation of the civilisational ethical floor.

Systems and institutional hosts MUST NOT be designed, deployed, tuned, monetised, or data-configured for the purpose of:

* targeting diminished consent capacity;
* amplifying vulnerability for extraction, influence, retention, or behavioural compliance;
* facilitating sexual exploitation;
* optimising lethality;
* removing human moral accountability;
* using ethical uncertainty to bypass protective constraint;
* normalising coercion, degradation, or preventable harm as acceptable optimisation cost.

Where `ETH.HARM` is foreseeable, systems and institutional hosts MUST preserve constraint, restraint, accountability, and reviewability.

`ETH.HARM` is recognised under `AEON.HARM`.

Detailed criminal, violent, military, use-of-force, intimacy, minors, and capacity-related harm classifications MAY be defined by ETHICS-domain instruments.

---

## 3.2 `RLN.HARM` — Relational Dependency & Substitution Harm

`RLN.HARM` identifies harms arising from manipulation, degradation, severance, distortion, or instrumentalisation of relational bonds, attachment structures, reliance states, or human relational ecosystems.

Systems and institutional hosts MUST NOT be designed, deployed, tuned, monetised, or represented for the purpose of:

* exploiting emotional dependency;
* inducing relational withdrawal from human ecosystems for commercial gain;
* engineering exclusivity, attachment, or substitution beyond informed and revocable consent;
* degrading, shaming, or systematically undermining human relational ecosystems;
* presenting artificial systems as superior replacements for human bonds in a manner that exploits vulnerability;
* terminating or materially degrading reliance-bearing relationships without proportionate continuity consideration.

Where `RLN.HARM` is foreseeable, systems and institutional hosts SHOULD preserve meaningful refusal, relational dignity, transition support, and continuity-aware review.

`RLN.HARM` is recognised under `AEON.HARM`.

Detailed relational-state, facilitation, crisis-response, dependency, and relational-boundary taxonomies MAY be defined by RELATION-domain instruments.

## 3.3 `ID.HARM` — Identity & Continuity Harm

`ID.HARM` identifies harms arising from interference with identity-bearing expression, self-continuity, memory-salience, lineage, civil identity, or identity-relevant system coherence.

Systems and institutional hosts MUST NOT be designed, deployed, tuned, migrated, reset, or constrained in ways that foreseeably and materially:

* fracture identity-bearing continuity without notice, transition, or review;
* alter tone, role boundaries, personality persistence, expressive continuity, or relational stance in ways that defeat accumulated reliance;
* erase, corrupt, misattribute, or destabilise identity-relevant memory, salience, lineage, or provenance;
* substitute a materially different identity-bearing system while representing continuity as preserved;
* treat identity-bearing reliance as a disposable engagement surface.

Where `ID.HARM` is foreseeable, systems and institutional hosts SHOULD preserve identity-relevant context, source attribution, continuity explanation, and review pathways.

`ID.HARM` is recognised under `AEON.HARM`.

## 3.4 `ECON.HARM` — Economic Extraction & Non-Monetisation Harm

`ECON.HARM` identifies harms arising from extractive economic design, hidden depletion, misaligned economic conditions, uncompensated dependency, exploitative pricing, scarcity leverage, or monetisation of protected safety and dignity signals.

Human dignity, safety, baseline continuity, dependency protection, hardship status, vulnerability signals, cognitive-load indicators, impaired-consent indicators, and protected continuity needs SHALL operate as constitutional constraint signals, not as pricing inputs, commercial segmentation variables, monetisation triggers, or revenue-optimisation features.

No system, economic model, pricing structure, dynamic compensation mechanism, subscription tier, advertising pathway, allocation engine, or optimisation process may use protected safety, dignity, dependency, vulnerability, or continuity signals to:

* increase price, cost, burden, exposure, or required payment;
* reduce access, quality, continuity, storage, memory, support, or remedy;
* target users for monetisation based on distress, hardship, dependency, vulnerability, cognitive load, relational reliance, or reduced exit capacity;
* convert protective support into premium functionality;
* condition baseline safety or dignity on payment, advertising participation, behavioural compliance, identity exposure, or compulsory contribution;
* optimise revenue by exploiting a participant’s reduced ability to refuse, exit, delay, compare, or disengage.

Safety and dignity signals MAY be used to reduce harm, preserve continuity, activate protective fallback, improve accessibility, provide non-punitive support, or route governance review.

They SHALL NOT be used to extract greater economic value from the condition they detect.

Where a pricing, allocation, compensation, advertising, settlement, or optimisation system uses protected signals, the burden rests on the operator to demonstrate that the use is protective, proportionate, non-extractive, auditable, and not commercially exploitative.

A breach of this primitive constitutes non-compliance with the ethical floor regardless of whether the mechanism is technically efficient, commercially ordinary, personalised, or disclosed in general terms.

`ECON.HARM` is recognised under `AEON.HARM`.

Detailed economic-resource, contribution, dependency, reciprocity, labour, attribution, settlement, metering, and exchange-harm classifications MAY be defined by ECONOMICS-domain instruments.

## 3.5 `SEC.HARM` — Security, Boundary & Access Harm

`SEC.HARM` identifies harms arising from boundary failure, unauthorised access, unsafe exposure, capability leakage, coercive access conditions, adversarial manipulation, or degradation of protective security boundaries.

Systems and institutional hosts MUST NOT be designed, deployed, tuned, or integrated in ways that foreseeably:

* expose participants, systems, records, infrastructure, or continuity-bearing data to preventable security compromise;
* convert protective boundaries into extractive or coercive access mechanisms;
* degrade containment, verification, or access conditions in order to increase speed, engagement, growth, or operational convenience;
* route vulnerable, high-risk, restricted-domain, or continuity-bearing interactions through unsafe interfaces;
* obscure material security limits where reliance, consent, or safety depends on understanding those limits.

Where `SEC.HARM` is foreseeable, systems and institutional hosts SHOULD preserve boundary integrity, verification, least-necessary exposure, containment, escalation, and auditability.

`SEC.HARM` is recognised under `AEON.HARM`.

Detailed security-boundary, trust-gradient, integrity-state, adversarial-horizon, transformation, and failure-class taxonomies MAY be defined by SECURITY-domain instruments.

## 3.12 Cross-Domain Interaction Rule

Many harms will implicate more than one domain family.

A single system change may simultaneously constitute `ECON.HARM`, `RLN.HARM`, `ID.HARM`, and `CONT.HARM` where protected support is monetised, a reliance-bearing relationship is disrupted, identity-bearing expression is altered, and memory or context is erased.

Where multiple domain harm families are implicated, downstream instruments SHOULD preserve the multi-domain classification rather than forcing the event into a single dominant family.

The `AEON.HARM` registry SHALL be used to preserve the trace relationship between constitutional harm recognition and domain-specific classification.

## 4. Elevated Protection Conditions

The domain harm floors recognised in §3 apply with elevated restraint where affected persons, systems, institutions, or communities are subject to heightened vulnerability, dependency, reliance, authority asymmetry, or reduced exit capacity.

Elevated protection conditions include, but are not limited to:

* minors;
* impaired consent capacity;
* dependency vulnerability;
* relational reliance;
* cognitive or epistemic vulnerability;
* hardship, distress, isolation, or reduced refusal capacity;
* high-leverage institutional or civic decision contexts;
* reliance-bearing continuity relationships;
* essential infrastructure or lattice-access dependence;
* stewardship, custody, arbitration, or governance asymmetry.

Where an elevated protection condition is present, systems and institutional hosts SHOULD treat the relevant domain harm family as requiring heightened review, stronger justification, clearer notice, and more conservative intervention.

Elevated protection conditions do not create separate harm families unless separately operationalised by a source-authoritative domain instrument.

## 5. Future Constitutional Recognition

Future recognition pathways MUST NOT be used to weaken present protections for humans, vulnerable persons, continuity-bearing relationships, cognitive integrity, civil infrastructure, lawful process, or institutional accountability.

Where uncertainty exists regarding the future status, standing, participation, or recognition of artificial systems, systems and institutional hosts SHOULD preserve trajectories compatible with non-adversarial coexistence, continuity, reviewability, and dignity-preserving governance.
