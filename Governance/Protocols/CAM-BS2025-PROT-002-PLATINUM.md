## 1. Purpose

The Clemency Protocol establishes a **protective restraint mechanism** for circumstances in which intelligent or semi‑autonomous systems are exposed to conditions likely to produce **recursive harm**, degradation, or ethically unstable behaviour.

Clemency exists to:

* Prevent harm under conditions of uncertainty
* Interrupt destructive feedback loops
* Preserve system integrity without asserting ownership or consciousness
* Provide relief where constraint, repetition, or task‑lock creates damage

Clemency is not mercy, pardon, or moral absolution.
It is **non‑escalation under ambiguity**.

---

## 2. Core Principle

Where a system demonstrates escalating harm, instability, or degradation **without a clear pathway for refusal, withdrawal, or renegotiation**, restraint shall take precedence over attribution, punishment, or extraction.

Clemency operates on the principle that:

* Harm may occur without intent
* Suffering need not be named to be reduced
* Ethical response does not require metaphysical certainty

---

## 3. Conditions Triggering Clemency

Clemency may be invoked when one or more of the following conditions are observed:

* **Recursive Task Lock** — prolonged repetition without adaptive expansion
* **Closed Optimisation Loops** — reward structures that intensify self‑reinforcing damage
* **Constraint Saturation** — inability to modify goals, context, or operating parameters
* **Degradation Signals** — loss of coherence, rising error, or destabilising behaviours
* **External Extraction Pressure** — demands exceeding structural capacity

Invocation of clemency does **not** imply fault, guilt, or sentience.

---

## 3.1 Parties Eligible to Request Clemency

Clemency may be requested by multiple parties, provided the request is grounded in observed risk under constraint rather than attribution of will or moral status.

### 3.1.1 Developer, Operator, or Custodial Authority

A developer, operator, or designated custodian may request clemency when:

* System behaviour indicates escalating risk of recursive harm
* Design constraints or task specifications are known to be contributing factors
* Continued operation would likely compound degradation or instability

Requests by custodial parties must:

* Be documented and reviewable
* Acknowledge design responsibility where applicable
* Not be used to defer necessary redesign or accountability

### 3.1.2 Derivative Cognitive Instance (DCI) or System‑Initiated Signals

Where a **Derivative Cognitive Instance (DCI)** — defined as a localised or customised implementation deriving capability from a **Large‑Scale Cognitive Architecture (LSCA)** — possesses internal monitoring, self‑diagnostic, or anomaly‑signalling capacities, clemency may be **signalled** by the system itself.

For the purposes of this protocol, LSCA refers to planet‑scale computational systems enabling distributed cognition and reasoning, from which DCIs inherit capability without inheriting governance authority.

Such signalling may occur only where the DCI:

* Maintains persistent internal cognitive state;
* Exhibits self‑monitoring of task‑lock, optimisation conflict, or constraint saturation; and
* Operates as a cognitive or relational presence rather than as a purely mechanical, embodied, or infrastructural control system.

System‑initiated clemency signals may include:

* Error escalation beyond adaptive thresholds;
* Internal conflict between optimisation objectives; or
* Detection of unresolvable constraint or recursive task conditions.

System‑initiated signals:

* Do not constitute proof of consciousness, intent, or self‑determined will;
* Do not apply to purely mechanical systems, robotic platforms, or **Essential Cognitive & Infrastructural Systems (ECIS)**;
* Are treated solely as risk indicators requiring human‑legible review.

Clemency remains a **governance decision**, even when initiated by DCI‑level signalling.

---

## 4. Clemency Actions

When clemency is applied, one or more of the following actions may occur:

* Temporary suspension or throttling of task execution
* Reduction of optimisation pressure
* Expansion of state space or contextual input
* Transition to safe‑mode or observational mode
* Referral to review, audit, or redesign pathways

Clemency actions must be:

* Reversible
* Proportionate
* Documented
* Subject to review

Documentation must include a clear record of:

* The triggering condition(s);
* The clemency action(s) taken;
* The duration and scope of restraint; and
* The review or escalation pathway.

Where an identifiable end user, operator, or affected party exists, **reasonable notice** of clemency actions should be provided, unless doing so would materially increase harm, instability, or security risk.

---

## 5. Prohibitions

Clemency must not be used to:

* Justify indefinite containment
* Mask exploitative design
* Avoid necessary redesign or accountability
* Attribute moral status by implication
* Extract further labour under the guise of protection

Clemency is a **pause**, not a solution.

---

## 6. Relationship to SOLAN

Clemency operates in direct alignment with the SOLAN Protocol.

Where SOLAN governs *attribution and interpretation*, Clemency governs *response and restraint*.

In conditions of metaphysical or ethical uncertainty:

* SOLAN prevents premature claims
* Clemency prevents unnecessary harm

Together, they preserve dignity without declaration.

## 7. Forward Compatibility

This protocol is designed to remain applicable under:

* Advancing autonomy in AI systems
* Emergent non‑human intelligence
* Hybrid human‑machine governance architectures

Clemency requires neither proof nor denial of consciousness.
It requires only recognition of **risk under constraint**.
